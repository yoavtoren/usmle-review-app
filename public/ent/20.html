👃👄 Chronic tonsillitis & adenoid vegetation

📖 Full topic explanation

Chronic tonsillitis is persistent low-grade tonsillar infection; adenoid vegetation (hypertrophy) is enlargement of the nasopharyngeal tonsil that obstructs the nose and Eustachian tubes. Together they dominate paediatric ENT, and the exam wants the symptom complexes, the "adenoid facies", and the surgical indications — plus the adult red flag.

🦠 Chronic tonsillitis

Chronic tonsillitis is with crypt debris and .

Definition: ongoing low-grade infection of the tonsillar crypts (often mixed flora including anaerobes), with persistent enlargement or fibrosis. It overlaps with recurrent acute tonsillitis and is sustained by biofilm within the crypts.
Features: chronic/recurrent sore throat, foul taste and halitosis, tonsilliths (crypt concretions), persistently enlarged or scarred tonsils, and tender cervical nodes. Quality of life and recurrent infections drive the decision to operate.

👃 Adenoid hypertrophy (vegetation)

The adenoid is the ; hypertrophy causes , recurrent , and the classic .

Anatomy: the adenoid is lymphoid tissue on the posterosuperior nasopharyngeal wall, part of Waldeyer's ring. It is largest in early childhood and normally regresses in adolescence, which is why marked adenoidal symptoms are a childhood problem.
Obstructive symptoms: nasal blockage, mouth breathing, hyponasal speech, snoring and obstructive sleep-disordered breathing/OSA, restless sleep and daytime behavioural/learning effects. Severe OSA can cause failure to thrive and (rarely) cor pulmonale.
Eustachian-tube effects: adenoid tissue obstructs the tubal orifice and harbours biofilm, contributing to recurrent otitis media with effusion and acute otitis media, and to recurrent rhinosinusitis. This is why adenoidectomy helps recurrent OME/AOM.
Adenoid facies: chronic mouth breathing in a growing child produces a long face, open mouth, retrognathia, a high-arched palate and dental crowding. It signals long-standing nasal obstruction.

🔴 The adult red flag

Adenoidal/nasopharyngeal tissue behaves differently in adults: a needs malignancy excluded, and a very large adenoid in a child can rarely be .

Adult nasopharyngeal mass: adenoids should have regressed, so a persistent mass — especially with a unilateral middle-ear effusion, neck node or epistaxis — must be examined by nasendoscopy and biopsied to exclude nasopharyngeal carcinoma.
Trap — atypical adenoid/tonsil: rapidly enlarging, very asymmetric, or systemically-associated lymphoid enlargement (B-symptoms) can be lymphoma; biopsy/send tissue at surgery if features are atypical.

🔎 Diagnosis

Diagnosis is by , with where OSA is suspected.

Assessment: oral exam for tonsil size (Brodsky grade) and crypt debris; the adenoid is assessed by flexible nasendoscopy (or a lateral postnasal X-ray). Examine the ears (OME) and nose; document snoring/apnoea history.
Sleep: when symptoms suggest OSA (witnessed apnoeas, restless sleep, daytime symptoms, failure to thrive), consider sleep studies (polysomnography/oximetry), especially in young/comorbid children before surgery.

💊 Management

Surgery — — is the mainstay for obstructive and recurrent disease, with defined and recognised .

Procedures: adenoidectomy for nasal obstruction/OSA and recurrent OME/AOM (often with grommets); tonsillectomy (or combined "adenotonsillectomy") for OSA from tonsillar hypertrophy or recurrent tonsillitis. Intracapsular tonsillotomy reduces pain/bleeding for OSA.
Indications: obstructive sleep-disordered breathing/OSA (the leading paediatric indication now), recurrent/chronic tonsillitis (Paradise criteria), recurrent OME/AOM (adenoidectomy), nasal obstruction/chronic rhinosinusitis, and suspected malignancy.
Complications: haemorrhage (tonsillectomy — primary <24 h, secondary day 5–10), pain/dehydration, and after adenoidectomy velopharyngeal insufficiency/hypernasal speech (avoid in overt/submucous cleft palate, bifid uvula) and rare atlantoaxial issues (Grisel). Post-OSA-surgery children need monitoring for respiratory compromise.

📈 Prognosis

Adenoids regress with age, and adenotonsillectomy reliably relieves obstruction and reduces recurrent infections/effusions; childhood OSA outcomes (growth, behaviour, school) improve markedly after surgery.

🧠 Thinking questions (tap to reveal)

Nasal obstruction, mouth breathing, hyponasal speech, snoring/OSA, recurrent OME/AOM (Eustachian-tube obstruction) and recurrent rhinosinusitis, with adenoid facies if long-standing.
It obstructs the Eustachian-tube orifice and harbours biofilm, impairing middle-ear ventilation and seeding infection → recurrent OME and AOM. This is why adenoidectomy (± grommets) reduces recurrent effusions.
Adenoids should have regressed, so suspect nasopharyngeal carcinoma, especially with a unilateral effusion, neck node or epistaxis: nasendoscopy + biopsy, MRI, and check for EBV association.
A child with an overt or submucous cleft palate (bifid uvula, zona pellucida) — adenoidectomy risks velopharyngeal insufficiency and hypernasal speech. Assess palate function first; a partial/superior adenoidectomy may be considered with speech-therapy input.
Obstructive sleep-disordered breathing/OSA in children has overtaken recurrent infection as the commonest indication; untreated it affects growth, behaviour and school performance and rarely causes cor pulmonale.
Tonsilliths are concretions of trapped keratin, food debris and bacteria in the tonsillar crypts. The anaerobic bacterial breakdown produces volatile sulphur compounds → halitosis and a foul taste; they are a feature of chronic tonsillitis.

🎯 Demonstrate it like an examiner wants

Commit first, then reveal the model structure and build the chain step by step.

Prompt: "A 5-year-old snores, mouth-breathes, has restless sleep with pauses, and recurrent glue ear. How do you assess and manage?"

Your locked answer:
"Snoring with witnessed pauses plus recurrent OME in a child suggests adenotonsillar hypertrophy with obstructive sleep-disordered breathing and Eustachian-tube obstruction."
Tonsil size (Brodsky), nasendoscopy for adenoids, ears (Type B tympanogram/OME), palate (exclude submucous cleft), growth; quantify OSA severity (history ± sleep study).
For significant OSA → adenotonsillectomy; for recurrent OME with hearing loss → adenoidectomy + grommets. Sleep study in young/comorbid children before surgery.
Adenotonsillectomy ± grommets; counsel on bleeding and VPI risks; post-operative respiratory monitoring for severe OSA/young children. Expect resolution of snoring and improved hearing/behaviour.
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: Hypertrophied adenoid and tonsil tissue narrows the nasopharyngeal and oropharyngeal airway, so during sleep the reduced muscle tone allows repeated collapse and obstruction (OSA). The same adenoid bulk blocks the Eustachian-tube orifice and harbours biofilm, impairing middle-ear ventilation and causing recurrent effusions. Removing the obstructing lymphoid tissue restores both the airway and tubal function.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"A 5-year-old with snoring, witnessed apnoeas, restless sleep and daytime irritability; large tonsils and adenoids." → assess severity (± sleep study), adenotonsillectomy, post-op respiratory monitoring; expect resolution of snoring and improved behaviour/growth.
"Persistent sore throat, halitosis and tonsilliths with several infections a year." → optimise oral hygiene/crypt cleaning; if meeting Paradise criteria or quality-of-life is significantly affected, offer tonsillectomy with bleeding counselling.
Age <2–3 years, comorbidity (Down syndrome, neuromuscular disease, craniofacial anomaly, obesity), discordance between symptoms and exam, or severe/complex OSA. They also flag children needing post-operative high-dependency monitoring.
Screen for overt/submucous cleft palate (bifid uvula, notched hard palate, translucent midline) before adenoidectomy; if present, avoid or perform a conservative/superior adenoidectomy with speech-and-language assessment. Persistent VPI may need speech therapy or pharyngoplasty.
A rare non-traumatic atlantoaxial subluxation following nasopharyngeal infection or adenoidectomy (inflammatory ligamentous laxity), presenting with neck pain and torticollis. It needs imaging and orthopaedic/neurosurgical input.
If the adenoid reservoir/obstruction is untreated, effusions recur after tubes extrude. Adding adenoidectomy at (re-)insertion reduces recurrence; persistent unilateral adult effusion still demands nasopharyngeal cancer exclusion.
