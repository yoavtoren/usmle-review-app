👃 Acute & chronic rhinosinusitis

📖 Full topic explanation

Rhinosinusitis is symptomatic inflammation of the nasal and paranasal-sinus mucosa, divided by duration into acute (<12 weeks) and chronic (≥12 weeks). The key skills: know when acute sinusitis is bacterial (and when not to give antibiotics), subtype chronic disease by polyps, and recognise the red flags of complications and tumour.

🧬 Anatomy — the ostiomeatal complex

The sinuses drain by through the — the anatomical key to both disease and surgery.

Mucociliary clearance: cilia sweep mucus toward the natural ostia in defined patterns. Anything that blocks an ostium or impairs cilia (infection, allergy, polyps, anatomical narrowing, ciliary disease) causes stasis, retained secretions and infection — the basis of rhinosinusitis.
Ostiomeatal complex (OMC): the common drainage channel of the maxillary, anterior ethmoid and frontal sinuses into the middle meatus. Obstruction here causes anterior-sinus disease, and relieving it is the central aim of functional endoscopic sinus surgery (Topic 35).

🤧 Acute rhinosinusitis

Acute rhinosinusitis is usually ; suspect with specific features, and always watch for .

Viral (commonest): the "common cold" — nasal blockage, discharge, facial pressure and reduced smell, peaking and resolving within ~10 days. Antibiotics are not indicated; treat with analgesia, saline and intranasal steroids.
Acute bacterial rhinosinusitis: suspect when symptoms are >10 days without improvement, are severe (high fever, purulent discharge, unilateral facial pain), or show "double-sickening" (initial improvement then worsening). Organisms: S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae, Moraxella. Then consider antibiotics (e.g. amoxicillin/co-amoxiclav).
Red flags (→ Topic 31): periorbital swelling/erythema, proptosis, painful or restricted eye movements, reduced vision, severe frontal headache, altered consciousness, or frontal swelling signal an orbital or intracranial complication — urgent CT, IV antibiotics and ophthalmology/neurosurgery.

🔁 Chronic rhinosinusitis

Chronic rhinosinusitis (CRS) lasts and is subtyped by , with several important .

Definition: ≥12 weeks of ≥2 symptoms — nasal blockage, (anterior/posterior) discharge, facial pain/pressure, reduction/loss of smell — one of which must be blockage or discharge, PLUS objective evidence on nasal endoscopy or CT (inflammation/polyps/mucopus). Symptoms alone are not enough.
Phenotypes: CRS with nasal polyps (CRSwNP) — often eosinophilic/type-2 inflammation, smell loss prominent, associated with asthma; CRS without polyps (CRSsNP). This split guides treatment (steroids, biologics) and prognosis.
Associations to ask about: asthma and aspirin sensitivity (Samter's triad/AERD), allergic fungal rhinosinusitis, primary ciliary dyskinesia and cystic fibrosis (especially children/bilateral polyps), immunodeficiency, and granulomatosis with polyangiitis. Bilateral polyps in a child → think cystic fibrosis.

🔎 Diagnosis

Diagnosis is clinical + , with and a key .

Assessment: history, anterior rhinoscopy and nasal endoscopy (mucopus from the middle meatus, polyps), allergy testing where relevant. Acute uncomplicated sinusitis needs no imaging.
CT sinuses: reserved for CRS not responding to medical therapy (surgical planning), suspected complications, or atypical/unilateral disease. It maps the OMC and anatomy for FESS; it is not a screening test for acute sinusitis.
Trap — unilateral disease: unilateral nasal obstruction, bloody discharge, facial pain or a unilateral polyp/mass must prompt endoscopy, imaging and biopsy to exclude a neoplasm (inverted papilloma, SCC) — not simply "sinusitis".

💊 Management

Acute disease is mostly ; CRS is treated with , escalating to for refractory disease.

Acute: analgesia, saline irrigation, intranasal steroids, and decongestants short-term; antibiotics only for bacterial features (>10 days, severe, double-sickening). Most viral/post-viral cases resolve without antibiotics.
CRS first-line: intranasal corticosteroids + saline irrigation (the mainstay), treat allergy, and avoid irritants/smoking. A short course of oral steroids can shrink polyps and improve smell.
Refractory CRS: biologics (anti-IL-5/IL-4Rα, e.g. dupilumab) for severe CRSwNP/type-2 disease; functional endoscopic sinus surgery (FESS) when maximal medical therapy fails — to restore OMC drainage and ventilation (Topic 35).

📈 Prognosis

Acute viral sinusitis resolves spontaneously; bacterial sinusitis responds to antibiotics. CRS is a chronic relapsing disease controlled (not usually cured) by topical steroids/irrigation ± biologics/surgery; CRSwNP and aspirin-exacerbated disease are more recalcitrant and recur after surgery.

The common drainage channel of the maxillary, anterior ethmoid and frontal sinuses into the middle meatus. Obstruction here causes anterior-sinus disease, and relieving it is the central aim of FESS.
Cystic fibrosis (and primary ciliary dyskinesia) — nasal polyps are uncommon in children and bilateral polyps warrant a sweat test/CF work-up. In adults with polyps + asthma + aspirin sensitivity, think Samter's triad (AERD).
Intranasal corticosteroids + saline irrigation (mainstay), allergy treatment, smoking cessation; short oral steroids for polyps; biologics for severe type-2 CRSwNP; FESS if maximal medical therapy fails.

🎯 Demonstrate it like an examiner wants

Commit first, then reveal the model structure and build the chain step by step.

Prompt: "A 35-year-old has 4 months of nasal blockage, loss of smell and postnasal drip, with bilateral polyps on endoscopy. How do you assess and manage?"

Your locked answer:
"≥12 weeks of blockage, smell loss and discharge with bilateral polyps is chronic rhinosinusitis with nasal polyps (CRSwNP) — confirmed by endoscopy."
Confirm the CRS criteria; screen for asthma and aspirin sensitivity (AERD), allergy, and (if relevant) CF/PCD/immunodeficiency.
Nasal endoscopy (confirm polyps/mucopus), allergy testing, and CT sinuses if medical therapy fails (surgical planning) or disease is atypical/unilateral.
Intranasal steroids + saline irrigation, short oral steroids for polyps, treat allergy/asthma; biologics for severe type-2 disease; FESS if maximal medical therapy fails, with ongoing topical maintenance.
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: Persistent mucosal inflammation (often eosinophilic/type-2) thickens the mucosa and forms oedematous polyps that obstruct the ostiomeatal complex. Blocked drainage perpetuates mucus stasis, infection and further inflammation, impairing the olfactory cleft to cause smell loss. Topical steroids and irrigation dampen the inflammation, while surgery restores ventilation and drainage so topical therapy can reach the sinuses.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"Twelve days of facial pain, purulent discharge and fever after a cold, having initially improved (double-sickening)." → acute bacterial rhinosinusitis: analgesia, intranasal steroid, saline; antibiotics (amoxicillin/co-amoxiclav); safety-net for orbital/intracranial red flags.
"Chronic blockage, anosmia, bilateral polyps, asthmatic, aspirin-sensitive." → CRSwNP (Samter's triad): intranasal steroids + irrigation, short oral steroid, treat asthma/allergy; biologic if severe; FESS if refractory, with maintenance topical therapy.
A type-2 hypersensitivity to fungi in atopic patients with nasal polyps, eosinophilic "allergic mucin", characteristic heterogeneous CT opacification, and raised IgE. Treated with surgery to remove mucin + topical/oral steroids (not antifungals primarily); distinct from invasive fungal sinusitis.
Most acute sinusitis is viral/self-limiting, but purulent discharge is misread as bacterial. Overuse drives resistance and side-effects without benefit. Reserve antibiotics for the defined bacterial features (>10 days, severe, double-sickening).
In immunocompromised/diabetic (ketoacidotic) patients with rapid facial pain, mucosal necrosis (black eschar), orbital/cranial-nerve signs — acute invasive fungal sinusitis (mucormycosis/Aspergillus). It is a life-threatening emergency needing urgent biopsy, antifungals and surgical debridement, and reversal of immunosuppression.
Granulomatosis with polyangiitis — crusting rhinitis, septal perforation/saddle nose, with c-ANCA/PR3 and renal/pulmonary involvement. Biopsy and ANCA testing distinguish it from ordinary CRS.
