🧠👂 Vestibular schwannoma

📖 Full topic explanation

A vestibular schwannoma is a benign Schwann-cell tumour of the vestibular nerve in the internal auditory meatus and cerebellopontine angle. It is the great "don't-miss" of asymmetric hearing loss: the misnamed "acoustic neuroma" presents with unilateral SNHL, tinnitus and imbalance, and is diagnosed by gadolinium MRI.

🗂️ Definition & origin

It is a benign tumour of , the , usually ; bilateral tumours mean .

Origin: Schwann cells of the vestibular nerve (usually the superior or inferior division), most often at the Obersteiner–Redlich zone (the glial–Schwann junction near the porus). Although it arises from the vestibular nerve, it presents with hearing symptoms by compressing the adjacent cochlear nerve — hence "acoustic neuroma" is a misnomer.
CPA tumours: vestibular schwannoma accounts for ~80% of cerebellopontine-angle tumours; the main differentials are meningioma and epidermoid. It grows slowly, from within the IAM out into the CPA cistern (the "ice-cream cone" shape).
Sporadic: the great majority are unilateral, sporadic, presenting in middle age. Growth rate is variable and often slow (some do not grow), which underpins a "watch-and-scan" option.
NF2: bilateral vestibular schwannomas are diagnostic of neurofibromatosis type 2 (chromosome 22, merlin/NF2 tumour-suppressor gene). NF2 also causes meningiomas, ependymomas and other schwannomas, presents younger, and threatens hearing in both ears — a different management problem.

👂 Clinical features

Typical features are , with ; as it enlarges it causes .

Hearing: asymmetric/unilateral SNHL (often high-frequency, progressive — occasionally sudden), unilateral tinnitus, and word-discrimination scores worse than the pure-tone average predicts (a retrocochlear hallmark). Any of these mandates MRI.
Balance: patients usually have unsteadiness/disequilibrium rather than rotational vertigo, because the slow growth lets central compensation keep pace with the gradual vestibular loss. A reduced caloric response is common.
Enlargement (CPA): reduced corneal reflex / facial numbness (CN V), eventually facial weakness (CN VII — late, because the motor nerve is resistant), then cerebellar signs, brainstem compression, and obstructive hydrocephalus with raised intracranial pressure in very large tumours.

🩺 Signs & "don't-miss" rule

Look for the , and apply the rule that .

Hitselberger sign: reduced sensation of the posterosuperior external auditory canal from involvement of the sensory (Wrisberg) fibres of the facial nerve — a subtle clue to a CPA lesion.
The rule: unilateral/asymmetric SNHL, unilateral tinnitus, or sudden SNHL must be investigated with gadolinium MRI of the IAM to exclude a schwannoma. This is the single highest-yield action of the topic.

🔎 Investigation

The gold standard is , supported by and tumour .

MRI: a gadolinium-enhancing mass in the IAM/CPA (the "ice-cream cone" with the cone in the canal) is diagnostic; high-resolution T2 (CISS/FIESTA) is used for screening. MRI also distinguishes meningioma (dural tail, broad base) and epidermoid (restricted diffusion).
Functional tests: audiometry (asymmetric SNHL, poor discrimination), ABR (delayed wave V / prolonged I–V — retrocochlear), and calorics (reduced response). These support but no longer replace MRI for diagnosis.
Grading: the Koos grade (I–IV) classifies by size/extension (intracanalicular → brainstem-compressing), guiding management and monitoring growth on serial scans.

💊 Management

Three options — , , and — are chosen by , with facial-nerve preservation a priority.

Observation ("watch and scan"): for small, asymptomatic or non-growing tumours, the elderly or comorbid — serial MRI (e.g. yearly), intervening if growth or symptoms progress. Many small tumours never need treatment.
Stereotactic radiosurgery (e.g. Gamma Knife): for small-to-medium tumours — high tumour-control rates with good facial-nerve preservation, avoiding open surgery; risks include hearing loss over time and (rarely) malignant change. Not for very large/brainstem-compressing tumours.
Microsurgery: for larger or growing tumours / brainstem compression. Approaches: translabyrinthine (sacrifices hearing, best facial-nerve access), retrosigmoid and middle fossa (hearing-preservation attempts for small tumours). Facial-nerve monitoring is routine.
Decision factors: tumour size and growth, useful hearing (preserve where possible), age/comorbidity, patient preference, and (in NF2) the contralateral ear — sometimes favouring hearing preservation or an auditory brainstem implant.

📈 Prognosis

As a benign tumour the prognosis is good; the challenge is preserving hearing and facial-nerve function. Small tumours are often observed; treated tumours have high control rates, with outcomes dominated by facial-nerve and hearing preservation and, in NF2, by bilateral disease.

🧠 Thinking questions (tap to reveal)

It arises on the vestibular nerve but compresses the adjacent cochlear nerve (and its blood supply) within the confined IAM, producing asymmetric SNHL and tinnitus. Slow growth allows central vestibular compensation, so balance is affected more than true vertigo — hence the misnomer "acoustic neuroma".
Gadolinium MRI of the IAM/CPA — ordered for any asymmetric SNHL, unilateral tinnitus or sudden SNHL, or retrocochlear ABR. It shows an enhancing "ice-cream cone" mass.
Neurofibromatosis type 2 (chromosome 22, merlin/NF2 gene) — also meningiomas, ependymomas and other schwannomas, presenting younger; both ears are at risk, changing management toward hearing preservation/ABI.
The motor facial nerve is relatively resistant to slow compression, so facial weakness is a late sign of a large tumour; early facial palsy should make you doubt the diagnosis (think facial-nerve schwannoma or malignancy). The sensory CN V (corneal reflex) is often affected earlier.
Observation (small/non-growing, elderly), stereotactic radiosurgery (small–medium), microsurgery (large/growing/brainstem compression). Choice depends on size, growth, hearing, age and patient choice; preserve the facial nerve and hearing where possible.
Translabyrinthine — excellent facial-nerve access but sacrifices hearing (for non-serviceable hearing/large tumours). Retrosigmoid and middle fossa — attempt hearing preservation for small tumours, with differing exposure of the IAM and CPA.

🎯 Demonstrate it like an examiner wants

Commit first, then reveal the model structure and build the chain step by step.

Prompt: "A 50-year-old has progressive left hearing loss with left tinnitus and poor clarity on the phone. Work this up and outline management."

Your locked answer:
"Asymmetric SNHL with unilateral tinnitus and disproportionately poor discrimination is a vestibular schwannoma until excluded — it mandates a gadolinium MRI of the IAM."
Benign vestibular-nerve Schwann-cell tumour in the IAM/CPA causing a retrocochlear pattern; state the NF2 caveat for bilateral disease.
Audiometry (asymmetric SNHL, word scores worse than expected), examine CN V/VII and cerebellum (Hitselberger sign), ABR if used, and the definitive gadolinium MRI IAM; grade by Koos.
Small/non-growing → observe with serial MRI; small–medium → stereotactic radiosurgery; large/growing/brainstem compression → microsurgery (translabyrinthine vs hearing-preserving retrosigmoid/middle fossa) with facial-nerve monitoring.
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: Loss of the merlin tumour-suppressor lets vestibular-nerve Schwann cells proliferate into a benign mass at the glial–Schwann junction. Within the bony confines of the internal auditory meatus it compresses the cochlear nerve and labyrinthine artery, causing progressive retrocochlear hearing loss and tinnitus. Continued growth into the cerebellopontine angle then stretches CN V and VII and ultimately compresses the brainstem and obstructs CSF flow.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"Progressive unilateral SNHL, unilateral tinnitus, mild imbalance, word scores poorer than the audiogram predicts." → vestibular schwannoma: gadolinium MRI IAM confirms; manage by size/hearing/age (observe vs radiosurgery vs microsurgery).
"A large CPA mass with facial numbness, ataxia and headache." → brainstem compression ± hydrocephalus: microsurgical resection (translabyrinthine if hearing non-serviceable) with facial-nerve monitoring; manage hydrocephalus; MDT skull-base care.
By compromising the labyrinthine artery or cochlear nerve acutely. Up to ~1–2 in 10 sudden SNHL cases harbour a schwannoma, which is why MRI is part of the sudden-SNHL work-up even after steroid treatment.
If hearing is serviceable and the tumour small, radiosurgery or a hearing-preserving approach (retrosigmoid/middle fossa) is preferred; if hearing is poor or the tumour large, translabyrinthine resection (which sacrifices hearing for the best facial-nerve access) is reasonable.
Bilateral tumours threaten all hearing, so strategy aims to preserve hearing as long as possible (observation, bevacizumab in some, hearing-preserving surgery), with cochlear or auditory brainstem implants when hearing is lost. It is managed in specialist NF2 centres.
Serial gadolinium MRI to monitor growth (observation) or control (post-radiosurgery), plus audiometry and facial-nerve assessment. Growth or new symptoms prompt escalation; long-term surveillance is standard.
