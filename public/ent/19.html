📖 Full topic explanation

Tonsillitis is inflammation of the palatine tonsils within Waldeyer's ring. The exam wants you to separate viral from group A streptococcal disease, recognise the dangerous mimics (infectious mononucleosis, quinsy, diphtheria), treat appropriately, and know the evidence-based indications for tonsillectomy.

🧬 Anatomy of Waldeyer's ring

The palatine tonsils sit between the , with a clinically important that explains post-tonsillectomy bleeding.

Waldeyer's ring: palatine tonsils + nasopharyngeal (adenoid) + lingual + tubal tonsils form a ring of mucosa-associated lymphoid tissue guarding the aerodigestive inlet. The palatine tonsil's deep crypts trap debris (tonsilliths) and organisms.
Blood supply (high-yield for bleeding): mainly the tonsillar branch of the facial artery, plus the ascending pharyngeal, lingual (dorsal lingual) and descending palatine arteries. The rich supply and proximity to the carotid explain why post-tonsillectomy haemorrhage is a recognised, occasionally life-threatening, complication.

🦠 Aetiology & classification

Most acute tonsillitis is ; the key bacterial cause is . Classify as , and recognise .

Viral (commonest): rhinovirus, adenovirus, influenza, EBV. Usually accompanied by coryza/cough, with diffuse erythema and minimal exudate; self-limiting and not helped by antibiotics.
GABHS (Streptococcus pyogenes): the bacterial cause that matters — tonsillar exudate, fever, tender anterior cervical nodes, absence of cough. It is treated to relieve symptoms and prevent suppurative and immune complications (rheumatic fever).
Clinical classes: acute (catarrhal → follicular → parenchymatous), recurrent acute, and chronic (persistent low-grade infection, tonsilliths, halitosis). This classification drives whether tonsillectomy is considered.
Membranous/specific forms: infectious mononucleosis (EBV — membranous exudate, lymphadenopathy, hepatosplenomegaly), diphtheria (grey adherent membrane that bleeds on removal — airway/toxin emergency), Vincent's angina (fusospirochaetal ulceromembranous), and tonsillar changes in agranulocytosis/leukaemia.

👄 Symptoms & signs

Core features are , often with ; severity scores estimate the chance of strep.

Presentation: sore throat, painful swallowing, fever, malaise, halitosis, and tender, enlarged jugulodigastric (tonsillar) lymph nodes; the tonsils are enlarged and inflamed ± exudate. Children may have abdominal pain and refuse food.
Referred otalgia: the tonsil is innervated by the glossopharyngeal nerve (IX), which also supplies the middle ear — so tonsillitis (and especially quinsy/post-tonsillectomy) causes referred ear pain with a normal ear.

📊 Identifying streptococcal tonsillitis

Use the to estimate GABHS likelihood and guide antibiotics, with .

Centor criteria (one point each): tonsillar exudate, tender anterior cervical nodes, fever >38 °C, absence of cough (+ age adjustment). Higher scores raise the probability of GABHS and the case for testing/antibiotics; FeverPAIN is an alternative score.
Trap — amoxicillin + EBV: giving amoxicillin/ampicillin in infectious mononucleosis causes a florid maculopapular rash. If glandular fever is possible (membranous tonsillitis, marked lymphadenopathy, splenomegaly, atypical lymphocytes), avoid amoxicillin and use penicillin V if a bacterial cause is treated.

💊 Treatment

Most cases need only ; is used for likely GABHS, and for the unwell. has defined indications.

Supportive: analgesia/antipyretics, hydration and rest for the majority (viral). Most sore throats resolve in a week regardless of antibiotics.
Antibiotics: phenoxymethylpenicillin (penicillin V) for ~10 days for confirmed/likely GABHS (or a macrolide if penicillin-allergic); avoid amoxicillin where EBV is possible. Antibiotics shorten symptoms modestly and reduce suppurative/rheumatic complications.
Admit if: unable to swallow/dehydrated, systemically unwell/septic, airway concern, suspected quinsy or deep-neck infection, or immunocompromise. Consider IV fluids, IV antibiotics and steroids.
Tonsillectomy indications (Paradise/SIGN): recurrent acute tonsillitis — ≥7 episodes in 1 year, ≥5/year for 2 years, or ≥3/year for 3 years (disabling); ≥2 episodes of quinsy; obstructive sleep-disordered breathing/OSA; and suspected malignancy (unilateral enlargement/ulcer). Counsel on bleeding risk.

⚠️ Complications (signposted)

Tonsillitis can lead to (covered fully in Topic 21).

Complications: peritonsillar abscess (quinsy), parapharyngeal/retropharyngeal abscess, Lemierre syndrome; and the immune sequelae of GABHS — rheumatic fever, post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis, scarlet fever. Antibiotic treatment of GABHS aims partly to prevent these.

🧠 Thinking questions (tap to reveal)

Use Centor/FeverPAIN: exudate, tender anterior nodes, fever >38 °C, no cough raise the probability of GABHS. High scores justify testing/penicillin; low scores favour supportive care. Throat swab/rapid antigen testing can confirm.
Amoxicillin/ampicillin in EBV infectious mononucleosis causes a widespread maculopapular rash. Suspect EBV with membranous tonsillitis, marked lymphadenopathy, splenomegaly and atypical lymphocytes; confirm with Monospot/Paul-Bunnell and use penicillin V if treating.
Recurrent tonsillitis (≥7/1 yr, ≥5/yr×2, ≥3/yr×3, disabling), ≥2 quinsies, obstructive sleep-disordered breathing/OSA, and suspected malignancy (unilateral enlargement). Also some systemic indications (e.g. PFAPA, recurrent febrile convulsions).
Referred otalgia via the glossopharyngeal nerve (IX), which supplies both the tonsil/oropharynx and the middle ear. Marked unilateral referred otalgia in a smoker should also prompt exclusion of an oropharyngeal carcinoma.
Diphtheria (Corynebacterium diphtheriae) — a toxin-mediated emergency with airway obstruction and myocarditis/neuropathy risk. Isolate, give antitoxin + antibiotics, secure the airway, and notify public health. Now rare with vaccination.
Mainly the tonsillar branch of the facial artery, with contributions from the lingual, ascending pharyngeal and descending palatine arteries. Primary haemorrhage (<24 h) is a surgical/airway emergency; secondary haemorrhage (~day 5–10) usually follows infection.

🎯 Demonstrate it like an examiner wants

Commit first, then reveal the model structure and build the chain step by step.

Prompt: "A 19-year-old has a week of severe sore throat, fever, membranous tonsils and large neck nodes. How do you assess and treat?"

Your locked answer:
"Membranous tonsillitis with marked lymphadenopathy in a young adult suggests EBV infectious mononucleosis — so I must avoid amoxicillin and check the spleen and airway."
Examine tonsils/airway, palpate nodes and spleen; FBC (atypical lymphocytes), Monospot/Paul-Bunnell, LFTs; throat swab if bacterial co-infection suspected. Apply Centor for GABHS.
Analgesia, hydration, rest; avoid amoxicillin; steroids/airway care if severe obstruction; admit if not swallowing or airway-threatened. Penicillin V only for proven GABHS.
Advise avoid contact sports for ~3–4 weeks (splenic rupture risk), expect weeks of fatigue, and return for breathing difficulty. Consider tonsillectomy later only for recurrent disease.
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: EBV infects B-lymphocytes and proliferates in the lymphoid tissue of Waldeyer's ring, producing a membranous tonsillitis with massive reactive lymphadenopathy and splenomegaly. The reactive (atypical) T-lymphocyte response drives the systemic illness. Ampicillin-class antibiotics form immune complexes with viral antigens, causing the characteristic morbilliform rash.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"A child with fever, exudative tonsils, tender anterior nodes and no cough (Centor 4)." → likely GABHS: analgesia, hydration, penicillin V 10 days; safety-net for quinsy/dehydration; consider tonsillectomy only if recurrent.
"Seven documented episodes this year, each disabling." → meets Paradise criteria: discuss tonsillectomy, counsel on pain and the ~2–5% bleeding risk (primary <24 h, secondary day 5–10), and post-op care.
Streptococcus pyogenes can cause suppurative (quinsy, deep-neck abscess, otitis/sinusitis) and immune-mediated complications (rheumatic fever, post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis, scarlet fever). Penicillin reduces these and shortens illness.
Primary (<24 h): ABC, IV access, return to theatre — an airway/haemorrhagic emergency. Secondary (day 5–10): usually infection — admit, IV antibiotics, fluids; theatre if active/heavy bleeding. Have suction and senior help ready; blood loss is easily underestimated in children.
Cold steel dissection (lower secondary-bleed rate, classic), diathermy/bipolar and coblation (less intraoperative blood, possibly higher secondary-bleed risk), and intracapsular tonsillotomy (for OSA in children — less pain/bleeding, small regrowth risk). Choice balances pain, bleeding and indication.
Massive tonsillar/adenoid hypertrophy (e.g. EBV) or a deep-neck/retropharyngeal abscess can obstruct the airway — look for stridor, drooling, respiratory distress; manage with steroids, careful airway assessment, and senior ENT/anaesthetic involvement.
