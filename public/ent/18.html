🌀👂 Ménière disease

📖 Full topic explanation

Ménière disease is idiopathic endolymphatic hydrops producing the tetrad of episodic vertigo, fluctuating sensorineural hearing loss, tinnitus and aural fullness. It is a clinical diagnosis of exclusion, managed up a ladder from lifestyle to ablative therapy, and its key trap is mistaking it for a vestibular schwannoma or migraine.

🗂️ Definition & diagnostic criteria

Ménière's is defined by recurrent , in the absence of a better explanation. The frames the history.

Criteria (AAO-HNS / Bárány): ≥2 spontaneous vertigo episodes lasting 20 minutes–12 hours; audiometrically documented low-to-mid-frequency SNHL in the affected ear (at least once); fluctuating aural symptoms (hearing, tinnitus, fullness); and not better accounted for by another diagnosis. Meeting these distinguishes "definite" from "probable" disease.
The tetrad: episodic rotational vertigo (the disabling feature), fluctuating SNHL (low frequencies first), tinnitus (often roaring), and aural fullness. Symptoms cluster in attacks with symptom-free intervals early on.

🧬 Pathophysiology

The pathological correlate is , with attacks attributed to .

Endolymphatic hydrops: distension of the endolymphatic compartment from impaired endolymph homeostasis/absorption (endolymphatic sac dysfunction). It is the histological hallmark, though hydrops can also be asymptomatic, so it is necessary but not solely sufficient.
Attack mechanism: the leading model is rupture of Reissner's membrane mixing K⁺-rich endolymph with perilymph, transiently depolarising and "poisoning" the hair cells/nerve → acute vertigo and hearing change, recovering as the membrane heals and ions normalise.

📊 Epidemiology & natural history

It typically begins in , and follows a .

Demographics: onset commonly 40–60 years; initially unilateral but becomes bilateral in a substantial minority over years. Associations include migraine and autoimmunity; most cases are idiopathic.
Natural history: attacks fluctuate over years; vertigo often eventually "burns out" leaving permanent SNHL and imbalance. Tumarkin otolithic crises ("drop attacks") — sudden falls without warning or loss of consciousness — are dangerous and an indication to escalate treatment.

🔎 Investigation

Diagnosis is clinical; tests , with optional .

Core tests: pure-tone audiometry (fluctuating low-frequency SNHL, later flat/severe); MRI with gadolinium to exclude a vestibular schwannoma in any asymmetric SNHL; calorics often show reduced response on the affected side.
Adjuncts: electrocochleography (a raised SP/AP ratio supports hydrops), VEMPs, and delayed intravenous/intratympanic gadolinium MRI which can directly visualise hydrops — supportive rather than mandatory.

💊 Management — the therapeutic ladder

Treatment escalates from to and finally , always managing the separately.

First-line: low-salt diet, reduced caffeine/alcohol/stress, and betahistine ± a diuretic to reduce attack frequency. Most patients are controlled at this level; treat coexisting migraine.
Intratympanic therapy: intratympanic steroids (non-destructive, preserve hearing) for poorly-controlled disease; intratympanic gentamicin ("chemical labyrinthectomy") ablates vestibular hair cells to stop vertigo but carries a hearing-loss risk, so it is reserved for unilateral disease with poor hearing/severe vertigo.
Surgery: endolymphatic sac decompression (hearing-preserving, modest evidence); vestibular neurectomy (abolishes vertigo, preserves hearing, but intracranial); labyrinthectomy (definitive for vertigo but sacrifices hearing — only for a non-serviceable ear). Add vestibular rehabilitation after ablation.
Acute attack: reassurance, lie still, and a short course of a vestibular sedative/antiemetic (prochlorperazine, antihistamine) for ≤72 hours; do not use long-term, as it impairs compensation.

⚠️ Mimics to exclude

Always exclude before settling on Ménière's.

Key mimics: a vestibular schwannoma (asymmetric SNHL — MRI mandatory), vestibular migraine (the commonest mimic: vertigo + migrainous features, often without true fluctuating SNHL), autoimmune inner-ear disease, and syphilis. Misdiagnosis leads to the wrong (sometimes ablative) treatment.

📈 Prognosis

Vertigo is controlled in most with lifestyle/medical measures; a minority need intratympanic or surgical therapy. Hearing tends to decline stepwise regardless, and disease may become bilateral — so hearing rehabilitation (aids, occasionally cochlear implant) and counselling are part of long-term care.

Prompt: "A 48-year-old has recurrent 1-hour vertigo attacks with a roaring tinnitus, ear fullness and fluctuating hearing on the left. Diagnose and manage."

Your locked answer:
"The tetrad of episodic vertigo, fluctuating SNHL, tinnitus and fullness is Ménière's disease — but unilateral SNHL means I must exclude a vestibular schwannoma with MRI first."

🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: Impaired endolymph homeostasis distends the endolymphatic compartment (hydrops). Episodic rupture of Reissner's membrane lets K⁺-rich endolymph contaminate the perilymph, depolarising and transiently disabling the cochlear and vestibular hair cells — producing an attack of vertigo with hearing change. Repeated insults cause cumulative hair-cell loss, so hearing declines stepwise even as the vertigo eventually burns out.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"Recurrent 1–2 h attacks of spinning with a roaring left tinnitus, fullness, and an audiogram showing fluctuating low-frequency left SNHL; MRI normal." → Ménière's: lifestyle + betahistine ± diuretic, vestibular rehab, hearing follow-up; escalate if uncontrolled.
"Frequent disabling vertigo and Tumarkin falls despite medical therapy, poor hearing in that ear." → escalate to intratympanic gentamicin (or labyrinthectomy given non-serviceable hearing); vestibular rehabilitation afterward.
Gentamicin is vestibulotoxic, preferentially ablating vestibular hair cells (titrated dosing) to abolish the abnormal vestibular signal and stop vertigo. The risk is cochlear toxicity → hearing loss, so it is reserved for unilateral disease with poor/sacrificeable hearing.
It aims to decompress/drain the sac while preserving hearing, but evidence is modest and debated (placebo effect concerns). It is offered to selected patients who want a hearing-preserving surgical option before ablative procedures.
Early: fluctuating low-frequency SNHL that recovers between attacks. Over years: the loss becomes fixed, flatter and more severe as hair cells are lost; some patients end with a "burnt-out" ear with poor hearing but few vertigo attacks.
It is the commonest mimic and is treated quite differently (migraine prophylaxis/triggers), so labelling it "Ménière's" exposes the patient to unnecessary diuretics or ablative therapy. Look for headache, photophobia, aura and a migraine history with preserved hearing.
