🔍 Differential diagnosis of a neck mass

The Overview is a full walk-through of the topic. Tap any coloured word for an extra exam pearl. Then work through all 5 tabs.

📖 Full topic explanation

A neck mass is approached by age, site (midline vs lateral), and duration. The governing rule: in an adult, a persistent neck mass is metastatic cancer until proven otherwise, worked up with ultrasound + FNAC and panendoscopy — never an open biopsy first.

🗂️ The framework

Triage by , , and apply the .

Age: in children/young adults, most masses are congenital or inflammatory; in adults >40, a persistent mass is neoplastic (metastatic SCC, lymphoma, thyroid/salivary) until excluded. Age reshapes the probabilities dramatically.
Site: midline masses — thyroglossal cyst, dermoid, thyroid, Delphian node; lateral masses — lymph nodes (commonest), branchial cyst, salivary gland (parotid/submandibular), carotid-body tumour, nerve-sheath tumour. Site narrows the differential quickly.
The rule: a neck mass persisting >3 weeks in an adult is metastatic head-and-neck cancer until proven otherwise — examine the upper aerodigestive tract and arrange US-FNAC; don't reassure or excise blindly.

🧒 Congenital masses

Key congenital lesions are the , the , and others (dermoid, cystic hygroma, laryngocele).

Thyroglossal duct cyst: a midline cyst that moves up on tongue protrusion and swallowing (attached to the hyoid/foramen caecum tract). Treated by Sistrunk's operation (excise the cyst + central hyoid + tract). Confirm normal thyroid first (rarely the only thyroid tissue).
Branchial cyst: a lateral cyst (2nd cleft) at the anterior border of the upper SCM, typically in young adults, sometimes presenting after an URTI. Beware: in an adult >40 a "branchial cyst" may be a cystic metastatic (HPV+) node — FNAC/p16 first.

🦠 Inflammatory masses

Inflammatory causes (commonest overall) include .

Inflammatory: reactive lymphadenitis (viral/bacterial URTI), abscess, infectious mononucleosis (EBV), tuberculous/atypical mycobacterial lymphadenitis (matted nodes, "cold abscess", collar-stud abscess), cat-scratch and toxoplasmosis. Usually tender/short-history in the young; persistence warrants work-up.

🎗️ Neoplastic masses

Neoplastic causes are , , , and the .

Metastatic SCC: a hard, often level-II node from a head-and-neck primary (or a cystic node in HPV+ oropharyngeal cancer). The most important adult diagnosis — examine the UADT and arrange FNAC + imaging (Topic 46).
Lymphoma: rubbery, painless, often multiple nodes ± B-symptoms (fever, night sweats, weight loss). Diagnosis needs architecture, so a core or excisional node biopsy is appropriate here (unlike SCC, where FNAC comes first).
Thyroid & salivary: a thyroid mass moves on swallowing (Topic 48); a parotid/submandibular mass sits over the gland (Topic 50). FNAC/US guide both; a parotid mass with facial palsy = malignant.
Carotid-body tumour (paraganglioma): a pulsatile mass at the carotid bifurcation that is mobile side-to-side but not vertically, splaying the carotids ("lyre sign" on angiography), ± a bruit. Do not biopsy (vascular); image (CT/MR angiography).

🔎 Investigation

The cornerstone is , with and the .

US + FNAC: first-line — ultrasound characterises the mass and guides FNAC, which gives cytology to direct further work-up. Bloods (FBC, EBV/HIV, TFTs) as indicated.
Further work-up: for suspected metastatic SCC → flexible nasendoscopy, EUA + panendoscopy, CT/MRI and PET-CT to find the primary; CT/MR angiography for vascular lesions; cross-sectional imaging for staging.
Trap: never perform an open/excisional biopsy of a neck node before FNAC in suspected SCC (seeding, compromises definitive surgery). The exception is suspected lymphoma, which needs a core/excisional biopsy for architecture after FNAC raises the suspicion.

📈 Approach summary

Combine age + site + duration with US-FNAC; treat congenital/inflammatory causes appropriately, refer suspected malignancy urgently, and remember the can't-miss adult metastatic node and the don't-biopsy vascular lesion.

🎯 Demonstrate it like an examiner wants

Commit first, then reveal the model structure and build the chain step by step.

Prompt: "A 58-year-old smoker has a hard, painless lump in the upper neck for 6 weeks. How do you assess?"

Your locked answer:
"A persistent hard neck mass in an adult smoker is metastatic head-and-neck SCC until proven otherwise — I examine the upper aerodigestive tract and do US-FNAC, not an open biopsy."
Note the level (often II), characterise the mass, and list the differential (metastatic SCC, lymphoma, thyroid/salivary, carotid body, congenital/inflammatory) weighted by age/site.
US + FNAC (± p16/EBV); flexible nasendoscopy; if SCC, EUA + panendoscopy, CT/MRI, PET-CT to find the primary. Avoid open biopsy in suspected SCC.
Treat per diagnosis: metastatic SCC → find/treat the primary + neck (Topic 46); lymphoma → core/excisional biopsy then haem-oncology; carotid body → vascular team; congenital/inflammatory → appropriate management.
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: A mucosal head-and-neck carcinoma metastasises along lymphatics to predictable cervical levels, so a hard upper-neck node in an adult smoker most often reflects an occult or overt UADT primary. The node's site and FNAC cytology (with p16/EBV) point to the source. Systematic endoscopy and imaging then localise the primary, while avoiding open biopsy preserves the integrity of definitive surgery.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"A 58-year-old smoker with a hard level-II node for 6 weeks." → metastatic SCC until excluded: US-FNAC (± p16), nasendoscopy, EUA/panendoscopy, CT/MRI/PET-CT to find the primary; treat per Topic 46.
"A 20-year-old with a midline neck lump that rises on swallowing and tongue protrusion." → thyroglossal duct cyst: US (confirm normal thyroid), then Sistrunk's operation; excise infected ones after settling.
It is safe, quick and avoids tumour seeding/scarring that would compromise definitive surgery in SCC. It triages the differential; only when lymphoma is suspected is a core/excisional biopsy needed for architecture.
Matted, sometimes fluctuant nodes ("cold abscess"), with a collar-stud abscess and overlying skin involvement, often with systemic features. Confirm with FNA/biopsy for AFB/culture/PCR and histology (caseating granulomas); treat with anti-TB therapy.
Most paediatric nodes are reactive, but a persistent (>4–6 weeks), enlarging, hard or matted node, or B-symptoms warrants work-up (US-FNAC/biopsy) for lymphoma, atypical mycobacteria, or rarely metastatic disease.
Vascular lesions — carotid-body/paraganglioma tumours (haemorrhage; image with angiography) and, in suspected SCC, avoid open biopsy before FNAC. Also image a pulsatile mass before any intervention.
