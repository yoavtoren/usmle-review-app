🦠⚠️ Tonsillitis complications

The Overview is a full walk-through of the topic. Tap any coloured word for an extra exam pearl. Then work through all 5 tabs.

📖 Full topic explanation

Complications of tonsillitis split into suppurative (local abscesses and deep-neck/vascular spread) and non-suppurative immune sequelae of group A streptococcus. The exam priorities are recognising quinsy, the deep-neck-space emergencies (including airway and Lemierre syndrome), and the immune complications.

🗺️ Classification

Group complications as versus .

Suppurative: peritonsillar abscess (quinsy), parapharyngeal and retropharyngeal abscess, descending deep-neck infection/mediastinitis, Lemierre syndrome, and airway obstruction. These are infections that need drainage/antibiotics and can be life-threatening.
Non-suppurative (post-streptococcal immune): acute rheumatic fever, post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis, scarlet fever, and PANDAS. These follow GABHS by immune mechanisms (molecular mimicry/immune complexes) rather than direct spread.

🔴 Peritonsillar abscess (quinsy)

Quinsy is the commonest suppurative complication: a , with a classic treated by .

Anatomy: pus collects in the peritonsillar space (between the tonsil capsule and the superior pharyngeal constrictor), usually superior to the tonsil. Often polymicrobial (streptococci + anaerobes). It is largely a disease of adolescents/young adults.
Classic features: severe unilateral sore throat, trismus, "hot-potato" muffled voice, drooling, uvular deviation to the opposite side, and a bulging peritonsillar swelling with the tonsil pushed medially; referred otalgia and tender nodes. Trismus distinguishes it from simple tonsillitis.
Management: needle aspiration or incision-and-drainage (under local anaesthesia), IV antibiotics covering streptococci + anaerobes (e.g. penicillin + metronidazole), analgesia, fluids and a single dose of steroid; admit. Consider interval/"quinsy" tonsillectomy after recurrent episodes.

🦠 Deep-neck space & vascular spread

Infection can spread to the , descend to the , or cause — all airway/sepsis emergencies.

Parapharyngeal/retropharyngeal abscess: spread into deep neck spaces causes neck swelling/stiffness, odynophagia and airway compromise; the retropharyngeal abscess is classically in young children (febrile, neck held still, drooling). Diagnose with contrast CT neck; treat with IV antibiotics ± surgical drainage and airway protection.
Descending mediastinitis: deep-neck pus can track down fascial planes into the mediastinum — a high-mortality emergency needing urgent CT chest/neck, IV antibiotics, surgical drainage and intensive care.
Lemierre syndrome: Fusobacterium necrophorum oropharyngeal infection causing septic thrombophlebitis of the internal jugular vein with septic emboli (classically to the lungs). Suspect with persisting fever, neck tenderness and pulmonary symptoms after a sore throat; diagnose with CT/Doppler and treat with prolonged IV antibiotics ± anticoagulation.

💉 Non-suppurative (immune) complications

GABHS triggers , , and .

Acute rheumatic fever: an immune (molecular-mimicry) reaction ~2–4 weeks after GABHS pharyngitis, diagnosed by the Jones criteria (carditis, polyarthritis, chorea, erythema marginatum, subcutaneous nodules + minor criteria). Carditis → rheumatic valve disease is the feared sequel; penicillin treatment/prophylaxis prevents it.
Post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis: immune-complex nephritis ~1–2 weeks after GABHS (pharyngitis or skin), with haematuria, hypertension and oedema. It is largely self-limiting; antibiotic treatment of GABHS does not reliably prevent it (unlike rheumatic fever).
Scarlet fever: GABHS strains producing erythrogenic exotoxin → a sandpaper rash, strawberry tongue, circumoral pallor and flushed cheeks with the sore throat. Treat with penicillin and notify public health; it is part of the streptococcal disease spectrum.

🚩 Assessment & airway

Any complicated tonsillitis needs with .

Red flags: stridor, drooling, respiratory distress, severe trismus, neck swelling/stiffness, sepsis or rapid deterioration. Prioritise the airway (senior ENT + anaesthetics), resuscitate, and give IV antibiotics/steroids before/with imaging.
Imaging: contrast CT of the neck (± chest) defines and localises deep-neck abscesses, vascular thrombosis (Lemierre) and mediastinal spread, guiding drainage. Bloods/cultures and (for immune sequelae) ASO titre, urinalysis and ECG/echo as indicated.

📈 Prognosis

Quinsy and most deep-neck infections resolve with prompt drainage and antibiotics; mediastinitis and Lemierre carry significant mortality and need aggressive multidisciplinary care. Rheumatic fever's prognosis hinges on preventing carditis through penicillin treatment and secondary prophylaxis.

🎯 Demonstrate it like an examiner wants

Commit first, then reveal the model structure and build the chain step by step.

Prompt: "A 22-year-old with tonsillitis now has a unilateral severe sore throat, trismus and a muffled voice. Manage."

Your locked answer:
"This is a quinsy (peritonsillar abscess). First I assess the airway and hydration, then drain it and give antibiotics."
Unilateral peritonsillar bulge, uvular deviation to the opposite side, trismus, hot-potato voice, tender ipsilateral nodes; exclude deeper spread (neck swelling/stiffness) suggesting a parapharyngeal abscess.
Bloods/cultures; contrast CT neck only if deep-neck spread/airway concern or diagnostic doubt. Most quinsies are a clinical diagnosis drained at the bedside.
Needle aspiration or incision-and-drainage under local anaesthesia; IV penicillin + metronidazole, fluids, analgesia, single-dose steroid; admit. Discuss interval tonsillectomy if recurrent.
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: Acute tonsillitis spreads beyond the tonsil capsule into the loose peritonsillar space, where pus collects between the capsule and the superior constrictor. The expanding abscess and muscle irritation cause trismus, displaces the uvula, and muffles the voice. Drainage decompresses the collection, and antibiotics covering streptococci and anaerobes clear the infection.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"Unilateral severe sore throat, trismus, hot-potato voice, uvula pushed across." → peritonsillar abscess: airway/hydration check, aspiration/I&D, IV penicillin + metronidazole, steroid, admit; interval tonsillectomy if recurrent.
"A young adult, days after a sore throat, has swinging fever, a tender neck and pleuritic chest pain with lung nodules." → Lemierre: blood cultures (Fusobacterium), CT neck/chest + IJV Doppler, prolonged IV antibiotics (e.g. β-lactam + metronidazole) ± anticoagulation.
Abscesses in the parapharyngeal/retropharyngeal spaces displace and compress the airway, and pus can track down the fascial planes into the mediastinum (mediastinitis). Hence early airway assessment, CT, antibiotics and surgical drainage with ICU support.
Both are effective; needle aspiration is simpler and confirms pus, while I&D gives more complete drainage and is preferred for large/recurrent collections. Both are combined with antibiotics; quinsy tonsillectomy ("hot" or interval) is reserved for recurrence/complications.
Molecular mimicry: antibodies against streptococcal M-protein cross-react with cardiac (and other) tissue, causing pancarditis and, chronically, valve disease (especially mitral stenosis). Secondary penicillin prophylaxis prevents recurrent attacks and progressive valve damage.
Paediatric Autoimmune Neuropsychiatric Disorders Associated with Streptococci — abrupt-onset OCD/tics temporally related to GABHS infection, thought to be immune-mediated. It is a recognised (debated) non-suppurative association and occasionally cited as a tonsillectomy consideration.
