🗣️ Laryngeal injuries & innervation disorders

📖 Full topic explanation

Two themes: laryngeal trauma (where the airway is the immediate threat) and vocal-cord paralysis (where the innervation anatomy explains the cause and the voice/airway pattern). The recurrent and superior laryngeal nerves are the spine of this topic.

🧬 Laryngeal innervation

The larynx is supplied by two branches of the vagus: the and the , the .

Superior laryngeal nerve (SLN): its external branch supplies the cricothyroid (tenses the cord → controls pitch); its internal branch is sensory above the cords (protective cough). SLN palsy causes voice fatigue and loss of high notes, and reduced supraglottic sensation → aspiration risk.
Recurrent laryngeal nerve (RLN): supplies all intrinsic muscles except cricothyroid and is sensory below the cords. The key abductor it powers is the posterior cricoarytenoid — the only cord abductor — so RLN palsy threatens both voice and airway.
Left RLN course: the left RLN loops under the aortic arch, giving it a long intrathoracic course, so it is more often affected by chest pathology (lung/mediastinal tumour, aortic aneurysm) than the right (which loops under the subclavian artery).

🤕 Laryngeal trauma

Blunt or penetrating laryngeal injury is an , recognised by , including the catastrophic ; are an important iatrogenic group.

Airway first: blunt trauma (steering wheel, "clothesline") can fracture the thyroid/cricoid cartilage and cause mucosal tears, haematoma and oedema → rapidly progressive obstruction. Manage the airway early; if the larynx is disrupted, a careful awake tracheostomy is often safer than intubation (which can create a false passage or worsen disruption).
Signs: hoarseness/voice change, surgical emphysema, haemoptysis, stridor, dysphagia/odynophagia, anterior neck pain, and loss of the normal thyroid/laryngeal prominence. Assess with flexible laryngoscopy and CT (cartilage fractures) once the airway is safe.
Laryngotracheal separation: cricotracheal disruption is rapidly fatal; clues are severe surgical emphysema and respiratory distress. Avoid blind intubation; secure the airway distal to the injury (tracheostomy) and repair urgently.
Intubation injuries: prolonged or traumatic intubation causes posterior glottic/subglottic stenosis, vocal-process granuloma, arytenoid dislocation, and vocal-cord palsy. Suspect with persistent hoarseness/stridor after extubation; assess by laryngoscopy.

🎙️ Vocal-cord paralysis

Paralysis is classified by ; the commonest cause is , but a unilateral palsy .

Unilateral vs bilateral: unilateral palsy → a breathy, weak voice, poor cough and aspiration (the cord lies paramedian and the other compensates) — voice problem, not airway. Bilateral palsy → both cords paramedian, a reasonable voice but airway obstruction/stridor — an airway problem that may need tracheostomy.
Causes: iatrogenic (thyroid/parathyroid/neck/chest surgery) is the commonest; also malignancy (thyroid, lung, oesophageal along the RLN), idiopathic/viral, neurological, trauma, and aortic aneurysm. Always seek a cause.
Don't-miss: an unexplained unilateral cord palsy (especially left) demands imaging of the entire nerve course from skull base to mediastinum (CT neck + chest) to exclude a lung, thyroid or oesophageal tumour or other compressive lesion.

🔎 Investigation

Assess with , and distinguish palsy from .

Work-up: flexible laryngoscopy (cord position/mobility), CT skull base to mediastinum for an idiopathic unilateral palsy (trace the nerve), and laryngeal EMG to confirm denervation and assess prognosis/recovery potential.
Trap — fixation vs palsy: a fixed, immobile cord can be due to cricoarytenoid joint fixation (after intubation, trauma, arthritis or tumour) rather than neural palsy. Palpation of the arytenoid under anaesthesia and EMG distinguish them — important because management differs.

💊 Management

Treatment depends on uni- vs bilateral: ; ; and repair/stabilisation for trauma.

Unilateral: if recovery is possible, speech therapy ± temporary injection medialisation; for permanent palsy, injection augmentation or medialisation (type I) thyroplasty ± arytenoid adduction to improve voice and protect the airway from aspiration.
Bilateral: the priority is the airway — a tracheostomy acutely, then airway-widening procedures (cordotomy, arytenoidectomy, or cord lateralisation) trading voice for a safe airway; reinnervation/pacing in selected cases.

📈 Prognosis

Many traumatic/viral palsies recover over months (hence early temporary measures); permanent palsies are well rehabilitated by phonosurgery. Laryngeal-trauma outcomes depend on early airway control and accurate cartilage repair; the overriding rule is "airway first, voice second".

🎯 Demonstrate it like an examiner wants

Commit first, then reveal the model structure and build the chain step by step.

Prompt: "A patient has a breathy voice and cough after thyroidectomy; another presents with stridor after a road traffic accident with neck bruising. Discuss."

Your locked answer:
"The post-thyroidectomy breathy voice is a unilateral RLN palsy (voice/aspiration problem); the trauma patient with stridor is a laryngeal injury (airway emergency) — airway first."
RLN supplies all intrinsics except cricothyroid; thyroid surgery is the commonest iatrogenic cause. In trauma, cartilage fracture/oedema threatens the airway.
Palsy: flexible laryngoscopy (paramedian cord), and if not clearly surgical, CT neck-to-chest + EMG. Trauma: secure the airway, then flexible scope + CT for cartilage injury.
Unilateral palsy: speech therapy ± injection/medialisation thyroplasty for voice and aspiration. Trauma: awake tracheostomy if disrupted, then repair mucosa/cartilage; watch for late stenosis.
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: The recurrent laryngeal nerve drives the posterior cricoarytenoid (the sole abductor) and the adductors; injuring it leaves the cord paramedian, so a single palsy gives a breathy voice while bilateral palsy obstructs the airway. In trauma, cartilage fracture, mucosal tear and submucosal haematoma rapidly narrow the laryngeal lumen. Securing the airway distal to the disruption and accurately repairing the framework restores both ventilation and voice.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"Breathy voice, weak cough and mild aspiration after thyroid surgery; laryngoscopy shows a paramedian immobile right cord." → unilateral RLN palsy: speech therapy, swallow safety; if persistent, injection or medialisation thyroplasty. Many recover over months.
"A cyclist hit a wire; hoarse voice, neck crepitus and worsening stridor." → laryngeal fracture: secure the airway (awake tracheostomy if disrupted), flexible scope + CT, repair mucosa/cartilage; monitor for late stenosis.
Median, paramedian (typical of RLN palsy — good voice/airway compromise pattern depending on uni/bilateral), intermediate ("cadaveric", combined RLN+SLN), and abducted. The position reflects which nerves are affected and predicts voice vs airway impact.
If the nerve is bruised/neuropraxic rather than divided, axonal recovery occurs over months, and the contralateral cord may compensate by crossing the midline. Hence temporary measures (injection) and speech therapy precede permanent surgery, guided by EMG.
Tracheostomy for acute airway compromise, then airway-widening surgery — posterior cordotomy, arytenoidectomy, or cord lateralisation (trading some voice quality for a patent airway); reinnervation or laryngeal pacing in selected cases.
Laryngotracheal stenosis (subglottic/glottic) from scarring, presenting with progressive stridor/exertional dyspnoea and voice change. It is assessed by laryngoscopy/CT and managed with endoscopic dilatation/laser or open laryngotracheal reconstruction.
