🔻 Hypopharyngeal tumors

📖 Full topic explanation

Hypopharyngeal cancer is an aggressive, usually late-presenting squamous cell carcinoma of the pyriform sinus, postcricoid region or posterior wall. Because the hypopharynx is a "silent" area with rich lymphatics and submucosal spread, it has the worst prognosis of the head-and-neck sites.

🗂️ Subsites & aetiology

The subsites are the ; the cause is usually , with for postcricoid disease.

Subsites: pyriform (piriform) sinus (commonest), postcricoid, and posterior pharyngeal wall. Site influences spread and reconstruction; postcricoid lesions can extend circumferentially into the oesophagus.
Aetiology: tobacco and alcohol (synergistic), as for other SCC. Nutritional deficiency also contributes.
Plummer–Vinson syndrome: postcricoid web + iron-deficiency anaemia + dysphagia, classically in middle-aged women, is premalignant for postcricoid carcinoma — a distinctive aetiology for this subsite.

👤 Presentation

It presents , with , and frequently a .

Late presentation: the hypopharynx is "silent", so tumours are usually advanced at diagnosis — a key reason for the poor prognosis. Weight loss is common.
Symptoms: progressive dysphagia/odynophagia, referred otalgia (CN IX/X), a globus sensation, and hoarseness if the larynx/recurrent laryngeal nerve is involved; later, aspiration and airway compromise.
Neck node: a high rate of (often bilateral) cervical nodal metastasis is common at presentation (rich lymphatics), and a neck lump may be the first sign.

🧬 Behaviour — why it is dangerous

Hypopharyngeal SCC shows .

Aggressive behaviour: submucosal spread and skip lesions mean the true extent exceeds the visible mucosal tumour; early and bilateral nodal metastasis and a high rate of synchronous second primaries (oesophagus/lung) all worsen outcomes — hence panendoscopy and generous margins.

🔎 Diagnosis & staging

Assess with and .

Endoscopy: flexible nasendoscopy (pooling of saliva in the pyriform fossa is a clue) and rigid endoscopy/panendoscopy + biopsy to map the (often submucosal) extent and exclude synchronous oesophageal/airway primaries.
Imaging: CT/MRI (local extent, cartilage, nodes), barium swallow (postcricoid extent), ultrasound-guided FNAC of nodes, and PET-CT for distant disease/second primary. Stage with TNM.

💊 Treatment

Options are or , always with .

Chemoradiotherapy: concurrent chemoRT is used for organ preservation in suitable tumours, sparing the larynx/swallowing where possible.
Surgery: for advanced/bulky disease or chemoRT failure — partial or total pharyngolaryngectomy with reconstruction (gastric pull-up, jejunal free flap, or tubed fasciocutaneous (ALT) flap) ± adjuvant (chemo)RT. Total laryngectomy may be needed, with voice rehabilitation (Topic 29).
Neck: bilateral neck treatment (dissection or radiation) is usual because of the high rate of bilateral occult nodal disease.

📈 Prognosis

Hypopharyngeal SCC has the poorest prognosis of head-and-neck sites owing to late presentation, submucosal/nodal spread and second primaries. Function (swallowing/voice) and survival are both challenged, making MDT care and rehabilitation essential.

🎯 Demonstrate it like an examiner wants

Commit first, then reveal the model structure and build the chain step by step.

Prompt: "A 65-year-old smoker has progressive dysphagia, referred otalgia, hoarseness and a neck node. How do you proceed?"

Your locked answer:
"Progressive dysphagia + referred otalgia + hoarseness + a neck node in a smoker is a hypopharyngeal carcinoma until excluded — an aggressive, late-presenting cancer."
Pyriform sinus most likely; expect submucosal spread, skip lesions and early/bilateral nodes; look for pooling of saliva on nasendoscopy.
Nasendoscopy, panendoscopy + biopsy (exclude synchronous primaries), CT/MRI, US-FNAC of nodes, PET-CT; assign TNM; assess airway and nutrition.
Organ-preserving chemoradiotherapy or pharyngolaryngectomy + reconstruction (gastric pull-up/jejunal/ALT flap) with bilateral neck treatment; voice/swallow rehabilitation; manage the guarded prognosis honestly.
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: Carcinogen exposure transforms the hypopharyngeal mucosa, but the region's lack of early symptoms lets the tumour grow and spread submucosally beyond its visible margin. Its rich lymphatic network seeds cervical (often bilateral) nodes early, and field cancerisation produces synchronous oesophageal/airway primaries. These factors explain both the late stage at diagnosis and the poor survival despite aggressive treatment.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"A smoker with progressive dysphagia, otalgia, hoarseness and a level-III node; pooling of saliva on the right pyriform fossa." → hypopharyngeal SCC: panendoscopy + biopsy, CT/MRI, US-FNAC, PET-CT; MDT — chemoRT or pharyngolaryngectomy + reconstruction with bilateral neck treatment.
"A middle-aged woman with iron-deficiency anaemia, a postcricoid web and worsening dysphagia, now with a mass." → biopsy for postcricoid SCC; correct iron deficiency; stage and treat (often surgery + reconstruction); survey the syndrome.
Because of submucosal spread and skip lesions, the microscopic extent exceeds the visible tumour, so wide margins, frozen-section control and panendoscopic mapping are needed — incomplete excision drives local recurrence.
Gastric pull-up (especially with oesophageal extension), free jejunal flap, or a tubed fasciocutaneous (anterolateral thigh) flap — restoring swallowing continuity after total pharyngolaryngectomy. Each has trade-offs in fistula, stricture and voice.
A pharyngocutaneous fistula — common after salvage surgery/irradiated tissue. Manage with wound care, drainage, nutrition (NBM/enteral feeding) and antibiotics; large/persistent fistulae need flap repair.
Secure the airway first (senior ENT/anaesthetics; awake tracheostomy if needed) before definitive staging/treatment — bulky hypopharyngeal/laryngeal tumours can obstruct and the airway always takes priority.
