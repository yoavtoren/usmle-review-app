🥜 Foreign bodies of the upper respiratory & digestive tract

The Overview is a full walk-through of the topic. Tap any coloured word for an extra exam pearl. Then work through all 5 tabs.

📖 Full topic explanation

Foreign bodies split into the airway (an immediate asphyxia risk) and the digestive tract (mostly the oesophagus). The exam wants the choking algorithm, the bronchial-FB picture in children, and the two digestive emergencies — button battery and sharp objects.

🫁 Airway foreign bodies

Airway FBs are mainly a problem of ; a laryngeal/tracheal FB causes , while a bronchial FB lodges more often on the and may present late, with .

Who/what: toddlers exploring with the mouth; the classic object is a peanut/nut (organic FBs swell and incite intense inflammation). Risk factors: age <3, no molars, running/playing while eating.
Laryngeal/tracheal FB = asphyxia: sudden choking, stridor, aphonia, cyanosis. Complete obstruction in a conscious patient → back blows then abdominal thrusts (Heimlich) (chest thrusts in infants/pregnant); if unconscious → CPR + laryngoscopy/removal. This is the most time-critical scenario.
Right main bronchus: a bronchial FB lodges more commonly on the right because the right main bronchus is wider, shorter and more vertical. It causes cough, unilateral wheeze and reduced air entry.
Presentation & imaging: may be acute (witnessed choking) or delayed (chronic cough, unilateral wheeze, recurrent/non-resolving pneumonia). Most organic FBs are radiolucent, so the CXR may be normal or show obstructive hyperinflation/air trapping on the affected side (worse on expiratory films) or collapse. A normal CXR does not exclude it.

🔬 Airway-FB management

The definitive treatment is , and a strong history justifies it even with normal imaging.

Rigid bronchoscopy under general anaesthesia is both diagnostic and therapeutic for a suspected airway FB; a convincing history of a choking episode mandates bronchoscopy regardless of a normal CXR. Delayed removal risks pneumonia, bronchiectasis and granulation.

🍽️ Digestive-tract foreign bodies

Most lodge at the ; the emergencies are a and , while are the classic pharyngeal FB.

Oesophageal FBs: impact at the cricopharyngeus (~15 cm — narrowest), then the aortic/bronchial crossing and the hiatus. Present with dysphagia, drooling, the sensation of something stuck, and retrosternal pain (see Topic 23).
Button battery = emergency: in the oesophagus it causes liquefactive (alkaline) necrosis within hours with perforation/fistula risk → immediate endoscopic removal; never delay. (In the canal/nose, the same principle: urgent removal, no irrigation.)
Other emergencies: sharp/pointed objects, multiple magnets, and complete obstruction with inability to swallow saliva (drooling) require urgent endoscopic removal; airway compromise takes priority.
Fish bones: commonly lodge in the tonsil, base of tongue, vallecula or pyriform fossa. Examine carefully (and with flexible endoscopy); many small bones are not radio-opaque, so a normal X-ray doesn't exclude one — persistent symptoms warrant endoscopic examination/removal.

🔎 Assessment

Take a focused with targeted , prioritising the airway throughout.

History/exam: what, when, witnessed choking?; symptoms (cough/stridor/wheeze vs dysphagia/drooling); airway and respiratory assessment; oropharyngeal and flexible endoscopic examination for pharyngeal/laryngeal FBs.
Imaging: plain radiographs (neck/chest) — a button battery shows a double-ring/halo sign; airway FBs may show hyperinflation/air trapping or collapse; remember many FBs (peanuts, fish bones, plastic) are radiolucent, so imaging cannot exclude them.

💊 Management principle

Definitive treatment is , with .

Removal: rigid bronchoscopy for airway FBs; flexible/rigid oesophagoscopy for oesophageal FBs (emergency for battery/sharp/obstruction). Most are done under GA with a shared airway and skilled anaesthesia.
Airway first: in choking/obstruction, basic life-support manoeuvres (back blows/abdominal thrusts; CPR if unconscious) precede everything; keep a calm, controlled approach and senior ENT/anaesthetic help for theatre removal.

📈 Outcome

Prompt removal gives excellent outcomes; delays cause pneumonia/bronchiectasis (airway) or perforation/stricture (digestive — especially batteries). Prevention (supervision, age-appropriate foods, battery safety) is an important counselling point.

🧠 Thinking questions (tap to reveal)

The right main bronchus is wider, shorter and more vertical, so aspirated objects pass into it preferentially. The result is unilateral wheeze, reduced air entry and air trapping on that side.
A convincing choking history mandates rigid bronchoscopy regardless of a normal CXR, because organic FBs (peanuts) are radiolucent and a CXR can be normal early. Delay risks pneumonia/bronchiectasis.
Button batteries (liquefactive necrosis/fistula), sharp/pointed objects, multiple magnets, and complete obstruction with drooling. All need urgent endoscopic removal; airway compromise takes priority.
Tonsil, base of tongue, vallecula and pyriform fossa. Examine carefully with a good light and flexible nasendoscopy; many are not radio-opaque, so persistent symptoms justify endoscopic examination even with a normal X-ray.
Conscious, partial obstruction: encourage coughing. Conscious, complete: 5 back blows then 5 abdominal thrusts (Heimlich) — chest thrusts in infants/pregnancy — repeat. Unconscious: start CPR and attempt removal under direct vision (laryngoscopy/Magill forceps).
Recurrent or non-resolving pneumonia, persistent cough/wheeze, lobar collapse, bronchiectasis and granulation tissue. A "non-resolving pneumonia" in a child should prompt consideration of an inhaled FB.

🎯 Demonstrate it like an examiner wants

Commit first, then reveal the model structure and build the chain step by step.

Prompt: "A 2-year-old had a sudden coughing fit eating peanuts; now has a unilateral wheeze but a normal CXR. How do you proceed?"

Your locked answer:
"A witnessed choking episode with a unilateral wheeze is an inhaled foreign body (likely right bronchus) — a normal CXR does not exclude it."
Confirm the child is currently stable (not in extremis); examine for stridor, unilateral reduced air entry/wheeze. Keep calm; avoid distressing manoeuvres if partially obstructed.
Peanuts are radiolucent; an inspiratory CXR may be normal, an expiratory film may show air trapping. But imaging is supportive only — the history drives the decision.
Arrange rigid bronchoscopy under GA (diagnostic + therapeutic) with skilled anaesthesia; remove the FB, treat secondary infection, and counsel on prevention.
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: An aspirated peanut passes preferentially into the wider, more vertical right main bronchus and acts as a ball-valve, letting air in but not out, which traps air distally (hyperinflation). Being radiolucent it is invisible on plain film, so the CXR is often normal. The organic material also incites mucosal inflammation, so delay leads to obstruction, pneumonia and eventually bronchiectasis — hence prompt rigid bronchoscopy.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"Sudden choking on a peanut, then a persistent cough and right-sided wheeze." → airway FB: stabilise, recognise the radiolucency caveat, arrange rigid bronchoscopy; treat any secondary pneumonia; counsel prevention.
"A toddler swallowed a 'coin'; the X-ray shows a double-ring sign in the oesophagus." → button battery emergency: immediate endoscopic removal, inspect for mucosal injury, observe for delayed perforation/fistula/stricture.
Acute: witnessed choking, cough, stridor/wheeze, distress. Delayed: the choking episode is forgotten and the child presents with chronic cough, focal wheeze, or recurrent/non-resolving pneumonia. Both warrant bronchoscopy if suspected.
A shared airway between surgeon and anaesthetist, the risk of fragmenting/displacing the FB (further obstruction), hypoxia during manipulation, and laryngospasm. It requires an experienced team, often spontaneous ventilation, and readiness for total obstruction.
They swell with moisture and incite intense mucosal inflammation and granulation (vegetal/peanut "bronchitis"), making them harder to remove and more likely to cause obstruction and infection the longer they remain — a reason for prompt removal.
A small, smooth, blunt object beyond the oesophagus in an asymptomatic patient usually passes spontaneously and can be observed. Batteries, sharps, multiple magnets, large objects, or any oesophageal lodgement/obstruction need active removal.
