👁️ Complications of rhinosinusitis

The Overview is a full walk-through of the topic. Tap any coloured word for an extra exam pearl. Then work through all 5 tabs.

📖 Full topic explanation

Sinusitis complications are grouped as orbital, intracranial and bony. They arise mostly from acute sinusitis (ethmoiditis in children) and are sight- and life-threatening, so the highest-yield skills are recognising the orbital red flags and the Chandler classification.

🗺️ Classification & spread

Complications are , spreading by , classically from .

Three families: orbital (commonest — periorbital → cavernous sinus), intracranial (meningitis, abscess, sinus thrombosis), and bony (osteomyelitis, Pott's puffy tumour, mucocele). Acute sinusitis (especially frontal/ethmoid) is the usual source.
Routes: the lamina papyracea (paper-thin medial orbital wall) lets ethmoid infection enter the orbit; the thin posterior tables and the valveless ophthalmic/diploic veins allow retrograde spread to the cavernous sinus and cranium.
Demographics: orbital complications are commonest in children, typically from acute ethmoiditis, because the lamina papyracea is thin and may be dehiscent. Frontal-sinus complications (Pott's puffy tumour, intracranial) cluster in adolescents.

👁️ Orbital complications (Chandler)

The orbital complications follow the ; the crucial distinction is , and the sight-threatening signs of a .

Chandler I–V: I preseptal cellulitis → II orbital (postseptal) cellulitis → III subperiosteal abscess → IV orbital abscess → V cavernous sinus thrombosis. Rising grade = rising threat to vision and life.
Preseptal vs postseptal (the key call): preseptal = lid swelling/erythema with normal eye movements, normal vision, no proptosis (anterior to the orbital septum — manage with antibiotics, observe). Postseptal = proptosis, chemosis, painful/restricted eye movements (ophthalmoplegia), reduced acuity/colour vision — needs CT and urgent intervention.
Sight-threatening: a subperiosteal or orbital abscess causes proptosis, ophthalmoplegia and reduced visual acuity / a relative afferent pupillary defect (RAPD) — a surgical emergency to drain and decompress the orbit before irreversible optic-nerve damage/blindness.

🧠 Intracranial complications

Intracranial spread causes and the dangerous .

Meningitis/abscess: meningitis, epidural/subdural empyema, and frontal-lobe abscess (from frontal/ethmoid sinusitis) present with severe headache, fever, vomiting, seizures, focal deficits and altered consciousness. Need contrast CT/MRI, IV antibiotics crossing the BBB, and neurosurgical drainage.
Cavernous sinus thrombosis: retrograde spread (often from sphenoid/ethmoid) → headache, proptosis, chemosis, and palsies of CN III, IV, V1, V2 and VI (which run in/through the sinus), often becoming bilateral (the sinuses communicate). A life-threatening emergency needing IV antibiotics ± anticoagulation.

🦴 Bony complications

Bone involvement causes , and chronic obstruction causes a .

Pott's puffy tumour: frontal-bone osteomyelitis with a subperiosteal abscess → a doughy forehead swelling, classically in adolescents from frontal sinusitis; often coexists with intracranial spread. Needs IV antibiotics and surgical drainage ± frontal-sinus surgery.
Mucocele: an obstructed sinus fills with mucus and expands, eroding bone (commonest in the frontal/ethmoid sinuses) → proptosis, globe displacement and diplopia. Treated by marsupialisation/drainage (usually endoscopic).

🔎 Management

Suspected complications need , with .

Work-up: contrast CT of the sinuses, orbit and brain (± MRI for intracranial/sinus-thrombosis detail), bloods/cultures, and urgent ophthalmology and neurosurgery involvement. Start IV broad-spectrum antibiotics crossing the BBB immediately.
Surgery: drain a subperiosteal/orbital abscess (endoscopic or external) and the source sinus, and decompress the orbit if vision is threatened; neurosurgical drainage of intracranial collections. Postseptal disease with any visual deterioration is a surgical emergency.

📈 Prognosis

Preseptal cellulitis does well on antibiotics; postseptal disease and intracranial complications can cause blindness or death if treatment is delayed. Early recognition of the orbital red flags, prompt imaging and timely drainage are what save sight and life.

🎯 Demonstrate it like an examiner wants

Commit first, then reveal the model structure and build the chain step by step.

Prompt: "A child with a cold develops a red, swollen, proptosed eye that won't move properly and blurred vision. Manage."

Your locked answer:
"Proptosis, ophthalmoplegia and reduced vision after sinusitis is postseptal orbital cellulitis ± abscess — a sight-threatening emergency from ethmoiditis until proven otherwise."
Document visual acuity, colour vision, pupils (RAPD), proptosis and eye movements; this is the postseptal end of the Chandler spectrum.
Contrast CT of sinuses + orbits (± brain), bloods/cultures, and immediate ophthalmology + ENT (± neurosurgery) review; start IV antibiotics at once.
IV broad-spectrum antibiotics crossing the BBB; surgical drainage of any abscess and the source sinus (endoscopic/external) + orbital decompression if vision is threatened or no improvement in ~24–48 h.
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: Acute ethmoiditis breaches the thin lamina papyracea, letting infection enter the orbit and form a subperiosteal then orbital abscess. Rising orbital pressure and inflammation compress the optic nerve and its blood supply, threatening vision (proptosis, ophthalmoplegia, RAPD). Draining the pus and the source sinus, with antibiotics, decompresses the orbit before irreversible optic-nerve injury.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"A child with ethmoiditis has proptosis, restricted painful eye movements and blurred vision." → postseptal orbital cellulitis ± subperiosteal abscess: assess vision/RAPD, contrast CT, IV antibiotics, urgent ENT/ophthalmology; drain abscess + source sinus if vision threatened/no improvement.
"A teenager with frontal sinusitis develops a tender, doughy forehead swelling and headache." → Pott's puffy tumour: contrast CT/MRI (exclude intracranial abscess), IV antibiotics, and surgical drainage ± frontal-sinus surgery; neurosurgical input if intracranial.
Preseptal cellulitis and many small medial subperiosteal abscesses in young children may settle on IV antibiotics with close monitoring; large/lateral/superior abscesses, any visual deterioration, or failure to improve in 24–48 h require surgical drainage and orbital decompression.
The two cavernous sinuses communicate via intercavernous sinuses, so thrombus and infection spread across the midline — producing bilateral proptosis, chemosis and cranial-nerve palsies, a hallmark distinguishing it from simple orbital cellulitis.
Severe headache, fever, vomiting, seizures, focal neurological deficits and reduced consciousness — from meningitis, epidural/subdural empyema or a frontal-lobe abscess (often from frontal/ethmoid disease). Urgent contrast CT/MRI, IV antibiotics and neurosurgical drainage.
A medial subperiosteal abscess along the lamina papyracea can be drained endoscopically through the ethmoid, simultaneously clearing the source sinus, avoiding an external scar. Lateral/superior collections or limited access may still need an external approach.
