👂🎗️ Ear tumors

📖 Full topic explanation

Ear tumours are organised by site: external ear (sun-related skin cancers), external canal/middle ear (SCC, glomus), and the temporal bone/skull base (glomus jugulare, temporal-bone SCC). The recurring rules: biopsy a non-healing canal lesion, and never biopsy a vascular retrotympanic mass — image it first.

👂 External ear (pinna & canal)

The pinna is a common site of ; the external canal can harbour .

Pinna skin cancers: basal cell carcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma and melanoma on sun-exposed skin (helix/antihelix); BCC is commonest, SCC more aggressive on the ear with nodal risk. Treat by excision (± reconstruction) with margins; sun protection counselling.
EAC carcinoma: usually SCC, presenting with chronic otorrhoea, bleeding, pain, granulation or a mass — it mimics chronic/necrotizing otitis externa, so biopsy any non-healing canal granulation. Ceruminous-gland tumours also occur. Spread is via the fissures of Santorini to the parotid/skull base.

🥁 Middle ear & temporal bone

The middle ear/temporal bone harbour and the vascular .

Temporal-bone/middle-ear SCC: often on a background of chronic suppurative otitis media; presents with bloody otorrhoea, deep pain, hearing loss and facial palsy. Staged by the Pittsburgh system; treated by temporal-bone resection + radiotherapy. A facial palsy in a chronically discharging ear must raise this (and cholesteatoma).
Glomus tumours (paragangliomas): glomus tympanicum (on the promontory — pulsatile tinnitus, a red retrotympanic mass, Brown sign) and glomus jugulare (jugular foramen — pulsatile tinnitus, conductive loss, and lower cranial-nerve palsies IX–XII). Highly vascular and slow-growing.

🩸 Paraganglioma specifics

Paragangliomas have important and a strict .

Genetics/secretion: associated with SDH gene mutations, can be multicentric/familial, and a minority secrete catecholamines — screen with plasma/urinary metanephrines if there are suggestive symptoms (and before any surgery).
Trap — do NOT biopsy a pulsatile/red middle-ear mass: it is likely a vascular glomus (or an aberrant carotid/high jugular bulb) and biopsy risks catastrophic bleeding. Image first (CT, MRI/MRA, angiography).

🔎 Diagnosis & 💊 Treatment

Use , then .

Diagnosis: biopsy a non-healing canal/skin lesion (SCC/BCC/melanoma); for vascular/temporal-bone lesions, CT (bone) + MRI/MRA (extent/flow voids) ± angiography; assess cranial nerves and hearing. Glomus shows a "salt-and-pepper" appearance on MRI.
Treatment: skin cancers → excision ± reconstruction (± neck for SCC); EAC/temporal-bone SCC → temporal-bone resection + radiotherapy; glomus tumours → surgery (often with pre-operative embolisation), radiotherapy/stereotactic radiosurgery, or observation in the elderly; vestibular schwannoma (Topic 52) is the commonest CPA tumour.

📈 Prognosis

Pinna skin cancers have a good prognosis with adequate excision (SCC carries nodal risk). Temporal-bone SCC is aggressive and stage-dependent. Glomus tumours are usually benign and slow-growing but locally complex at the skull base, with cranial-nerve morbidity.

🎯 Demonstrate it like an examiner wants

Commit first, then reveal the model structure and build the chain step by step.

Prompt: "A patient has pulsatile tinnitus and a red mass behind the eardrum. How do you proceed?"

Your locked answer:
"A pulsatile red retrotympanic mass is a vascular lesion (glomus tumour or aberrant carotid/high jugular bulb) — I image before any biopsy or myringotomy to avoid catastrophic bleeding."
Glomus tympanicum (promontory) vs jugulare (jugular foramen + IX–XII palsies) vs vascular anomaly; look for Brown sign and lower-cranial-nerve signs.
CT (bone), MRI/MRA (extent/flow voids), angiography; screen metanephrines (catecholamine secretion); audiometry and cranial-nerve assessment; consider SDH genetic testing.
Surgery (often with pre-operative embolisation), radiotherapy/stereotactic radiosurgery, or observation (elderly/comorbid), depending on size, cranial-nerve status and patient factors.
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: A glomus tumour is a highly vascular paraganglioma arising from paraganglia on the promontory (tympanicum) or in the jugular foramen (jugulare). Its rich blood supply transmits arterial pulsation to the middle ear (pulsatile tinnitus) and makes blind biopsy dangerous, while jugular-foramen growth compresses the lower cranial nerves. Pre-operative embolisation reduces vascularity before surgical removal at the skull base.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"An elderly patient with chronic bloody otorrhoea, pain and canal granulation not settling on drops." → biopsy the granulation (exclude SCC and necrotizing OE), CT/MRI, Pittsburgh staging; temporal-bone resection + radiotherapy for confirmed SCC.
"Pulsatile tinnitus, conductive loss and a hoarse voice with tongue deviation." → suspect glomus jugulare with lower-cranial-nerve palsies: image (CT/MRI/MRA/angiography), screen metanephrines; treat by embolisation + surgery or radiotherapy/radiosurgery via the skull-base MDT.
A T-staging system for EAC/temporal-bone SCC based on bony erosion, soft-tissue/facial-nerve involvement and extent (canal-confined → bone/medial-wall → beyond/facial nerve/dura). It guides the extent of temporal-bone resection and prognosis.
A minority of head-and-neck paragangliomas secrete catecholamines; an unrecognised secretor risks an intra-operative hypertensive crisis. Screen with plasma/urinary metanephrines and manage (alpha-blockade) before surgery, as for phaeochromocytoma.
Because these tumours are benign and slow-growing, observation with serial imaging or stereotactic radiosurgery is often preferred over morbid skull-base surgery, especially when cranial-nerve function is intact and comorbidity is high.
Generally good with adequate excision, but SCC on the ear has a higher nodal-metastasis risk than facial SCC; risk rises with size, depth, perineural invasion, recurrence and immunosuppression — prompting parotid/neck assessment and adjuvant treatment.
