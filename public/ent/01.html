👂 External otitis & foreign body of the external auditory meatus

📖 Full topic explanation

External-ear disease runs from trivial wax impaction to necrotizing otitis externa, which can kill. This overview moves outward-in: anatomy and physiology first, then every disease entity in depth, then foreign bodies, examination and treatment.

🗂️ Definition & classification

The external ear comprises the auricle (pinna) and the external auditory canal (EAC), ending at the lateral surface of the tympanic membrane. Disease is best classified as infective versus non-infective, and by extent. Infective causes split into bacterial (acute localized furunculosis, acute diffuse otitis externa, and invasive ), fungal (otomycosis) and viral (herpes zoster oticus). Non-infective disease includes , trauma, bony lesions (exostoses, osteoma), keratin disorders (keratosis obturans, EAC cholesteatoma) and . Framing your answer as "infective vs non-infective, and don't miss necrotizing OE or carcinoma" is exactly what an examiner wants to hear first.

Why it leads the list: "malignant" here means aggressive, not cancerous — it is a skull-base osteomyelitis. It must sit at the front of your differential in any diabetic/immunocompromised patient with an "ordinary" ear infection, because missing it is fatal.
Dermatoses matter: eczema, psoriasis and contact allergy (classically to neomycin in ear drops) break the skin barrier and itch, predisposing to secondary bacterial/fungal infection. Treating the underlying skin disease (topical steroid, stop the allergen) is often the cure.
Never forget carcinoma: squamous cell carcinoma of the EAC presents with chronic pain, discharge, bleeding and granulation that mimics necrotizing OE exactly. Any non-healing granulation must be biopsied.

🧬 Surgical anatomy of the auricle & EAC

The auricle is elastic cartilage covered by tightly adherent skin with almost no subcutaneous layer — except the lobule, which has (the basis of "perichondritis spares the lobule"). The EAC is about 2.5 cm long and sigmoid; in adults you pull the pinna up and back to straighten it (down and back in infants). The and the , with the isthmus the narrowest point at the junction. Spread of infection or tumour to the parotid and skull base occurs through natural clefts — the and the foramen of Huschke. Sensation is shared by several nerves, including the clinically important , and lymphatic drainage is to .

Lobule = no cartilage: because perichondritis and relapsing polychondritis are diseases of cartilage, they spare the fleshy lobule. Erysipelas (a skin/Streptococcal infection) does the opposite and involves the lobule — a one-line bedside discriminator.
Cartilaginous third: the only part with adnexa — hair follicles, sebaceous and ceruminous (modified apocrine) glands. Therefore furuncles arise only here, and this is where cerumen is produced.
Bony two-thirds: skin is only ~0.2 mm thick, adherent to periosteum, with no glands or hair. This makes instrumentation here exquisitely painful and explains why infection readily reaches bone in necrotizing OE.
Fissures of Santorini: vertical clefts in the anterior cartilaginous canal. They are the anatomical highway by which necrotizing OE and EAC carcinoma escape the canal into the parotid, infratemporal fossa and skull base.
Arnold's nerve (vagus, X): supplies part of the canal/concha. It explains the cough or syncope reflex when the canal is instrumented and referred otalgia from the larynx, hypopharynx and oesophagus — so a normal ear with otalgia means examine the throat.
Lymphatics: the route by which EAC carcinoma metastasises and by which severe otitis externa produces tender pre-/post-auricular and upper cervical nodes. Post-auricular nodes also mimic the swelling of mastoiditis.

🧪 Physiology — cerumen & the self-cleaning mechanism

Cerumen is a mix of ceruminous and sebaceous secretions plus desquamated keratin. It is , hydrophobic (waterproofing) and traps debris. The canal also : skin migrates outward from the drum, carrying wax and debris to the meatus. , which is why they are a leading cause of otitis externa.

Acid mantle: cerumen pH is roughly 4–5 / skin 5.5, and it contains lysozyme and free fatty acids that inhibit bacteria and fungi. Anything that raises canal pH (water, soap, repeated drops) removes this protection — the reason acidifying (acetic-acid) drops help treat and prevent OE.
Migrating epithelium: canal skin is unique in the body — its surface keratin migrates laterally from the umbo at ~0.05 mm/day, an in-built conveyor that exports wax. This is also why keratosis obturans (failed migration) and EAC cholesteatoma occur when the mechanism breaks down.
Trap — cotton buds: they push wax medially, abrade the thin canal skin, strip the protective acid layer and reverse migration. "Stop using cotton buds" is a genuine therapeutic instruction, not a throwaway line.

⚙️ Pathophysiology & predisposing factors of otitis externa

Otitis externa develops when the protective barrier — an intact, acidic cerumen layer over intact skin — is breached, allowing organisms to invade. The classic triad of predisposers is . Add : dermatoses, narrow or hairy or exostotic canals (poor drainage), diabetes and immunosuppression, and the absence of cerumen.

The big three: moisture (swimming, humidity, sweat) macerates skin and washes out wax; trauma (cotton buds, hearing aids, earplugs, scratching in eczema) breaches the skin; and raised pH (water, soap) disables the acid mantle. Together they convert a healthy canal into a warm, moist, alkaline culture medium — ideal for Pseudomonas.
Host factors: eczema/psoriasis keep the skin inflamed and itchy; narrow/exostotic canals trap water and debris; diabetes and immunosuppression both predispose to OE and to its lethal necrotizing form. Recurrent OE should prompt a check for diabetes and an underlying dermatosis.

💧 Acute diffuse otitis externa ("swimmer's ear")

This is diffuse inflammation of the canal skin, the commonest form. The dominant organism is , followed by Staphylococcus aureus. It evolves through a . The cardinal, almost pathognomonic sign is , with a tender, oedematous canal full of debris, and conductive hearing loss once the lumen is occluded. The drum, if seen, is normal.

Pseudomonas aeruginosa: a Gram-negative, oxidase-positive, non-fermenting aerobic rod that thrives in moist, alkaline canals; it produces blue-green pus and a sweet odour and is intrinsically antibiotic-resistant. This is why first-line topical therapy is an anti-pseudomonal quinolone and why it also drives the necrotizing form.
Severity ladder: mild = itch, minimal pain, slight canal narrowing; moderate = more pain, partially occluded canal with debris; severe = intense pain, fully occluded lumen, periauricular oedema, lymphadenopathy and fever. Grading guides whether a wick and systemic antibiotics are needed.
The discriminating sign: moving the cartilaginous canal drags inflamed skin over periosteum, reproducing pain. This separates otitis externa from acute otitis media, in which the tragus is non-tender and the drum bulges. Itch dominates early disease; severe pain means the periosteum is stretched.

🔘 Acute localized otitis externa (furunculosis)

A staphylococcal infection of a hair follicle forming a furuncle/abscess. Because hairs exist only in the cartilaginous third, the lesion is , with severe localized pain and point tenderness. Treat with analgesia, local heat and anti-staphylococcal antibiotics; incise only if it points. Recurrent furunculosis should prompt a search for diabetes.

Localized vs diffuse: the furuncle is a single tender swelling in the hair-bearing outer third with a normal deep canal and drum, whereas diffuse OE involves the whole canal circumferentially. Knowing the gland/hair anatomy lets you predict the site.

🌫️ Chronic & eczematous otitis externa

Chronic otitis externa is recurrent or persists beyond three months: the canal skin becomes thickened and scaly, itch exceeds pain, the lumen narrows and there is scant discharge. It usually reflects an underlying dermatosis or repeated trauma, and the feared end-result is . Management centres on treating the skin disease (topical steroids), removing irritants and allergens, regular microsuction and acidifying drops.

Canal stenosis: chronic inflammation lays down fibrous tissue that narrows the canal, trapping debris and perpetuating infection and conductive loss. Severe cases need a canalplasty. This is why chronic OE is actively managed rather than tolerated.

🍄 Otomycosis (fungal otitis externa)

Fungal canal infection, most often or Candida, classically . Intense itch, blockage and discharge predominate; pain is less than bacterial OE unless the drum is involved (myringitis). Treatment is built on meticulous microsuction plus topical antifungals (clotrimazole), acidifying agents, and keeping the ear dry.

Recognising the fungus: Aspergillus niger shows black/grey spore heads on white filamentous hyphae — the "wet newspaper" or "blotting paper" appearance; Candida gives a creamy white discharge with no visible spores. Identifying the morphology on otoscopy clinches the diagnosis.
Iatrogenic setup: prolonged antibiotic/steroid drops suppress bacteria and local immunity, letting fungi flourish — so otomycosis often appears in an ear "treated" for bacterial OE that is not settling. Stopping the antibiotic drops is part of the cure.

🔴 Necrotizing (malignant) otitis externa

This is invasive osteomyelitis of the temporal bone and skull base, almost always due to . The infection breaches the osseocartilaginous junction and spreads along the skull base. The hallmark is , progressing to . Diagnosis rests on a markedly , with biopsy of the granulation to exclude carcinoma. Treatment is .

Host & organism: over 90% are diabetics (microangiopathy, neutrophil dysfunction, alkaline cerumen) or immunosuppressed; the organism is almost always Pseudomonas aeruginosa (Aspergillus in the neutropenic). The demographic is itself the first clue.
Cardinal features: pain that is persistent, nocturnal and disproportionate to modest otoscopic findings, failing topical therapy, with granulation tissue on the canal floor at the bony–cartilaginous junction — the single most useful sign. Always biopsy it to exclude SCC.
Cranial nerves track the spread: the facial nerve (VII) at the stylomastoid foramen goes first (facial palsy = poor prognosis); the jugular foramen takes out IX, X, XI; the hypoglossal canal adds XII. New cranial neuropathy demands urgent imaging and admission.
Investigations: ESR markedly raised (used to monitor response). CT shows bony erosion (needs ~30–50% demineralisation to appear). Tc-99m bone scan is sensitive for early osteomyelitis but stays positive after cure; Ga-67 or labelled-WBC scan follows active inflammation and normalises with healing, so it decides when to stop antibiotics. MRI best shows soft-tissue, marrow and intracranial spread.
Treatment: admit; 6–8+ weeks of systemic anti-pseudomonal antibiotics (ciprofloxacin ± ceftazidime or piperacillin-tazobactam, culture-guided); aural toilet and topical therapy; strict glycaemic control; surgical debridement of sequestra/abscess; hyperbaric oxygen in refractory disease. Mortality is high once intracranial structures are involved.

🌶️ Herpes zoster oticus (Ramsay Hunt syndrome)

Reactivation of varicella-zoster virus in the geniculate ganglion produces the triad of , sometimes with sensorineural loss, tinnitus or vertigo from VIII involvement. It carries a worse facial-recovery prognosis than Bell's palsy; treat early with aciclovir and corticosteroids, and protect the eye.

Why the triad: the geniculate ganglion lies on the facial nerve, which carries sensation to the concha (and taste). Reactivation there inflames VII (palsy) and the sensory zone (vesicles + pain), and can spread to the adjacent VIII (hearing/balance). Vesicles in the ear with a facial palsy is Ramsay Hunt until proven otherwise.

🧱 Perichondritis, chondritis & auricular cellulitis

Perichondritis is Pseudomonas infection of the auricular cartilage's perichondrium, typically after trauma, a , burns or surgery. The pinna is red, hot and exquisitely tender but ; untreated it causes cartilage necrosis and a cauliflower ear. Treat with systemic anti-pseudomonal antibiotics, drain any abscess and remove the foreign body. Distinguish erysipelas (Streptococcus, sharply demarcated, involves the lobule) and relapsing polychondritis (autoimmune, recurrent, bilateral, also nose/airway/joints).

High piercings: "cartilage" piercings through the helix introduce Pseudomonas directly into avascular cartilage. They are the typical modern cause of perichondritis in young patients and should be removed when infection occurs.
Lobule rule again: sparing of the cartilage-free lobule confirms a cartilage disease (perichondritis/polychondritis) rather than a spreading skin infection (erysipelas/cellulitis), which involves the lobule.

🦴 Non-infective canal disease

Several non-infective lesions narrow or occlude the canal. are multiple, broad-based, bilateral bony swellings from cold-water exposure; an osteoma is single, pedunculated and unilateral. is a keratin plug widening the canal in younger patients (linked to bronchiectasis/sinusitis), while EAC cholesteatoma erodes bone focally in older patients. Any chronic, painful, bleeding or granulating canal that does not heal must raise .

Exostoses vs osteoma: exostoses are multiple, sessile, bilateral and arise from repeated cold-water exposure, trapping water/wax and causing recurrent OE and conductive loss; an osteoma is a single pedunculated unilateral benign tumour at the tympanosquamous suture. Symptomatic exostoses need canalplasty.
Keratosis obturans vs EAC cholesteatoma: keratosis obturans = bilateral keratin plug + circumferential canal widening + severe pain + conductive loss in young patients (abnormal epithelial migration); EAC cholesteatoma = unilateral focal bony erosion with squamous tissue and dull pain in older patients.
Carcinoma red flag: EAC squamous cell carcinoma mimics chronic OE and necrotizing OE — pain, discharge, bleeding, granulation, sometimes facial palsy. Biopsy any non-healing granulation and image; treatment is en-bloc temporal-bone resection ± radiotherapy (see Topic 51).

🔵 Foreign bodies of the external canal

Foreign bodies are common in , and may be inanimate or a live insect. Most are harmless, but two are emergencies: a and a . Removal technique is dictated by the object, and irrigation is . A single, careful, well-visualised attempt is the rule; failed or uncooperative cases go to ENT, sometimes under general anaesthetic.

Who and how they present: beads, seeds, paper and toy parts in children; cotton-bud tips and broken matchsticks in adults. Many are found incidentally; others cause pain, discharge, bleeding, hearing loss or, with an insect, distressing noise and movement.
Button battery = emergency: it generates a local current and leaks alkali, causing liquefactive necrosis of the canal and drum within hours, with perforation and stenosis. Remove immediately under direct vision and never irrigate (moisture completes the circuit and worsens injury).
Live insect: movement against the drum is intensely distressing. First kill/immobilise it with mineral oil, 2% lidocaine or alcohol, then remove it by suction, forceps or irrigation. Killing it first prevents stinging and further trauma.
Irrigation contraindications (classic trap): (1) button battery; (2) organic/vegetable matter (peas, beans — they swell and lodge tighter); (3) known/suspected perforation or grommet; (4) sharp objects. Smooth round objects are removed with a right-angle hook or suction passed beyond them, never forceps (which push them medially). Use the microscope and re-examine the drum afterwards.

🔎 Examination & investigation

Examine the pinna and periauricular area for swelling, vesicles, lobule involvement and lymph nodes; test tragal pressure/pinna traction; then perform otoscopy/otomicroscopy (canal, debris, drum) and pneumatic otoscopy. Tuning forks/audiometry confirm a conductive loss; a swab guides therapy in severe or non-resolving cases. In suspected necrotizing OE or tumour, add ESR/CRP, CT, MRI, nuclear scans and biopsy.

💊 Principles of treatment

Aural toilet/microsuction is the cornerstone — it removes debris, lets drops reach the skin and confirms the diagnosis. Topical therapy combines an , with acidifying agents for mild/fungal disease and antifungals for otomycosis. A wick delivers drops when the canal is occluded. for spreading cellulitis, diabetes/immunosuppression or necrotizing OE. Add analgesia, water precautions, stopping cotton buds and treating any dermatosis.

Choosing drops: a topical quinolone (ciprofloxacin/ofloxacin) ± steroid is first-line and is safe with a perforation. Aminoglycoside-steroid drops are ototoxic and must be avoided if the drum is perforated (or used only briefly with an intact drum). Acetic-acid drops restore the protective acid pH.
When to go systemic: most OE needs no oral antibiotics. Escalate to systemic therapy for spreading peri-auricular cellulitis, immunocompromise/diabetes, perichondritis, or necrotizing OE — where systemic anti-pseudomonal therapy is mandatory and prolonged.

📈 Prognosis & prevention

Most otitis externa resolves within days to weeks with aural toilet and drops. Prevent recurrence by keeping the ears dry (ear plugs, cool hair-dryer), avoiding cotton buds, using acidifying drops after swimming, treating eczema and controlling diabetes. Necrotizing OE needs prolonged therapy with imaging follow-up, and any non-healing ear must be biopsied to exclude carcinoma.

🧠 Thinking questions (tap to reveal)

OE: pain on tragal pressure/pinna traction, tender swollen canal full of debris, drum normal or not visible, itch prominent. AOM: tragus non-tender, bulging red immobile drum, preceding URTI, systemic fever in a child. If the drum is normal and the canal hurts to touch → externa.
Treat as necrotizing otitis externa until excluded: admit, examine cranial nerves, take a granulation-tissue biopsy (exclude SCC), send ESR/CRP, arrange CT temporal bone + Tc-99m bone scan, and start systemic anti-pseudomonal therapy. Optimise glycaemic control.
Gentamicin/neomycin are cochleo- and vestibulotoxic. With an intact drum they cannot reach the inner ear, but through a perforation they cross the round window → permanent SNHL/imbalance. Use topical quinolones, which are safe with perforation.
Organic/vegetable matter absorbs water and swells, wedging tighter and worsening pain. Remove mechanically under microscope (hook/suction); if uncooperative, do it under general anaesthetic rather than risk canal or drum trauma.
Granulation tissue at the bony–cartilaginous junction (canal floor). On that same tissue you must biopsy to exclude squamous cell carcinoma of the canal, which presents identically.
Exostoses: multiple, broad-based, bilateral bone — cold-water swimmers. Osteoma: single, pedunculated, unilateral. Keratosis obturans: bilateral keratin plug + canal widening + pain in young patients (assoc. bronchiectasis), vs EAC cholesteatoma which erodes bone focally in older patients.
Microangiopathy reduces perfusion and antibiotic delivery; impaired neutrophil function blunts the response to Pseudomonas; and diabetic cerumen has a higher pH, weakening its bacteriostatic effect. Together these let canal infection invade bone.

🎯 Demonstrate it like an examiner wants

First commit to an answer, then reveal the model structure and build the reasoning chain.

Prompt: "A 68-year-old diabetic has 3 weeks of severe right otalgia and otorrhoea, unresponsive to ciprofloxacin drops. How do you proceed?"

Your locked answer:
Open by naming the danger: "In an elderly diabetic, otorrhoea and severe pain failing topical therapy is necrotizing otitis externa — skull-base osteomyelitis, usually Pseudomonas — until proven otherwise."
Define otitis externa, then the necrotizing variant: invasive osteomyelitis of the temporal bone & skull base in diabetics/immunocompromised, organism P. aeruginosa. State the spread route via the osseocartilaginous junction and fissures of Santorini.
Deep nocturnal pain out of proportion, chronic discharge, poor glycaemic control. Exam: granulation at the bony–cartilaginous junction, then a full cranial-nerve survey. Always biopsy the granulation to exclude SCC.
ESR/CRP raised; microbiology + biopsy; CT for bony erosion, MRI for soft-tissue/intracranial extent; Tc-99m to diagnose, Ga-67/labelled-WBC to confirm resolution.
Admit. Prolonged (6–8 week) systemic anti-pseudomonal therapy, aural toilet, glycaemic control, debridement for sequestra. Bottom line: early recognition + systemic antibiotics prevents cranial-nerve palsy and death.
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: Diabetic microangiopathy, neutrophil dysfunction and altered cerumen pH let Pseudomonas invade canal skin and breach the osseocartilaginous junction. It spreads along the fissures of Santorini into the temporal bone, producing skull-base osteomyelitis. Progression to the stylomastoid and jugular foramina causes sequential VII then IX–XII palsies, the hallmark of advanced disease.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model "out-loud" presentations and the advanced follow-ups examiners push on. Tap to reveal.
"A young swimmer has 3 days of itchy, painful, blocked right ear with scanty discharge. Tragal pressure is painful, the canal is oedematous and full of debris, the drum is intact. This is acute diffuse otitis externa, likely Pseudomonas. I would do aural toilet under the microscope, prescribe topical ciprofloxacin–dexamethasone, insert a Pope wick if the canal is occluded, give analgesia and water-precautions, and review in 48–72 h."
"A 70-year-old poorly controlled diabetic has weeks of deep nocturnal otalgia and discharge unresponsive to drops, with granulation at the canal floor and a developing facial droop." → Necrotizing otitis externa. Admit; cranial-nerve exam; biopsy granulation (exclude SCC); ESR/CRP, cultures, CT + Tc-99m / Ga-67; systemic anti-pseudomonal antibiotics 6–8 weeks; tight glycaemic control; debride sequestra.
Pseudomonas aeruginosa — Gram-negative, oxidase-positive, non-fermenting rod; aerobic; favours moist alkaline environments; produces pyocyanin (blue-green) and a sweet odour; intrinsically resistant to many antibiotics → anti-pseudomonal quinolones/ceftazidime. Staph aureus causes localized furuncles.
Tc-99m (MDP) bone scan detects osteoblastic activity → very sensitive for diagnosis, but stays positive long after cure, so it cannot judge resolution. Ga-67 citrate (or labelled-WBC) follows active inflammation and normalises with healing, so it guides when to stop antibiotics. CT shows bone erosion; MRI shows marrow/soft-tissue and intracranial spread.
Steps: microscope + good light; choose the tool by object (hook/suction for smooth beads, forceps for graspable, kill insects first); single careful attempt; refer/GA if uncooperative, deep, or failed. Do NOT irrigate with: button battery, organic matter, suspected perforation/grommet, or sharp objects. Re-examine the drum afterwards.
Referred otalgia via shared nerves: V (teeth, TMJ, sinuses), VII/geniculate, IX (tonsil, oropharynx, post-tonsillectomy), X via Arnold's nerve (larynx, hypopharynx, GORD), C2–3 (cervical spine). Red flag: unilateral referred otalgia + smoking/alcohol → exclude a hypopharyngeal/laryngeal carcinoma with endoscopy.
Auricular haematoma: blood between perichondrium and cartilage strips the cartilage of its blood supply → necrosis and cauliflower ear. Incise/aspirate and apply a pressure bolster urgently, with antibiotics. Untreated perichondritis after trauma/piercing similarly destroys cartilage.
