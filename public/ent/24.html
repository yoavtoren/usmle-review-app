💧 Non-tumorous diseases of the salivary glands

The Overview is a full walk-through of the topic. Tap any coloured word for an extra exam pearl. Then work through all 5 tabs.

📖 Full topic explanation

Non-tumorous salivary disease covers obstruction (stones), infection (bacterial/viral), autoimmune and granulomatous conditions, and sialadenosis. The structuring questions are which gland, acute or chronic, and obstructive vs inflammatory vs systemic? — always keeping a neoplasm in the differential of a persistent lump.

🧬 Applied anatomy

The major glands are the and , plus the sublingual and minor glands — their secretions and duct anatomy explain disease patterns.

Parotid: purely serous secretion; the facial nerve runs through it (dividing it into superficial and deep lobes); Stensen's duct opens opposite the upper second molar. Serous, watery saliva makes stones less common here but bacterial (Staph) sialadenitis more so.
Submandibular: mixed sero-mucinous; Wharton's duct runs a long, uphill, anterior course (related to the lingual nerve). The thick mucinous saliva and uphill stagnation make it the site of ~80% of salivary calculi.

🪨 Sialolithiasis (stones)

Calculi mostly affect the and cause classic , managed from .

Why submandibular: ~80% of stones are submandibular because the saliva is more mucinous/alkaline and Wharton's duct drains uphill against gravity, promoting stasis and stone formation. Most submandibular stones are radio-opaque.
Mealtime syndrome: pain and swelling of the gland on eating (saliva backs up behind the obstruction), settling between meals; a stone may be palpable in the floor of mouth/duct. Obstruction predisposes to secondary bacterial sialadenitis.
Management: hydration, sialogogues (citrus/lemon), gland massage and warmth; sialendoscopy (diagnostic + therapeutic stone retrieval), intra-oral duct stone removal, lithotripsy, or gland excision for recurrent/large/intraglandular stones. Imaging: ultrasound first, then sialography/CT/sialendoscopy.

🦠 Sialadenitis (infection/inflammation)

Distinguish from and from .

Acute bacterial (suppurative) sialadenitis: typically the parotid of a dehydrated/elderly/post-operative patient — a hot, tender, swollen gland with pus expressible from the duct; usually Staphylococcus aureus. Treat with rehydration, antibiotics, sialogogues and massage; drain an abscess.
Mumps (viral parotitis): paramyxovirus causing bilateral painful parotid swelling in (usually) unvaccinated children, with possible orchitis, meningitis and pancreatitis. Diagnosis is clinical/serological; management is supportive; it is vaccine-preventable (MMR) and notifiable.
Chronic/recurrent sialadenitis: recurrent obstruction/infection causing ductal damage (sialectasis) and repeated swelling; juvenile recurrent parotitis in children. Managed with conservative measures, sialendoscopy, and occasionally gland excision.

🧫 Autoimmune & granulomatous disease

Important systemic causes are and .

Sjögren's syndrome: autoimmune exocrine destruction → dry eyes and dry mouth (sicca) with parotid enlargement; anti-Ro/La antibodies, positive labial gland biopsy. Primary or secondary (with rheumatoid/connective-tissue disease). Carries an important increased risk of MALT lymphoma — investigate a dominant or enlarging gland.
Granulomatous/IgG4: sarcoidosis (parotid enlargement; Heerfordt syndrome = uveoparotid fever + facial palsy), tuberculosis, actinomycosis; and IgG4-related disease (Küttner tumour of the submandibular gland, Mikulicz disease). Diagnose with bloods, imaging and biopsy.

💧 Sialadenosis, sialorrhoea & mucoceles

Non-inflammatory swelling () and mucus-related lesions () complete the picture, plus .

Sialadenosis (sialosis): bilateral, painless, non-inflammatory parotid enlargement from acinar hypertrophy — associated with diabetes, alcoholism, bulimia/malnutrition, and some drugs. Treat the underlying cause; biopsy if atypical.
Mucocele/ranula: a mucocele is a mucus-extravasation cyst of a minor gland (commonly the lower lip); a ranula is a sublingual-gland mucocele in the floor of mouth (a "plunging" ranula extends into the neck). Treated by excision/marsupialisation ± gland removal.
Sialorrhoea (drooling): excess/poorly-controlled saliva (e.g. cerebral palsy, Parkinson's, post-stroke); managed with positioning/behavioural therapy, anticholinergics, botulinum toxin to the glands, or surgery (duct relocation/ligation).

🔎 Investigation

Work-up uses , with a low threshold to exclude neoplasia.

Investigations: ultrasound first (stones, gland architecture, guides FNAC); sialography/sialendoscopy for ductal disease; FNAC/core biopsy for any persistent lump (exclude tumour/lymphoma); MRI/CT for deep/extensive disease; bloods (amylase, autoimmune serology, IgG4, ACE). Remember Frey syndrome (gustatory sweating) as a post-parotidectomy phenomenon.

📈 Prognosis

Most obstructive and infective disease resolves with conservative/minimally-invasive treatment; autoimmune and granulomatous disease needs systemic management and surveillance (lymphoma in Sjögren's). The key safety point is to biopsy a persistent or dominant gland lump to exclude malignancy.

🎯 Demonstrate it like an examiner wants

Commit first, then reveal the model structure and build the chain step by step.

Prompt: "A 40-year-old has recurrent painful swelling of the left submandibular gland that worsens at mealtimes. How do you assess and manage?"

Your locked answer:
"Mealtime swelling of the submandibular gland is obstructive sialadenitis, usually a calculus (the gland of ~80% of stones)."
Bimanual palpation of the floor of mouth/Wharton's duct for a palpable stone and pus; check for secondary infection and exclude a gland mass/tumour.
Ultrasound first (most stones are radio-opaque; plain film or CT can confirm); sialendoscopy is diagnostic and therapeutic; FNAC if a mass is suspected.
Conservative (hydration, sialogogues, massage, treat infection) → sialendoscopic/intra-oral stone removal or lithotripsy → gland excision for recurrent, large or intraglandular stones.
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: Mucinous, alkaline submandibular saliva and the uphill course of Wharton's duct promote stasis and calculus formation. A stone obstructs salivary outflow, so the gland swells painfully when stimulated by eating and settles as saliva slowly clears. Persistent obstruction predisposes to ascending bacterial sialadenitis and, over time, irreversible ductal/glandular damage.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"Recurrent mealtime swelling with a palpable stone in the floor of mouth." → sialolithiasis: ultrasound, conservative measures, then sialendoscopic/intra-oral removal; gland excision if recurrent/intraglandular.
"A persistent, painless, dominant parotid lump with no mealtime pattern." → don't assume inflammation: ultrasound + FNAC to exclude a neoplasm (and lymphoma in Sjögren's); image and refer as needed.
Chronic autoimmune lymphoproliferation predisposes to parotid MALT lymphoma. A persistently enlarging or dominant gland mass, new lymphadenopathy, or systemic symptoms should prompt imaging and biopsy.
A minimally-invasive endoscopy of the ductal system that is both diagnostic (stones, strictures) and therapeutic (stone retrieval, duct dilatation), enabling gland-preserving treatment of obstructive disease and recurrent sialadenitis.
Stepwise: positioning/behavioural and speech therapy, anticholinergics (e.g. glycopyrronium), botulinum toxin into the salivary glands, and surgery (duct relocation/ligation or gland excision) for refractory cases — common in neurological disability.
Sarcoidosis (incl. Heerfordt syndrome), tuberculosis, actinomycosis, and cat-scratch disease; plus IgG4-related disease (Küttner tumour/Mikulicz). Diagnose with bloods (ACE, IgG4), imaging and biopsy.
