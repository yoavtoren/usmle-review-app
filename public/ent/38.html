🎯 Tumors of the epipharynx (nasopharyngeal carcinoma)

📖 Full topic explanation

Nasopharyngeal carcinoma (NPC) is the dominant epipharyngeal tumour — an EBV-associated cancer arising at the fossa of Rosenmüller. It is unusual among head-and-neck cancers in being treated primarily by radiotherapy, and it classically presents with a neck node, a unilateral middle-ear effusion, nasal symptoms or cranial-nerve palsies.

🗂️ Histology & epidemiology

NPC has distinct and a striking .

WHO types: I keratinising SCC (more linked to smoking, less EBV, poorer radioresponse), II non-keratinising, and III undifferentiated (the commonest in endemic areas, strongly EBV-associated and radiosensitive).
Epidemiology/risk: markedly higher in Southern Chinese/Southeast Asian populations; risk factors are EBV infection, genetic susceptibility, and dietary nitrosamines (salted fish), with smoking contributing to keratinising types.

👤 Presentation

The classic presentations are a , a , , and .

Neck node (often the first sign): the nasopharynx has rich, bilateral lymphatics, so NPC frequently presents with an upper deep cervical or posterior-triangle (level V) node, sometimes bilateral. A neck node with no obvious primary should prompt nasopharyngeal examination.
Unilateral serous otitis media (adult red flag): the tumour at the fossa of Rosenmüller obstructs the Eustachian-tube orifice, causing a persistent unilateral middle-ear effusion in an adult — always examine the nasopharynx before placing a grommet.
Nasal symptoms: nasal obstruction, epistaxis or blood-stained postnasal discharge from the primary tumour.
Cranial-nerve palsies: skull-base extension (through the foramen lacerum) causes neuropathies — classically CN VI (diplopia), then III–V; jugular-foramen spread affects IX–XII. New cranial-nerve palsies with these other features strongly suggest NPC.

🔎 Diagnosis

Diagnosis uses , , and .

Endoscopy + biopsy: flexible nasendoscopy of the fossa of Rosenmüller and biopsy of any lesion is the key step in any adult with a unilateral effusion, neck node or nasal/cranial-nerve symptoms.
Imaging & EBV: MRI best shows skull-base/intracranial and soft-tissue extent; EBV serology and plasma EBV-DNA support diagnosis and monitor treatment response/recurrence.
Staging: FNAC of a neck node, PET-CT for distant disease (NPC has a higher rate of distant metastasis), and TNM staging. Examine for the other primary sites if presenting as an unknown primary.

💊 Treatment

Unlike most head-and-neck cancers, NPC is treated , with .

Radiotherapy-based: NPC is radiosensitive and surgically inaccessible, so radiotherapy (IMRT) is the mainstay, with concurrent chemoradiotherapy ± induction/adjuvant chemotherapy for advanced disease. The neck is included in the radiation field.
Surgery: reserved for residual/recurrent disease (salvage nasopharyngectomy or neck dissection) rather than primary treatment — a key contrast with other H&N SCC where surgery is often primary.

📈 Prognosis

Prognosis depends on stage and histology (EBV-associated undifferentiated type is radiosensitive); local control is good with IMRT, but distant metastasis is the main cause of failure. Plasma EBV-DNA is used for surveillance.

🧠 Thinking questions (tap to reveal)

A neck node (often the first sign, may be bilateral), a unilateral middle-ear effusion in an adult (ET obstruction), nasal obstruction/epistaxis, and cranial-nerve palsies (esp. VI) from skull-base spread.
A nasopharyngeal tumour at the fossa of Rosenmüller obstructs the Eustachian-tube orifice → a persistent one-sided effusion. Examine the nasopharynx (nasendoscopy) and biopsy any lesion before treating the ear.
EBV is strongly associated with undifferentiated (WHO III) NPC; EBV serology and plasma EBV-DNA aid diagnosis and are used to monitor treatment response and detect recurrence.
It is radiosensitive (esp. undifferentiated/EBV type) and anatomically inaccessible at the skull base, with frequent bilateral nodal spread. Radiotherapy ± chemotherapy covers the primary and necks; surgery is for salvage of residual/recurrent disease.
CN VI (abducens) is classically affected as the tumour erodes the skull base via the foramen lacerum toward the cavernous sinus; further spread involves III–V, and jugular-foramen extension affects IX–XII.
Examine/biopsy the nasopharynx (and tonsils/tongue base), test EBV (and HPV/p16), and image with MRI + PET-CT. EBV-positive undifferentiated carcinoma in a node points strongly to an occult nasopharyngeal primary.

🎯 Demonstrate it like an examiner wants

Commit first, then reveal the model structure and build the chain step by step.

Prompt: "A 45-year-old man has a unilateral middle-ear effusion, an upper-neck lump and occasional blood-stained postnasal discharge. How do you proceed?"

Your locked answer:
"Unilateral adult effusion + neck node + blood-stained postnasal discharge is nasopharyngeal carcinoma until excluded — examine and biopsy the nasopharynx."
EBV-associated carcinoma at the fossa of Rosenmüller; note ethnicity/diet; screen cranial nerves (esp. VI).
Nasendoscopy + biopsy, MRI (skull base), EBV serology/DNA, node FNAC and PET-CT; assign TNM.
Radiotherapy (IMRT) ± chemotherapy as primary treatment (covering the necks); surgery for salvage; monitor with EBV-DNA and imaging.
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: EBV-driven transformation of nasopharyngeal epithelium at the fossa of Rosenmüller produces an undifferentiated carcinoma that obstructs the adjacent Eustachian-tube orifice (causing the unilateral effusion) and the choana (nasal symptoms). Its rich bilateral lymphatics seed cervical nodes early, often as the presenting sign. Skull-base erosion through the foramen lacerum reaches the cavernous sinus, producing cranial-nerve palsies — and its radiosensitivity makes radiotherapy the primary treatment.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"A middle-aged man with unilateral glue ear, a neck node and epistaxis." → nasendoscopy + biopsy of the fossa of Rosenmüller, MRI, EBV-DNA, node FNAC, PET-CT; treat with radiotherapy ± chemo; never just grommet the ear.
"NPC with diplopia and headache." → CN VI palsy from skull-base/cavernous-sinus extension: MRI for intracranial extent, stage, and treat with IMRT/chemoradiotherapy; manage symptoms.
As a biomarker for diagnosis support, prognosis, treatment-response monitoring and detection of recurrence in EBV-associated NPC — falling levels indicate response, and a rise can herald relapse before imaging.
Its rich lymphovascular drainage and aggressive undifferentiated biology lead to early nodal and haematogenous spread (bone, lung, liver). This is why systemic staging (PET-CT) and chemotherapy feature prominently and why distant failure dominates outcomes.
Xerostomia, trismus, hearing loss (SNHL/middle-ear), hypothyroidism/hypopituitarism, cranial neuropathies, temporal-lobe necrosis, and carotid stenosis. IMRT reduces some toxicity; survivors need long-term surveillance.
For residual or recurrent disease after radiotherapy — salvage nasopharyngectomy (increasingly endoscopic) or neck dissection for nodal recurrence — not as primary treatment, given the radiosensitivity and surgical inaccessibility of the nasopharynx.
