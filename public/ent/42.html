👃🎗️ Nose & paranasal sinus tumors

📖 Full topic explanation

Sinonasal tumours are rare and present late because early symptoms mimic benign disease. The governing rule is that unilateral nasal obstruction, bleeding or a mass demands biopsy and imaging, and the differential spans benign (inverted papilloma) and several distinct malignancies with characteristic associations.

🗂️ Benign tumours

The key benign tumour is the ; others include osteoma and the .

Inverted (Schneiderian) papilloma: a unilateral benign lesion (lateral nasal wall), HPV-associated, that recurs and can harbour or transform to SCC (~10%). Treat with complete (often endoscopic medial-maxillectomy-type) excision and surveillance — not simple polypectomy.
Juvenile nasopharyngeal angiofibroma: a highly vascular benign tumour of adolescent males — do NOT biopsy (catastrophic bleeding); image instead (Topic 54). Osteomas are benign bony tumours (frontal/ethmoid), often incidental.

🦠 Malignant tumours

Malignancies include , , , and .

Squamous cell carcinoma: the commonest sinonasal malignancy, most often in the maxillary sinus; related to smoking and (some) occupational exposures. Presents late with unilateral nasal/facial/dental/orbital symptoms.
Adenocarcinoma & adenoid cystic carcinoma: ethmoid adenocarcinoma is linked to occupational wood/leather dust (an occupational cancer); adenoid cystic carcinoma shows perineural spread, a long natural history and late lung metastases.
Esthesioneuroblastoma (olfactory neuroblastoma): arises from olfactory epithelium high in the nasal vault, classically a "dumbbell" mass across the cribriform plate (Kadish staging; Hyams grade). Causes anosmia, obstruction and epistaxis; treated by craniofacial/endoscopic resection + radiotherapy.
Others: mucosal melanoma (pigmented, poor prognosis), sinonasal undifferentiated carcinoma (SNUC) (aggressive), and NK/T-cell lymphoma (midline destructive lesion). Histology is essential as treatment differs widely.

👤 Presentation

Sinonasal tumours present with and, when advanced, .

Early/local: unilateral nasal obstruction, epistaxis/blood-stained discharge, facial pain/swelling, and cheek/dental symptoms (loose teeth, ill-fitting denture). These mimic benign sinusitis, causing delay — hence unilateral persistence is a red flag.
Advanced: orbital invasion (proptosis, diplopia, epiphora, reduced vision), infraorbital/cheek numbness (V2), palatal/alveolar masses, and skull-base/intracranial extension. Cervical nodal spread is relatively uncommon early.

🔎 Diagnosis & staging

Assess with and , mindful of the and the .

Endoscopy + biopsy: nasendoscopy and biopsy of any unilateral mass establishes the histology (which dictates everything). Examine the orbit, cranial nerves, oral cavity and neck.
Imaging: CT (bony erosion of the orbit/skull base, sinus walls) and MRI (soft-tissue, orbital/dural/intracranial and perineural spread) are complementary; PET-CT for staging. They define resectability.
Trap: do not biopsy a suspected vascular lesion (angiofibroma) or a midline mass with possible intracranial connection without imaging first — risk of haemorrhage or CSF leak/meningitis.
Ohngren's line: a plane from the medial canthus to the mandibular angle dividing the maxillary sinus into suprastructure (posterosuperior — worse prognosis, near orbit/skull base) and infrastructure (anteroinferior — better). A useful prognostic concept for maxillary tumours.

💊 Treatment

Treatment is .

Treatment: surgical resection — endoscopic (limited disease) or open (e.g. craniofacial resection for skull-base/anterior cranial fossa involvement) — usually with postoperative radiotherapy; chemotherapy/chemoRT for SNUC/advanced disease; lymphoma is treated with chemoRT (not surgery). Multidisciplinary skull-base care (Topic 53); reconstruction with vascularised flaps.

📈 Prognosis

Outcomes are limited by late presentation and proximity to the orbit and skull base; SCC and SNUC do worse, adenoid cystic carcinoma has a long but relapsing course, and esthesioneuroblastoma can do well with craniofacial resection + radiotherapy. Inverted papilloma is benign but recurs and can harbour SCC.

🎯 Demonstrate it like an examiner wants

Commit first, then reveal the model structure and build the chain step by step.

Prompt: "A 60-year-old has unilateral nasal obstruction, recurrent epistaxis and cheek numbness. How do you assess?"

Your locked answer:
"Unilateral obstruction + epistaxis + V2 numbness is a sinonasal malignancy until excluded — the cheek numbness indicates infraorbital-nerve involvement."
SCC (maxillary), adenocarcinoma (wood dust), adenoid cystic (perineural), esthesioneuroblastoma, melanoma, SNUC, lymphoma; histology decides treatment.
Nasendoscopy + biopsy (avoid if vascular/midline-intracranial until imaged), assess orbit/cranial nerves/oral cavity/neck, and obtain CT + MRI (± PET-CT) for extent/resectability.
Skull-base MDT: endoscopic or open (craniofacial) resection + radiotherapy (chemoRT for SNUC/advanced); lymphoma → chemoRT; reconstruct; long follow-up.
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: A sinonasal tumour grows silently within an air-filled cavity, so symptoms (unilateral obstruction, epistaxis) appear only once it is sizeable. As it enlarges it erodes the thin bony walls into the infraorbital canal (V2 numbness), orbit (proptosis/diplopia) and skull base. Its histology determines behaviour and treatment, which is why biopsy and complementary CT/MRI are essential before resection.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"A smoker with unilateral obstruction, epistaxis, cheek numbness and a loose tooth." → suspected maxillary SCC: nasendoscopy + biopsy, CT + MRI (orbit/skull base), assess Ohngren level; MDT — resection + radiotherapy.
"A young adult with anosmia, unilateral obstruction and epistaxis; a high nasal-vault mass crossing the cribriform on MRI." → esthesioneuroblastoma: biopsy, Kadish/Hyams staging; craniofacial/endoscopic resection + radiotherapy.
CT shows bony erosion (orbit/skull-base/sinus walls); MRI distinguishes tumour from retained secretions and shows soft-tissue, dural/intracranial, orbital and perineural spread. Together they define resectability and plan the approach.
The orbit can often be preserved if the periorbita is not breached; exenteration is reserved for frank invasion of orbital fat/muscle/apex. The decision is made with imaging and intra-operative assessment in the skull-base MDT.
The sinonasal mucosa has relatively sparse lymphatics, so cervical nodal metastasis is less common at presentation (except for advanced/SNUC/SCC); spread is mainly local (orbit, skull base, perineural), which dominates management.
For selected localised tumours and reconstruction of skull-base defects, endoscopic resection (± skull-base MDT, nasoseptal flap repair) offers oncological clearance with less morbidity; extensive orbital/dural/skin involvement still needs open craniofacial surgery.
