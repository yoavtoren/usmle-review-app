🗣️🔥 Acute laryngitis

📖 Full topic explanation

Acute laryngitis is acute inflammation of the laryngeal mucosa causing hoarseness. In adults it is usually a benign self-limiting viral illness, but the exam priority is the paediatric airway emergencies that mimic it — epiglottitis, croup and bacterial tracheitis — and the red flag of persistent hoarseness.

🗂️ Definition & causes (adult)

Adult acute laryngitis is , with contributory , presenting with .

Aetiology: most cases follow a viral URTI (rhinovirus, influenza, parainfluenza), occasionally with secondary bacterial infection. It is self-limiting over ~1–2 weeks; antibiotics are rarely indicated.
Contributors: vocal abuse/overuse, laryngopharyngeal reflux, smoking and inhaled irritants, and allergy. Repeated insults predispose to chronic laryngitis (Topic 45).
Presentation: hoarseness/voice loss, dry cough, throat discomfort and a preceding/accompanying cold; laryngoscopy (if done) shows red, oedematous vocal cords. Systemic upset is mild in uncomplicated adult disease.

🔴 Paediatric airway emergencies (the must-know mimics)

In children, acute upper-airway inflammation can be life-threatening: , , and .

Acute epiglottitis (supraglottitis): classically Haemophilus influenzae type b (now rarer post-Hib vaccine; consider in adults too). Rapid onset of high fever, drooling, dysphagia, muffled voice, tripod posture and stridor; the child looks toxic. Do NOT examine the throat with a tongue depressor or distress the child — secure the airway in a controlled setting (theatre, senior ENT + anaesthetics), then antibiotics. A "cherry-red" epiglottis is seen on careful laryngoscopy.
Croup: parainfluenza laryngotracheobronchitis in toddlers — a barking ("seal") cough, hoarse voice, inspiratory stridor, usually preceded by coryza, worse at night. CXR may show subglottic narrowing ("steeple sign"). Treat with oral/IM dexamethasone; nebulised adrenaline for moderate-severe distress.
Bacterial tracheitis: a toxic child with stridor and thick purulent tracheal secretions (often Staph aureus), more unwell than croup and not responding to adrenaline/steroids; needs IV antibiotics and sometimes intubation/airway toilet. Diphtheria (grey membrane) is the historical mimic.

👂 Clinical assessment

Assess for and apply the .

Airway signs: stridor, drooling, respiratory distress, tachypnoea, recession, agitation then exhaustion, cyanosis. Inspiratory stridor localises to the supraglottis/glottis. Any of these → do not distress the patient; get senior airway help.
Red flag — persistent hoarseness: hoarseness lasting more than 3 weeks, especially in a smoker/drinker, must be referred for laryngoscopy to exclude laryngeal carcinoma — acute laryngitis should resolve within ~2 weeks.

💊 Management

Adult acute laryngitis needs only ; the paediatric emergencies need .

Adult: voice rest, hydration, humidification, analgesia, stop smoking and treat reflux; antibiotics only if clear bacterial infection. Reassure that it is self-limiting; review/scope if it persists beyond 3 weeks.
Paediatric emergencies: epiglottitis — keep the child calm, no throat examination, controlled airway in theatre, IV antibiotics (e.g. ceftriaxone); croup — steroids ± nebulised adrenaline, observe; bacterial tracheitis — IV antibiotics ± airway support. Senior ENT/anaesthetic/paediatric input throughout.

📈 Prognosis

Adult acute laryngitis resolves fully with voice rest; croup is usually self-limiting; epiglottitis and bacterial tracheitis are life-threatening but have good outcomes with prompt airway management and antibiotics. The persistent-hoarseness rule prevents missed cancers.

Croup = barking cough, hoarse, gradual, low-grade fever, looks reasonably well, responds to steroids/adrenaline. Epiglottitis = no/little cough, drooling, high fever, toxic, tripod, muffled voice, rapid onset — an airway emergency.
When it lasts >3 weeks (especially with smoking/alcohol, neck lump, dysphagia, otalgia, weight loss) — refer for laryngoscopy to exclude laryngeal carcinoma (Topics 43–45).

🎯 Demonstrate it like an examiner wants

Commit first, then reveal the model structure and build the chain step by step.

Prompt: "A 4-year-old has rapid-onset high fever, drooling, a muffled voice and soft stridor, sitting forward. How do you proceed?"

Your locked answer:
"Rapid fever, drooling, muffled voice and tripod posture is acute epiglottitis — a paediatric airway emergency. The first rule is do NOT distress the child or examine the throat."
Leave the child with a parent in a position of comfort, give no cannulas/examinations that upset them, and summon senior ENT, anaesthetics and paediatrics immediately.
Transfer to theatre for gaseous induction and controlled intubation (with tracheostomy backup); confirm the cherry-red epiglottis only under controlled conditions.
IV antibiotics (e.g. ceftriaxone), fluids, and ICU/HDU care; cultures; prophylaxis/vaccination considerations for Hib contacts. Extubate when the swelling settles.
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: Bacterial (classically Hib) infection inflames the supraglottic structures, and the loose submucosa of the epiglottis swells rapidly into a cherry-red mass. This narrows the supraglottic airway, producing drooling, a muffled "hot-potato" voice and stridor, with a danger of sudden complete obstruction if the child is agitated. Controlled airway control before any examination, plus antibiotics, prevents catastrophic occlusion.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"A teacher has 5 days of hoarseness after a cold, otherwise well." → acute viral laryngitis: voice rest, hydration, humidification, treat reflux/stop smoking; reassure; review/scope if it persists beyond 3 weeks.
"A 2-year-old with a barking cough, stridor at night, low-grade fever, looks well between episodes." → croup: dexamethasone; nebulised adrenaline if stridor at rest/distress; observe and discharge with advice when stable.
Hib vaccination dramatically reduced childhood epiglottitis, so the case mix has shifted, with relatively more adult supraglottitis (often other organisms). Adults present with severe sore throat, odynophagia and a normal-looking oropharynx but a tender larynx; have a low threshold for fibreoptic assessment and airway vigilance.
Inspiratory stridor = supraglottic/glottic; biphasic stridor = subglottic/tracheal (fixed lesion); expiratory stridor/wheeze = lower (intrathoracic) airway. This guides where to look and how urgently.
