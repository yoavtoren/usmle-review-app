🔗 Regional metastasis of head & neck cancer

📖 Full topic explanation

Cervical nodal status is the single most important prognostic factor in head-and-neck SCC. This topic covers the neck levels and drainage patterns, N staging and extranodal extension, neck-dissection types, and the occult/unknown-primary node — with the rule that you never open-biopsy a neck node before FNAC.

🗺️ Neck levels & drainage

The neck is divided into , and each primary site drains to .

Levels: I submental/submandibular; II–IV upper/mid/lower deep cervical (jugular chain); V posterior triangle; VI central/anterior (pre-/paratracheal, Delphian); (VII superior mediastinal). This map standardises examination, imaging and surgery.
Drainage patterns: oral cavity → I–III; oropharynx/larynx/hypopharynx → II–IV; nasopharynx → II and V (often bilateral); thyroid → VI then III–IV; the level of a node therefore points to the likely primary site.

📊 N staging & extranodal extension

Nodal stage uses , and the prognostically critical .

N staging: based on the number, size and laterality (ipsilateral/bilateral/contralateral) of involved nodes (with a separate, more favourable system for HPV+ oropharyngeal and EBV+ nasopharyngeal cancer). Nodal disease roughly halves survival.
Extranodal extension (ENE): tumour breaching the node capsule into surrounding tissue is a strong adverse prognostic factor and, with positive margins, the key indication for adjuvant concurrent chemoradiotherapy. It also upstages disease.

🔪 Neck-dissection types

Neck dissections are , defined by which levels and non-lymphatic structures are removed/preserved.

Types: radical neck dissection = levels I–V + the three key non-lymphatic structures (SCM, internal jugular vein, accessory nerve XI); modified radical = levels I–V but preserves ≥1 of those structures (less morbidity); selective = preserves uninvolved levels (e.g. I–III for oral cancer); extended = removes additional levels/structures. The trend is toward function-preserving selective dissections.

🩺 Management of the neck

The neck is treated , or , matched to the primary's modality.

N0 neck: treat electively (neck dissection or radiotherapy, or sentinel-node biopsy for early oral cancer) when the occult-metastasis risk is significant (e.g. oral-tongue depth of invasion, supraglottic primaries which need bilateral treatment).
N+ neck: therapeutic neck dissection (often selective/modified radical) ± adjuvant (chemo)RT, or definitive (chemo)RT with the primary; post-chemoRT necks are reassessed by PET-CT before any salvage dissection.

❓ The unknown primary

A metastatic node with no obvious source is a , worked up systematically with attention to , and the .

Unknown primary: a neck node with metastatic SCC and no visible primary → examination under anaesthesia + panendoscopy with directed/blind biopsies, including tonsillectomy and tongue-base sampling, and PET-CT to find the occult primary.
Node level/biomarkers guide the search: a cystic level-II node that is p16/HPV-positive → oropharyngeal (tonsil/tongue base) primary; a level-V/EBV-positive node → nasopharynx; a lower-neck/supraclavicular node → consider an infraclavicular (lung/GI) primary (Virchow's node on the left).
Trap — no open biopsy first: FNAC (ultrasound-guided) is the first-line test for a neck node; an open/excisional biopsy risks tumour seeding and compromises definitive neck surgery and flap design, and worsens outcome in SCC.

📈 Prognosis

Nodal metastasis is the dominant prognostic factor, and extranodal extension markedly worsens it (and dictates chemoRT). HPV+/EBV+ nodal disease behaves more favourably. Distant metastasis (lung commonest) signals incurable disease.

🎯 Demonstrate it like an examiner wants

Commit first, then reveal the model structure and build the chain step by step.

Prompt: "A 55-year-old presents with a metastatic SCC neck node but no obvious primary. How do you investigate and manage?"

Your locked answer:
"A metastatic SCC node with no obvious primary is a carcinoma of unknown primary; the priority is to find the occult primary, and I never open-biopsy the node first."
The level and p16/EBV status direct the search: level II + p16 → oropharynx; level V + EBV → nasopharynx; lower neck → consider infraclavicular primary.
US-FNAC + p16/EBV, PET-CT, then EUA + panendoscopy with directed/blind biopsies, tonsillectomy and tongue-base sampling to locate the primary.
If a primary is found → treat that site + neck. If truly unknown → neck dissection and/or radiotherapy to the neck and likely mucosal sites, guided by p16/EBV, via the MDT.
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: A mucosal SCC sheds cells into lymphatics that drain to predictable cervical levels, so a node's location encodes the likely primary site. Small oropharyngeal/nasopharyngeal primaries can metastasise while remaining clinically occult, presenting only as a neck node — often cystic in HPV disease. Biomarkers (p16/EBV) and PET-CT plus targeted endoscopic biopsies localise the source, which determines stage-directed treatment.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"An oral-tongue SCC with a level-II node and extranodal extension on imaging." → resection + therapeutic neck dissection; adjuvant concurrent chemoRT (ENE/positive margins); rehabilitation and surveillance.
"A cystic level-II p16+ node, no visible primary." → US-FNAC + p16, PET-CT, EUA/panendoscopy with tonsillectomy and tongue-base biopsies; treat the (likely oropharyngeal) primary + neck; good prognosis if HPV+.
Injury or sacrifice of the spinal accessory nerve (XI) causes shoulder dysfunction (trapezius weakness, shoulder drop, painful "shoulder syndrome"). Modified radical/selective dissections aim to preserve XI, with physiotherapy if affected.
For early oral SCC, sentinel-node biopsy maps and samples the first-echelon node(s) to select patients for completion neck dissection, sparing a formal elective dissection in node-negative patients — reducing morbidity.
Their favourable biology and survival mean that nodal burden carries less adverse weight than in HPV−/EBV− disease, so dedicated staging systems avoid over-staging and better predict outcome.
Accessory nerve (XI), hypoglossal (XII), marginal mandibular branch of VII, vagus, phrenic nerve, internal jugular vein/carotid, and the thoracic duct (left level IV). Knowing them underpins safe, function-preserving surgery.
