👂 Acute otitis media

📖 Full topic explanation

Acute otitis media (AOM) is acute suppurative inflammation of the middle-ear cleft lasting under three weeks, overwhelmingly a disease of young children. This overview covers why children are prone, the bugs, the stages, how to diagnose it, the antibiotic decision, and the complications you must never miss.

🗂️ Definition & the otitis-media spectrum

AOM is the rapid onset of signs and symptoms of middle-ear inflammation with an effusion. It sits within a spectrum: . is defined by episode frequency and changes management. Getting these definitions straight at the start frames the whole answer.

Spectrum: AOM = acute infection (pain, fever, bulging drum). OME ("glue ear") = effusion without acute infection (painless hearing loss). CSOM = a permanent perforation with chronic discharge >3 months. AOM and OME interconvert; OME is the usual aftermath of an AOM episode.
Recurrent AOM = ≥3 episodes in 6 months or ≥4 in 12 months (with at least one in the last 6 months). It triggers consideration of ventilation tubes ± adenoidectomy and a review of risk factors and vaccination.

🧬 Applied anatomy — why children?

AOM peaks at because the infant with an immature tensor veli palatini, so it ventilates and protects the middle ear poorly. The middle-ear cleft is continuous with the mastoid air cells and nasopharynx, which is why infection spreads to the mastoid and why .

Age: by age 3, over 80% of children have had at least one episode; incidence falls after 5–7 years as the skull base matures and the tube lengthens and steepens. A first episode before 6 months predicts recurrent disease.
Child vs adult tube: the child tube is ~10° (near-horizontal), short and floppy, draining poorly and refluxing nasopharyngeal organisms; the adult tube is ~45°, longer and stiffer. Cleft palate (abnormal tensor veli palatini) and Down syndrome worsen this and cause near-universal middle-ear disease.
Adult red flag: a persistent unilateral middle-ear effusion in an adult can be caused by a nasopharyngeal carcinoma obstructing the tubal orifice — always examine the nasopharynx (flexible endoscopy) before simply placing a grommet.

⚙️ Pathophysiology

Viral trigger: RSV, rhinovirus, influenza and adenovirus damage the tubal mucosa and impair clearance; about two-thirds of AOM has a viral component. This is why many episodes resolve spontaneously and why influenza and pneumococcal vaccination reduce AOM.
Bacterial superinfection: negative pressure and reflux carry nasopharyngeal flora into the effusion, which is an ideal culture medium. The result is suppuration, drum bulging and severe otalgia.
Biofilms: communities of bacteria encased in matrix on the middle-ear/adenoid mucosa resist antibiotics and host defences, explaining treatment failure, persistence and recurrence — and part of the rationale for adenoidectomy in recurrent disease.

📊 Epidemiology & risk factors

Risk rises with , and falls with breastfeeding beyond six months and vaccination.

Modifiable & non-modifiable factors: daycare (viral exposure), tobacco smoke (mucosal irritation), bottle-feeding lying flat (reflux), lack of breastfeeding (loss of passive immunity), allergic rhinitis, adenoid hypertrophy, and cleft palate/Down syndrome. Counselling on smoke avoidance and feeding position is part of management.

🦠 Microbiology

The classic trio is , and ; carry a different spectrum, and .

S. pneumoniae: historically the commonest and the least likely to resolve spontaneously, most associated with mastoiditis. Resistance is via altered penicillin-binding proteins (raised MIC), overcome by high-dose amoxicillin, not by clavulanate.
Non-typeable H. influenzae: relatively more common since PCV; frequently β-lactamase producing → needs amoxicillin–clavulanate; linked to the otitis–conjunctivitis syndrome.
Moraxella catarrhalis: almost always β-lactamase positive but tends to mild disease with high spontaneous resolution; also covered by co-amoxiclav.
Neonates (<6 weeks): add Gram-negative enterics, Staph aureus and group B Streptococcus. Presentation is non-specific and complications more dangerous, so the threshold for tympanocentesis, culture and parenteral antibiotics is lower.
Post-PCV shift: conjugate vaccines (PCV7→13→15→20) reduced vaccine-serotype pneumococcal AOM and complications, leaving NTHi relatively more prominent with some serotype replacement — strengthening the case for co-amoxiclav when H. influenzae is likely.

🔬 The four stages (Politzer)

AOM evolves through predictable stages, and recognising them guides treatment and warns of complications.

StageDrum / findingsSymptom
1. Tubal occlusion / hyperaemiaRetraction, injected malleus handle, "cartwheel" vesselsBlockage, mild pain
2. SuppurationRed, bulging drum, lost landmarks/light reflexSevere throbbing otalgia, high fever
3. PerforationCentral perforation, pulsatile mucopurulent otorrhoeaSudden relief of pain + fever
4. Resolution / complicationDrum heals OR → coalescent mastoiditis / other complicationSettles or worsens

The marks perforation; signals a complication.

Stage 3 logic: rising pus pressure necroses a small area of pars tensa → a central perforation discharges, pressure falls and the throbbing pain abruptly eases as the discharge appears. Most heal spontaneously within weeks.
Red flag: persistent fever, deep pain, postauricular swelling/protruding pinna, vertigo, facial weakness, or deterioration after apparent improvement → suspect acute mastoiditis or another complication and image (CT). Do not just keep changing antibiotics.

👂 Clinical features

Older children and adults describe otalgia, blockage, fever and conductive hearing loss, with sudden relief and discharge if the drum perforates. Pre-verbal infants show — irritability, ear-tugging, fever, poor feeding and disturbed sleep — so the diagnosis rests on examination, not history.

Infant presentation: because they cannot localise pain, infants present with crying, fever, vomiting/diarrhoea and ear-pulling; ear-pulling alone is unreliable. This is why pneumatic otoscopy is essential to confirm an effusion rather than treating on symptoms.

🔎 Examination & diagnosis

Diagnosis requires (the AAP triad). The most reliable sign is a . . objectively support the effusion and the conductive loss.

AAP triad: (1) acute onset; (2) middle-ear effusion (bulging, reduced mobility, air–fluid level or otorrhoea); (3) middle-ear inflammation (distinct erythema or clear otalgia). Requiring all three avoids over-diagnosis.
Pneumatic otoscopy = bedside gold standard: a bulging drum that does not move on insufflation confirms an effusion under pressure; loss of the light reflex and an obscured malleus handle support it.
Trap: a crying or febrile child can have a pink/red drum from vascular engorgement without AOM. Erythema alone, with a mobile non-bulging drum, is not AOM — require bulging/effusion, not just colour.
Adjuncts: tympanometry shows a flat Type B trace with normal canal volume (effusion); audiometry shows a conductive loss of ~20–40 dB; tuning forks give a Rinne negative result with Weber lateralising to the affected ear.

💊 Management & the antibiotic decision

Pain control first: paracetamol/ibuprofen for all — pain is the dominant symptom and often the only thing needing treatment in mild disease.
Antibiotics now vs wait: always treat <6 months; <2 years with bilateral AOM; any age with otorrhoea or severe illness (T ≥39 °C, marked otalgia). A 48–72 h safety-net is reasonable for mild unilateral AOM in a well child >2 years, treating if not improving.
High-dose amoxicillin 80–90 mg/kg/day: the high dose keeps middle-ear levels above the raised pneumococcal MIC, overcoming PBP-mediated resistance. Course 5–10 days (longer/younger).
Amoxicillin–clavulanate when there is recent amoxicillin use (<30 days), failure at 48–72 h, concurrent purulent conjunctivitis, or recurrent AOM — to cover β-lactamase-producing H. influenzae/Moraxella. Penicillin allergy → cefuroxime/ceftriaxone or a macrolide.
Myringotomy ± tympanocentesis indications: severe bulging with intractable pain, failure of medical therapy, neonates/immunocompromised needing culture, or a suspected/early complication (impending mastoiditis, facial palsy). It gives instant pain relief and a microbiological sample; the incision is in the antero-inferior quadrant to avoid the ossicles and round window.

🔁 Recurrent AOM

Recurrent AOM (≥3 in 6 months / ≥4 in 12 months) is managed by .

Strategy: reduce daycare/smoke exposure, ensure pneumococcal and influenza vaccination, and consider grommets (which ventilate the middle ear and let any infection drain through the tube) ± adenoidectomy (removes a biofilm reservoir and tubal obstruction), reducing both episode frequency and effusion-related hearing loss.

🔴 Complications

Untreated or aggressive AOM spreads beyond the middle ear. are the highest-yield danger material (covered fully in Topic 11).

Complications: intratemporal — acute mastoiditis, facial-nerve palsy, labyrinthitis, petrositis (Gradenigo's triad), TM perforation, OME; intracranial — meningitis (commonest), extradural/subdural empyema, brain abscess, sigmoid/lateral sinus thrombosis, otitic hydrocephalus. Any red-flag feature mandates urgent CT and admission.

Acute necrotizing OM: in children debilitated by measles, scarlet fever or influenza, rapid mucosal necrosis causes a large/total perforation, profuse discharge and ossicular destruction, often leaving a permanent perforation and significant hearing loss. Now rare with vaccination.
Neonatal/immunocompromised AOM: atypical organisms, non-specific presentation and higher complication risk justify early tympanocentesis for culture and broader/parenteral antibiotics.

📈 Prognosis & prevention

Most AOM resolves within days, many without antibiotics. Prevention centres on pneumococcal and influenza vaccination, breastfeeding, avoiding tobacco smoke and upright feeding, with grommets for the recurrent group. Persistent effusion after an episode (OME) is followed because of its effect on hearing and speech.

🎯 Demonstrate it like an examiner wants

Commit to an answer first, then reveal the model structure and build the chain step by step.

Prompt: "A 2-year-old has 1 day of fever, irritability and right ear-tugging after a cold. Walk me through diagnosis and management."

Your locked answer:
"This is acute otitis media following a viral URTI. My first jobs are to confirm it on examination and to decide whether this child needs antibiotics now — and to screen for any complication."
AOM = acute suppurative middle-ear inflammation <3 weeks from Eustachian-tube dysfunction after a viral URTI with bacterial superinfection (pneumococcus, NTHi, Moraxella). Children are prone because of the short horizontal tube.
Pneumatic otoscopy: bulging, erythematous, immobile drum with lost landmarks (AAP triad). Screen for complications: postauricular swelling, facial movement, vertigo, meningism. Adjuncts: Type B tympanogram, conductive loss.
Analgesia for all. <2 y — if bilateral, treat immediately; if unilateral and mildly unwell, a 48–72 h safety-net is acceptable. First-line high-dose amoxicillin; co-amoxiclav if recent antibiotics/failure/conjunctivitis. Safety-net advice + review.
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: A viral URTI inflames the Eustachian-tube mucosa, obstructing it; trapped middle-ear air is absorbed, creating negative pressure and a sterile effusion. Nasopharyngeal bacteria reflux up the short horizontal tube and multiply in that effusion, producing pus that bulges the drum. Rising pressure causes severe otalgia and, if it perforates, sudden relief with mucopurulent otorrhoea.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and the advanced follow-ups examiners push on.
"An 18-month-old has 2 days of fever, irritability and ear-pulling after a cold. Pneumatic otoscopy shows a bulging, red, immobile right drum with lost landmarks — acute otitis media. I'd give analgesia, and as he is unwell with a clear bulging drum, start high-dose amoxicillin, give safety-net advice and review at 48–72 h, escalating to co-amoxiclav if no better."
"A 4-year-old treated for AOM re-presents at day 10 with fever, a tender boggy postauricular swelling pushing the pinna down-and-out." → acute coalescent mastoiditis. Admit; CRP/WBC; CT temporal bone; IV antibiotics + myringotomy; cortical mastoidectomy if subperiosteal abscess or failure. Screen for intracranial spread.
Intratemporal: acute mastoiditis, facial-nerve palsy, labyrinthitis, petrositis (Gradenigo's triad = otorrhoea + retro-orbital pain + VI palsy), TM perforation, OME. Intracranial: meningitis (commonest), extradural/subdural empyema, brain abscess, sigmoid/lateral sinus thrombosis, otitic hydrocephalus.
In ~15% the facial (Fallopian) canal is dehiscent in the middle ear, so suppuration directly inflames/compresses CN VII. Manage with urgent myringotomy + IV antibiotics; AOM-related palsy usually recovers fully. (Contrast chronic OM/cholesteatoma palsy, which needs mastoid surgery.)
Conductive loss: Rinne negative (bone > air) on the affected side, Weber lateralises to the affected ear. Tympanometry: Type B (flat) with normal canal volume (high volume would suggest a perforation/patent grommet). Audiometry shows an air–bone gap of ~20–40 dB.
Conjugate vaccines reduced vaccine-serotype pneumococcal AOM and complications, leaving non-typeable H. influenzae relatively more prominent with some serotype replacement. Net effect: fewer episodes, fewer mastoiditis cases, and a stronger case for co-amoxiclav when H. influenzae is likely.
To drain pus and obtain culture when: severe intractable pain/hyperpyrexia, failed medical therapy, neonate/immunocompromised, or impending/early complication (mastoiditis, facial palsy, suspected intracranial spread). The incision is in the antero-inferior quadrant to avoid the ossicles and round-window niche.
