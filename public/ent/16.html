😐 ENT aspects of facial nerve paralysis

📖 Full topic explanation

Facial palsy is approached by first separating upper- from lower-motor-neuron weakness, then localising the lesion along the nerve's long course, and identifying the cause. The two ENT imperatives are protect the eye and never dismiss a slowly progressive palsy — that is a tumour until proven otherwise.

🧬 Anatomy of the facial nerve

CN VII has a with several branch points that allow , and mixed .

Course: brainstem → cerebellopontine angle → internal auditory meatus (with CN VIII) → labyrinthine segment (narrowest, geniculate ganglion → greater petrosal nerve) → tympanic segment (over the oval window, often dehiscent) → mastoid segment (gives nerve to stapedius, then chorda tympani) → stylomastoid foramen → parotid, dividing into five branches.
Topognostic tests use the branch order to localise: lacrimation (Schirmer) tests above the geniculate; the stapedial reflex tests above the nerve-to-stapedius; taste/submandibular flow tests above the chorda tympani. The most proximal affected function locates the lesion.
Functions: motor to facial muscles + stapedius; parasympathetic to lacrimal (via greater petrosal) and submandibular/sublingual glands (via chorda); taste to the anterior two-thirds of the tongue (chorda); and sensation to the concha. This is why a palsy can include dry eye, hyperacusis and taste loss.

⬆️⬇️ Upper- vs lower-motor-neuron

The first bedside step is to decide , because UMN palsy implies a central (often stroke) cause needing different management.

UMN vs LMN: the forehead has bilateral cortical innervation, so an UMN lesion (stroke) spares the forehead (the patient can wrinkle it) and often spares emotional movement, whereas an LMN lesion paralyses the whole half of the face including the forehead. Forehead sparing = think central, urgent stroke pathway.

📋 House–Brackmann grading

Severity and recovery are documented with the .

House–Brackmann: I normal, II mild, III moderate (obvious but not disfiguring, complete eye closure with effort), IV moderately severe (incomplete eye closure), V severe (barely perceptible motion), VI total paralysis. Grading guides prognosis and tracks recovery.

🩺 Causes

The commonest is , then , with and not to be missed.

Bell's palsy: acute idiopathic LMN palsy (linked to HSV-1 reactivation), a diagnosis of exclusion reaching maximal weakness within ~72 h; ~85% recover. Treat early with oral corticosteroids (± antiviral) and eye care.
Ramsay Hunt & otologic: Ramsay Hunt (VZV) = palsy + vesicles in the concha/canal ± SNHL/vertigo, worse prognosis, treat with aciclovir + steroid. Otologic: AOM (dehiscent canal → myringotomy + antibiotics) and cholesteatoma/CSOM (bony erosion → mastoid surgery).
Tumour (the trap): a slowly progressive palsy, no recovery by 3 weeks, facial twitching/spasm, or a recurrent palsy suggests a neoplasm — parotid malignancy (examine the gland and neck), facial-nerve schwannoma, or a vestibular schwannoma. Bell's palsy never slowly progresses; if it does, image.
Systemic/bilateral: Lyme disease (often bilateral, endemic areas), sarcoidosis (Heerfordt syndrome), Guillain–Barré (bilateral), diabetes, and trauma/iatrogenic injury. Bilateral facial palsy is rarely Bell's — investigate for a systemic cause.

🔎 Investigation

Assess with , then as indicated.

Examination: confirm LMN, grade (HB), then otoscopy (vesicles, AOM, cholesteatoma), parotid and neck palpation (mass), oral cavity, eye (closure, Bell's phenomenon, corneal sensation), and the other cranial nerves. The exam often reveals the cause.
Tests: audiometry; ENoG/EMG for prognosis in complete palsy (especially trauma); MRI (CPA/IAM, parotid, perineural spread) for progressive/atypical/no-recovery cases; CT temporal bone for trauma/cholesteatoma; bloods (Lyme, glucose, ACE) where relevant.

👁️ Management

Two universal priorities: and ; chronic palsy is addressed with .

Eye care first: incomplete eye closure risks exposure keratopathy and corneal ulceration. Use lubricating drops by day, ointment and taping/patching at night, and refer to ophthalmology; a gold/platinum upper-lid weight or tarsorrhaphy for persistent palsy. This is the commonest exam "safety" mark.
Cause-specific: Bell's — oral steroids within 72 h (± antiviral); Ramsay Hunt — steroid + aciclovir; otologic — treat the infection/operate; trauma — steroids for delayed/partial, explore/decompress immediate-complete with >90–95% ENoG degeneration; tumour — surgery ± grafting.
Reanimation (chronic): for incomplete recovery — nerve repair/interposition grafts, hypoglossal–facial or masseteric–facial transfer, gracilis free-muscle transfer, plus static procedures (brow lift, lid weight, fascial slings) and botulinum toxin for synkinesis. Restores tone, symmetry and eye closure.

📈 Prognosis

Bell's palsy recovers in ~85% (worse if complete or in older/diabetic patients); Ramsay Hunt recovers less well. Incomplete palsies generally recover fully; complete palsies with high ENoG degeneration and tumour-related palsies have poorer outcomes and may need reanimation. Aberrant regeneration causes synkinesis and crocodile tears.

🧠 Thinking questions (tap to reveal)

UMN (stroke) spares the forehead (bilateral cortical supply) and may spare emotional movement; LMN paralyses the whole hemiface including the forehead. Forehead sparing diverts the patient to a stroke pathway, not an ENT one.
Slowly progressive course, no recovery by 3 weeks, facial twitching/spasm, recurrence, bilateral palsy, or other cranial-nerve/ear signs. These mandate imaging (MRI/CT) and a search for tumour or systemic disease.
Eye protection (lubricants, night taping), oral corticosteroids within 72 h (e.g. prednisolone), consider an antiviral if severe/Ramsay-Hunt-like, reassure (~85% recover), and review for recovery — image if it doesn't recover or progresses.
Above the geniculate → dry eye (greater petrosal); above the nerve-to-stapedius → hyperacusis; above the chorda tympani → loss of taste and reduced salivation. Topognostic testing uses this order to localise.
Assume cholesteatoma eroding the facial canal: otomicroscopy, HRCT, IV antibiotics, and urgent mastoid surgery to decompress and eradicate disease. Contrast with AOM palsy (dehiscent canal → myringotomy + antibiotics, usually recovers).
Failure of eye closure causes exposure keratopathy and corneal ulceration, which can permanently damage vision. Lubrication and night-time taping/patching (and a lid weight/tarsorrhaphy if prolonged) protect the cornea regardless of the cause.

🎯 Demonstrate it like an examiner wants

Commit first, then reveal the model structure and build the chain step by step.

Prompt: "A 35-year-old wakes with a complete right-sided facial droop including the forehead, and ear pain. Assess and manage."

Your locked answer:
"Forehead involvement makes this a lower-motor-neuron palsy, so this is a peripheral CN VII problem — I must find the cause and protect the eye."
Grade (House–Brackmann); otoscopy for vesicles (Ramsay Hunt), AOM or cholesteatoma; palpate the parotid/neck; check the eye and other cranial nerves. Ear pain + vesicles would change this to Ramsay Hunt.
If a clear Bell's picture, treat clinically; otherwise audiometry, ENoG/EMG (complete palsy), and MRI/CT for atypical, progressive, otologic or traumatic causes.
Eye protection for all; Bell's → oral steroids within 72 h (± antiviral); Ramsay Hunt → aciclovir + steroid; otologic → treat infection/operate; safety-net for non-recovery → image for tumour.
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: In Bell's palsy, HSV-1 reactivation inflames the facial nerve, which swells within the rigid bony Fallopian canal — narrowest at the labyrinthine segment — causing compression, ischaemia and conduction block. This produces a complete lower-motor-neuron weakness of the whole hemiface. Early corticosteroids reduce the oedema and improve the odds of full recovery, while eye protection prevents corneal damage during the palsy.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"A 35-year-old has a sudden complete LMN right facial palsy with forehead involvement, normal ears and no other deficit." → Bell's palsy: eye protection, oral steroids within 72 h, reassure (~85% recover), safety-net; image only if it fails to recover or progresses.
"A facial weakness that has worsened over months with facial twitching and a firm parotid lump." → parotid malignancy with perineural invasion: examine the neck, MRI + biopsy/FNAC, and refer for oncologic surgery; a slowly progressive palsy is never Bell's.
Intracranial → CPA → IAM → labyrinthine (narrowest, geniculate) → tympanic (often dehiscent over the oval window) → mastoid (stapedius, chorda) → stylomastoid foramen → parotid (5 branches). The labyrinthine segment is the tightest, hence vulnerable to oedema in Bell's.
Aberrant regeneration: synkinesis = involuntary co-movement (e.g. eye closes when smiling); crocodile (gustatory) tears = lacrimation while eating, from misdirected secretomotor fibres to the lacrimal gland. Managed with botulinum toxin and rehabilitation.
Dynamic: primary repair/interposition graft, hypoglossal- or masseteric-to-facial transfer, and gracilis free-muscle transfer (with cross-face nerve graft) for a smile. Static: brow lift, fascial slings, upper-lid gold/platinum weight, tarsorrhaphy. Choice depends on chronicity and nerve availability.
In complete palsy (especially traumatic), within ~2 weeks: >90–95% degeneration versus the normal side predicts poor recovery and supports surgical decompression; lesser degeneration favours conservative care.
