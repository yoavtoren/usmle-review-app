🫁 Suffocation — causes, symptoms, treatment, tracheotomy & coniotomy

📖 Full topic explanation

This topic is acute upper-airway obstruction and how to relieve it. Know the causes by level, read the stridor to localise the block, and master the surgical airways — emergency cricothyroidotomy (coniotomy) versus planned tracheostomy.
🔴 fatal/critical🟠 risk/demographic🔵 key context🟢 confirmatory🟣 trap

🗂️ Causes of upper-airway obstruction

Causes span , and can be grouped by .

Causes: foreign body; infection (epiglottitis, croup, bacterial tracheitis, deep-neck/retropharyngeal abscess, Ludwig's angina); angioedema/anaphylaxis; trauma/burns/inhalation; tumour (laryngeal/pharyngeal); bilateral vocal-cord palsy; and laryngospasm. Identifying the cause guides specific treatment (e.g. adrenaline for anaphylaxis, Heimlich for FB).
Localise by stridor: inspiratory stridor → supraglottic/glottic; biphasic → subglottic/tracheal (fixed); expiratory wheeze → lower airway. The character and timing point to where the obstruction is and what airway will work.

🚩 Symptoms & signs

Recognise and the ominous transition to .

Signs: stridor, dyspnoea, use of accessory muscles, tracheal tug, intercostal/sternal recession, tachypnoea, tachycardia, restlessness/agitation, and the patient sitting upright/forward. Drooling suggests supraglottic pathology.
Decompensation: falling respiratory effort, exhaustion, drowsiness, bradycardia, cyanosis and silent chest herald imminent arrest. Do not be reassured by a "quieter" patient — declining stridor with rising drowsiness is a pre-terminal sign demanding an immediate airway.

🆘 Emergency management

Apply a with , escalating to a when intubation fails ("can't intubate, can't oxygenate").

Stepwise: call for senior help (ENT + anaesthetics), high-flow oxygen, position of comfort, monitor; choking → back blows then abdominal thrusts (Heimlich); prepare for definitive airway. Heliox and nebulised adrenaline can temporise.
Cause-specific: anaphylaxis/angioedema → IM adrenaline + steroids/antihistamine (C1-esterase concentrate for hereditary angioedema); croup → steroids/adrenaline; epiglottitis → controlled airway + antibiotics; foreign body → removal; abscess → drainage.
Surgical airway: when you can't intubate and can't oxygenate, perform an emergency cricothyroidotomy (coniotomy) — the fastest, safest emergency surgical airway — then convert to a formal tracheostomy.

🔪 Tracheostomy

A tracheostomy is a planned/semi-elective airway with broad , a defined , and important .

Indications: bypass upper-airway obstruction, prolonged ventilation/weaning, pulmonary toilet (clearing secretions), and airway protection (e.g. neurological). It also facilitates major head-and-neck surgery.
Technique/site: a vertical or horizontal incision, then entry between the 2nd–4th tracheal rings (avoiding the cricoid above and the innominate vessels below); the isthmus of the thyroid is divided/retracted. Percutaneous dilatational tracheostomy is common in ICU.
Complications: early — bleeding, tube misplacement/displacement, pneumothorax, surgical emphysema; late — tracheal stenosis, granulation, tracheo-innominate artery fistula (catastrophic bleed), tracheo-oesophageal fistula, infection and tube blockage. A displaced/blocked tube is an emergency (follow tracheostomy-emergency algorithms).

⚡ Cricothyroidotomy (coniotomy)

The emergency surgical airway is the , with a .

Cricothyroidotomy: a horizontal incision through the cricothyroid membrane (between thyroid and cricoid cartilages) and insertion of a small tube — faster and safer than tracheostomy in an emergency (superficial, avascular, easy landmark). Convert to a formal tracheostomy within ~24–48 h to avoid subglottic stenosis.
Paediatric caveat: surgical cricothyroidotomy is avoided in young children (<~10–12 years) because of the small, soft larynx and high stenosis risk; use needle cricothyroidotomy with jet/low-pressure oxygenation as a temporising measure instead.

📈 Outcome

Prompt recognition and the right airway are life-saving. The principles are: read the stridor, treat the cause, never let a tiring patient lull you, and have a clear "can't intubate, can't oxygenate" plan with surgical-airway competence.

🧠 Thinking questions (tap to reveal)

Foreign body; infection (epiglottitis, croup, bacterial tracheitis, deep-neck abscess, Ludwig's); anaphylaxis/angioedema; trauma/burns/inhalation; tumour; bilateral cord palsy; laryngospasm. Group by level using the stridor.
Inspiratory → supraglottic/glottic; biphasic → subglottic/tracheal; expiratory → lower airway. Combine with drooling (supraglottic) and voice change to pinpoint the level.
Reduced stridor with rising drowsiness, falling effort, bradycardia and cyanosis indicates exhaustion and near-complete obstruction, not improvement — it demands an immediate definitive airway.
Cricothyroidotomy (through the cricothyroid membrane) is the emergency "can't intubate, can't oxygenate" airway — fast and safe; convert to a tracheostomy (between the 2nd–4th tracheal rings) within 24–48 h. Tracheostomy is the planned/long-term airway.
Early: bleeding, tube misplacement/displacement, pneumothorax, surgical emphysema. Late: tracheal stenosis, granulation, tracheo-innominate fistula (massive bleed), tracheo-oesophageal fistula, infection, tube blockage.
The small, soft paediatric larynx makes it technically hard and prone to subglottic stenosis. Use a needle cricothyroidotomy with oxygenation as a temporising measure and secure a definitive airway promptly.

Similar because both are surgical airways through the neck.
✅ Different in: cricothyroidotomy = emergency, cricothyroid membrane, fast; tracheostomy = planned, 2nd–4th rings, long-term.
Similar because both are noisy breathing.
✅ Different in: stridor = upper airway (often inspiratory); wheeze = lower airway (expiratory).
Similar because both swell the airway.
✅ Different in: angioedema = rapid lip/tongue swelling, allergy/ACE-inhibitor/hereditary → adrenaline/C1-INH; infective = fever, toxic, abscess/epiglottitis.

Similar because both can have reduced stridor.

🎯 Demonstrate it like an examiner wants

Commit first, then reveal the model structure and build the chain step by step.

Prompt: "An adult with a known laryngeal tumour arrives with severe stridor and is tiring; attempts to intubate fail. What do you do?"

Your locked answer:
"Failed intubation in a tiring, obstructed patient is a 'can't intubate, can't oxygenate' (CICO) emergency — I move immediately to a surgical airway."
Maximal oxygenation, call senior ENT/anaesthetics, get the difficult-airway/front-of-neck-access kit; identify the cricothyroid membrane.
Perform an emergency cricothyroidotomy (scalpel-bougie-tube or surgical technique), confirm with capnography, and ventilate/oxygenate.
Once oxygenated, stabilise, then convert to a formal tracheostomy (and definitively manage the tumour). Document and debrief.
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: A glottic/supraglottic tumour narrows the airway so that, under the added insult of secretions and failed intubation attempts, oxygen delivery fails. The cricothyroid membrane is a superficial, relatively avascular gap between the thyroid and cricoid cartilages, allowing rapid front-of-neck access below the obstruction. A tube placed there restores oxygenation within seconds, after which a controlled tracheostomy provides a durable airway.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"Failed intubation, falling sats, obstructing laryngeal tumour." → declare CICO, maximal oxygen, call for help, emergency cricothyroidotomy, confirm with capnography, then convert to a formal tracheostomy.
"A ward patient with a tracheostomy becomes distressed and desaturates." → follow the tracheostomy emergency algorithm: oxygen to face and stoma, remove inner cannula, attempt suction/pass a catheter; if not patent, deflate cuff/remove tube and oxygenate via the upper airway or re-establish an airway. Call for help early.
A herald bleed may precede massive haemorrhage. Acutely over-inflate the cuff or apply digital pressure (finger in the stoma compressing the artery against the sternum — the Utley manoeuvre), resuscitate, and take to theatre for definitive vascular control.
High-flow oxygen, Heliox (lower-density gas eases flow through a narrowing), nebulised adrenaline (mucosal vasoconstriction), steroids (oedema), and positioning. These bridge to a definitive airway but do not replace it.
It is superficial, easily palpated, relatively avascular, and lies below the typical site of supraglottic/glottic obstruction, allowing the fastest reliable front-of-neck access. Formal tracheostomy is slower and more vascular — unsuitable for a crash airway.
Ensure the original indication has resolved, the patient can protect/clear the airway, then progressively downsize, cap and assess upper-airway patency (cuff-deflation trials, capping tolerance) before removal, with monitoring and the ability to re-secure the airway.
