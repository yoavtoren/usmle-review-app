👂❓ Differential diagnosis of hypacusis

📖 Full topic explanation

Hypacusis (hearing loss) is approached by answering four questions: conductive or sensorineural? one ear or both? sudden or gradual? congenital or acquired? The answers localise the lesion along the pathway and flag the two emergencies — sudden SNHL and an asymmetric SNHL hiding a vestibular schwannoma.

🗂️ Classification framework

First localise: , and add for SNHL. Then characterise by , because these reshuffle the differential.

Conductive vs sensorineural: conductive = a problem in sound delivery (canal, drum, ossicles); sensorineural = a problem in transduction/transmission (cochlea or cochlear nerve). Tuning forks and the air–bone gap on audiometry separate them — this is the first branch point.
Cochlear vs retrocochlear: within SNHL, distinguish a cochlear (hair-cell) lesion from a retrocochlear (nerve/brainstem) one. Recruitment, good word scores suggest cochlear; poor word scores out of proportion, tone decay, abnormal ABR suggest retrocochlear → MRI for vestibular schwannoma.
Onset/laterality/age: sudden SNHL is an emergency; unilateral/asymmetric SNHL needs a schwannoma excluded; bilateral symmetrical high-frequency SNHL in the elderly is presbycusis; congenital loss has its own genetic/syndromic differential (Topic 02).

👂 Conductive causes

Conductive loss arises in the or the , with a classic trap: a .

External ear: wax impaction (commonest reversible cause), foreign body, otitis externa, exostoses/osteoma, keratosis obturans, and congenital atresia. Usually obvious on otoscopy.
Middle ear: OME, AOM, perforation/CSOM, cholesteatoma, otosclerosis, ossicular discontinuity, tympanosclerosis, haemotympanum/barotrauma, and a glomus tumour. The drum and tympanogram point to the cause.
Trap — superior canal dehiscence: a low-frequency air–bone gap mimicking otosclerosis, but with intact/low-threshold stapedial reflexes, autophony and the Tullio phenomenon and a CT dehiscence. Don't operate on it as "otosclerosis".

🌀 Sensorineural causes

SNHL spans , , , , and the crucial .

Presbycusis & NIHL: presbycusis = age-related, bilateral, symmetrical, high-frequency SNHL with poor speech-in-noise; NIHL = a 4 kHz notch from noise exposure. Both are bilateral and gradual — the commonest acquired SNHLs.
Ototoxicity: aminoglycosides, cisplatin, loop diuretics, high-dose aspirin/quinine damage cochlear hair cells (high-frequency loss first, often with tinnitus/imbalance). Drug history is essential; some loss is preventable by monitoring.
Sudden SNHL = emergency: ≥30 dB over ≥3 contiguous frequencies within 72 h; mostly idiopathic but must exclude a retrocochlear lesion. Treat urgently with corticosteroids (oral ± intratympanic) and arrange MRI — early treatment improves recovery.
Other SNHL: Ménière's (fluctuating low-frequency SNHL + episodic vertigo + tinnitus + fullness), labyrinthitis, autoimmune inner-ear disease (rapidly progressive bilateral, steroid-responsive), and infections (mumps, measles, congenital CMV, meningitis).
Vestibular schwannoma: any unilateral/asymmetric SNHL, unilateral tinnitus, or poor word discrimination out of proportion must prompt MRI of the IAM to exclude this retrocochlear tumour. This is the single most important "don't miss" of asymmetric SNHL.

🔎 Clinical assessment

The work-up is .

Sequence: history (onset, laterality, tinnitus/vertigo, noise/drugs/family), otoscopy, tuning forks (Weber/Rinne), then pure-tone & speech audiometry, tympanometry and reflexes; add OAE/ABR and MRI for asymmetric/retrocochlear pictures, and bloods for autoimmune/infective causes.

🎵 Tuning forks & audiometry logic

give an immediate localisation, refined by .

Tuning forks (512 Hz): Rinne negative (BC>AC) = conductive on that side; Weber lateralises to the affected ear in conductive loss and to the better ear in SNHL. Beware the false-negative Rinne from a dead ear (the sound is heard by the opposite cochlea).
Audiogram: an air–bone gap = the conductive component; symmetrical high-frequency loss = presbycusis/NIHL; a 4 kHz notch = noise; low-frequency fluctuating loss = Ménière's. Speech audiometry rollover and abnormal ABR point retrocochlear.

🚩 Red flags & management principle

Two findings change urgency: and . Otherwise treat the underlying cause and rehabilitate hearing (Topic 12).

Sudden SNHL: a true ENT emergency — same-week corticosteroids and MRI. Delay reduces the chance of recovery; never dismiss "muffled hearing after a cold" as just wax without an audiogram.
Asymmetry: a persistent inter-aural difference (commonly ≥15 dB over several frequencies) or unilateral tinnitus warrants MRI of the internal auditory meatus to exclude a vestibular schwannoma.

📈 Outcome

Conductive losses are largely correctable (wax removal, surgery, aids); SNHL is managed by treating reversible causes early and rehabilitating with aids or implants. The diagnostic priority is to catch the treatable emergency (sudden SNHL) and the dangerous tumour (schwannoma).

🧠 Thinking questions (tap to reveal)

Answer four questions: conductive vs SNHL? unilateral vs bilateral? sudden vs gradual? congenital vs acquired? Then otoscopy + tuning forks localise, audiometry quantifies, and MRI/special tests are reserved for asymmetric/retrocochlear or sudden cases.
Conductive: Rinne negative (BC>AC) on the affected side, Weber lateralises to the affected ear. SNHL: Rinne positive bilaterally, Weber lateralises to the better ear. A false-negative Rinne indicates a dead ear.
Sudden SNHL (urgent steroids + MRI) and asymmetric SNHL/unilateral tinnitus (MRI for vestibular schwannoma). Also a discharging ear with new SNHL/vertigo (suppurative labyrinthitis).
Cochlear lesions show recruitment and reasonable word scores; retrocochlear lesions show disproportionately poor word discrimination, rollover, tone decay, abnormal ABR (prolonged wave V/I–V), and are confirmed by MRI.
Aminoglycosides, cisplatin, loop diuretics, high-dose salicylates/quinine. They damage outer hair cells, causing high-frequency SNHL first, with tinnitus ± imbalance; cisplatin and aminoglycosides can be permanent — monitor hearing during treatment.
Otosclerosis, ossicular discontinuity, congenital ossicular fixation, tympanosclerosis, and superior canal dehiscence (SSCD). SSCD is the trap — intact reflexes, autophony, Tullio, CT dehiscence — and must not be treated as otosclerosis.

🎯 Demonstrate it like an examiner wants

Commit first, then reveal the model structure and build the chain step by step.

Prompt: "A 55-year-old reports gradually worsening hearing in the right ear with right-sided tinnitus. How do you work this up?"

Your locked answer:
"Unilateral progressive SNHL with unilateral tinnitus is a vestibular schwannoma until excluded, so an MRI of the IAM is mandatory."
Use the four-question framework: SNHL, unilateral, gradual, acquired. State the localisation goal: cochlear vs retrocochlear.
Otoscopy (normal), tuning forks (Weber to the good ear, Rinne +), pure-tone + speech audiometry (look for word scores worse than pure tones), tympanometry/reflexes; MRI IAM with gadolinium.
If MRI confirms a schwannoma → observe/radiosurgery/microsurgery by size (Topic 52); if not, treat the SNHL cause and rehabilitate with a hearing aid. Counsel and monitor.
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: A vestibular schwannoma arises from Schwann cells of the vestibular nerve in the internal auditory meatus and grows to compress the cochlear nerve and its blood supply. This produces a retrocochlear pattern of asymmetric SNHL and tinnitus with word scores poorer than the pure-tone average. Because plain audiometry cannot exclude it, gadolinium MRI of the IAM is the definitive test.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"A 40-year-old wakes with a dead-feeling right ear; Weber goes to the left." → sudden SNHL: audiogram confirms, start corticosteroids urgently (oral ± intratympanic), and arrange MRI to exclude a retrocochlear lesion. Time-critical.
"A 72-year-old hears poorly in noise; symmetrical high-frequency SNHL on PTA." → presbycusis; exclude wax/ear disease, fit hearing aids, give communication tactics and review.
Word discrimination worse than the pure-tone average predicts, rollover on speech audiometry, tone decay, absent acoustic-reflexes/reflex decay, and an abnormal ABR (prolonged wave V or I–V interval). Confirm with gadolinium MRI.
Consider autoimmune inner-ear disease (a steroid trial is both diagnostic and therapeutic), infections, and syndromic causes; bloods (autoimmune screen), MRI, and serial audiograms. Early steroids may preserve hearing.
Otoscopy + tympanometry first (commonest cause is OME), then age-appropriate audiometry; if SNHL, work up genetic/syndromic causes, congenital CMV, and image; rehabilitate early to protect language (Topics 02, 12).
A claimed loss without an organic basis (often medico-legal/psychogenic). Suspect with inconsistent audiometry; detect with the Stenger test, objective OAE/ABR (normal despite claimed loss), and test–retest variability.
