👂💧 Secretory otitis media (otitis media with effusion)

The Overview is a full walk-through of the topic. Tap any coloured word for an extra exam pearl. Then work through all 5 tabs.

📖 Full topic explanation

Secretory otitis media (otitis media with effusion, "glue ear") is a middle-ear effusion without acute infection — the commonest cause of childhood hearing loss. The themes are: it is painless and conductive; most cases resolve; and an adult with a unilateral effusion needs the nasopharynx examined.
🔴 fatal/critical🟠 risk/demographic🔵 key context🟢 confirmatory🟣 trap

🗂️ Definition & spectrum

OME is the presence of fluid in the middle ear behind an intact drum without signs of acute inflammation, persisting (by convention) beyond 3 months when "chronic". It is one point on the otitis-media spectrum — distinct from — and frequently the residue of an AOM episode.

Spectrum: AOM = acute infection (pain, fever, bulging drum); OME = effusion without acute infection (painless hearing loss); CSOM = permanent perforation with chronic discharge. They interconvert, and OME is the typical aftermath of AOM.

📊 Epidemiology & risk groups

OME is extremely common in early childhood (peak ~2 and ~5 years), and is the leading cause of acquired hearing loss in children. Risk rises with , and is near-universal in .

Risk factors: passive smoking, daycare attendance, lack of breastfeeding, supine bottle-feeding, allergic rhinitis, adenoid hypertrophy, and recurrent URTIs/AOM. Winter incidence is higher. Most are otherwise-healthy children whose disease resolves spontaneously.
High-risk children: cleft palate (abnormal tensor veli palatini → persistent OME) and Down syndrome (narrow tube, hypotonia, immune factors) have chronic disease and a lower treatment threshold — though hearing aids are often preferred over grommets given anatomy and complication rates.

⚙️ Pathophysiology

The core lesion is : poor ventilation → absorption of middle-ear air → negative pressure → a sterile transudate. Persistent inflammation drives , and on the mucosa/adenoid sustains it.

ET dysfunction: the child's short, horizontal tube with an immature tensor veli palatini ventilates poorly; obstruction (adenoids, allergy, inflammation) compounds it. The same mechanism causes AOM, but here without acute infection.
Serous → mucoid: sustained inflammation causes goblet-cell and glandular metaplasia, thickening the effusion into tenacious glue that dampens ossicular movement and impairs hearing.
Biofilms: matrix-encased bacterial communities on the middle-ear and adenoid mucosa resist antibiotics and host defences, explaining why antibiotics rarely cure OME and part of the rationale for adenoidectomy.

🔴 The adult red flag

A demands nasopharyngeal examination before any grommet.

Unilateral adult OME = nasopharyngeal carcinoma until proven otherwise. A tumour at the fossa of Rosenmüller obstructs the Eustachian-tube orifice → one-sided effusion. Flexible nasendoscopy, check for a neck node/epistaxis, and biopsy any lesion. Do not just insert a grommet and discharge.

👂 Clinical features & examination

Children present with ; otoscopy shows a and reduced mobility. Confirm objectively with ; a .

Presentation: inattention, "turning up the TV", mishearing, poor school performance, speech/language delay, clumsiness/balance problems, and recurrent AOM. There is no pain or fever — the absence of acute symptoms defines it.
Otoscopy: a dull, retracted, amber/grey or bluish drum with a visible air–fluid level or bubbles, reduced mobility on pneumatic otoscopy, and sometimes early retraction pockets to look for.
Objective tests: tympanometry Type B (flat, normal canal volume) confirms effusion; Type C = negative pressure without frank effusion. Audiometry shows a conductive loss, typically ~25–30 dB; use age-appropriate testing (visual reinforcement/play) in young children.
Trap: erythema alone (e.g. a crying child) with a mobile, non-retracted drum is not OME. Diagnose on the effusion (dull, retracted, fluid level, reduced mobility, Type B), not colour.

🔄 Natural history

OME is usually self-limiting: roughly half resolve within 3 months and the majority within a year, which is why active observation is first-line rather than immediate intervention.

💊 Management

Begin with with hearing tactics; . Persistent disease with hearing/developmental impact → , with as an alternative (especially in high-risk children) and autoinflation for motivated older children.

Watchful waiting: two audiograms over ~3 months while most resolve; optimise the listening environment, treat allergy/adenoid symptoms, and counsel the family. Document persistence before intervening.
What NOT to use: antibiotics, oral/intranasal steroids, antihistamines and decongestants give no durable benefit for OME and are not recommended as treatment. Avoid repeated antibiotic courses for "glue ear".
Surgery: grommets for bilateral OME persisting >3 months with hearing loss/impact; adenoidectomy added for nasal obstruction/recurrence or at re-insertion (removes obstruction and biofilm reservoir). Tubes typically extrude in 6–12 months.
Hearing aids are a valid non-surgical option, particularly in Down syndrome/cleft palate (narrow canals, higher tube-complication rates) or where surgery is declined/contraindicated.

⚠️ Sequelae of chronic negative pressure

Persistent OME and tubal dysfunction can cause , plus tympanosclerosis and ossicular erosion — and the conductive loss itself threatens .

Structural sequelae: deep posterosuperior/attic retraction pockets can retain keratin and become an acquired cholesteatoma; tympanic atelectasis, tympanosclerosis and ossicular erosion also occur. This is why deep retractions are followed or treated, not ignored.
Developmental impact: fluctuating conductive loss during the language-acquisition window delays speech/literacy and causes inattention/behavioural problems sometimes mistaken for primary attention disorders. Restoring hearing protects development.

📈 Prognosis & prevention

Most childhood OME resolves spontaneously with normal long-term hearing; grommets reliably restore hearing while in place. Prevention/risk reduction includes smoke avoidance, breastfeeding, upright feeding, treating allergy, and vaccination (reducing AOM that seeds OME).

🎯 Demonstrate it like an examiner wants

Commit first, then reveal the model structure and build the chain step by step.

Prompt: "A 4-year-old's teacher reports he ignores instructions and the TV is always loud. No ear pain. How do you proceed?"

Your locked answer:
"Painless hearing loss with inattention in a child is most likely otitis media with effusion. I want to confirm an effusion, quantify the hearing, and exclude permanent SNHL."
OME = middle-ear effusion without acute infection from ET dysfunction. Ask about cleft palate, Down syndrome, allergy, smoke exposure and recent AOM, and screen for speech/behaviour impact.
Pneumatic otoscopy (dull retracted drum, fluid level), tympanometry (Type B), and age-appropriate audiometry showing a ~25–30 dB conductive loss. Examine the nose/adenoids.
Active observation 3 months with two audiograms (most resolve); hearing tactics. No antibiotics/steroids/antihistamines. If it persists with significant loss/impact → grommets ± adenoidectomy, or hearing aids (especially Down/cleft). Safety-net for cholesteatoma signs.
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: A poorly ventilating Eustachian tube lets middle-ear air be absorbed, creating negative pressure that draws in a sterile transudate. Ongoing inflammation drives goblet-cell metaplasia, thickening the fluid into "glue" that dampens ossicular movement and produces a conductive loss. Sustained negative pressure also retracts the drum, the long-term risk being a keratin-filled pocket that becomes cholesteatoma.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"A 4-year-old has painless hearing difficulty, speech that's a little behind and a dull retracted right drum with bubbles; tympanometry is Type B with a 30 dB conductive loss." → OME: active observation 3 months with repeat audiometry; hearing tactics; if persistent with impact, grommets ± adenoidectomy.
"A 48-year-old man has 2 months of unilateral aural fullness and a serous effusion; he also reports occasional blood-stained postnasal discharge." → urgent nasendoscopy for nasopharyngeal carcinoma, neck exam, MRI and biopsy. Never insert a grommet and discharge.
Bilateral OME persisting >3 months with a hearing level ~25–30 dB or worse and/or significant developmental, speech, behavioural or educational impact; recurrent AOM with effusion; and high-risk ears (with hearing-aid alternative considered). Document persistence with two audiograms first.
Otorrhoea (commonest), tympanosclerosis, persistent perforation after extrusion, premature extrusion or retention, granulation, and rarely a tube falling into the middle ear. Counsel on water precautions (routine swimming usually allowed) and review for extrusion at 6–12 months.
Adenoid tissue obstructs the tubal orifice and harbours biofilm. Adding adenoidectomy reduces OME recurrence and the need for repeat tubes, and is indicated with nasal obstruction, recurrent disease, or at second grommet insertion (≈ in children >4 years for greatest benefit).
Fluctuating conductive loss during the language-acquisition window can delay speech and literacy and cause inattention/behavioural problems often mistaken for primary attention disorders. Restoring hearing (observation→ventilation/aids) protects development — the reason we don't simply ignore "just fluid".
