👅 Oropharyngeal & oral cavity tumors

The Overview is a full walk-through of the topic. Tap any coloured word for an extra exam pearl. Then work through all 5 tabs.

📖 Full topic explanation

Oral-cavity and oropharyngeal cancers are mostly squamous cell carcinoma, but they diverge in biology: oral-cavity SCC is tobacco/alcohol/betel-driven, while oropharyngeal SCC is increasingly HPV-driven (younger patients, cystic neck nodes, better prognosis). This topic is presentation/diagnosis; treatment is Topic 40.
🔴 fatal/critical🟠 risk/demographic🔵 key context🟢 confirmatory🟣 trap

🗂️ Subsites & anatomy

Distinguish the from the , as their drainage and behaviour differ.

Oral cavity: lips, anterior two-thirds of tongue, floor of mouth, buccal mucosa, gingiva, hard palate and retromolar trigone. Drains to levels I–III; presents earlier (visible/symptomatic).
Oropharynx: tonsil/tonsillar fossa, base of tongue, soft palate and posterior pharyngeal wall. Rich lymphatics → early nodal spread (levels II–III), often the presenting cystic neck node in HPV disease.

🚬 Aetiology — two different stories

; , which changes the patient and prognosis.

Oral-cavity SCC: driven by tobacco (smoked/chewed), alcohol and betel/areca nut, often arising from leukoplakia/erythroplakia. Typically older patients with classic risk factors.
HPV+ oropharyngeal SCC: HPV-16-related, in younger, often non-smoking patients; presents with a small primary (tonsil/tongue base) and a cystic upper-neck node; p16-positive, with a better prognosis and separate (more favourable) staging.

👤 Presentation

Features include a , , and a .

Local: a persistent (>3 weeks) ulcer, mass, or red/white patch, with bleeding, loose teeth, ill-fitting dentures, or a non-healing extraction socket. Any such lesion in a smoker/drinker is biopsied.
Symptoms: oral pain, referred otalgia (CN IX/V), dysphagia/odynophagia, trismus (deep infiltration), altered speech, and a sensation of a mass. Otalgia with a normal ear is a classic referred-pain red flag.
Neck node: oropharyngeal (especially HPV+) cancer commonly presents as a painless, sometimes cystic, upper-neck (level II) node with an inconspicuous primary — do not mistake a cystic node in an adult for a simple branchial cyst.

🔎 Diagnosis & staging

Diagnosis uses , , and .

Tissue: full oral/oropharyngeal examination and palpation, biopsy of the lesion, and EUA + panendoscopy to map the primary and exclude synchronous tumours (field cancerisation).
Imaging: MRI (soft-tissue/tongue extent), CT (bone/mandible, neck, chest), an OPG for mandibular involvement, ultrasound-guided FNAC of a neck node, and PET-CT for staging/unknown primary. Assess depth of invasion (predicts nodal risk).
p16/HPV: test oropharyngeal tumours/nodes for p16/HPV — it defines the favourable HPV+ group and uses a separate TNM staging. Stage with AJCC TNM.

🧭 Spread & the neck

Both spread by , which determines neck treatment (Topic 46).

Lymphatic spread: oral cavity → levels I–III (tongue tip can skip to III/IV); oropharynx → levels II–III (often bilateral for midline/soft-palate/tongue-base lesions). Depth of invasion and site predict occult nodal disease and the need for elective neck treatment.

📈 Prognosis (overview)

Prognosis depends on site, stage and HPV status: HPV+ oropharyngeal cancer has a markedly better prognosis than HPV− or oral-cavity SCC of the same stage. Detailed treatment and outcomes are in Topic 40.

🎯 Demonstrate it like an examiner wants

Commit first, then reveal the model structure and build the chain step by step.

Prompt: "A 50-year-old non-smoker has a painless cystic upper-neck lump; the tonsil looks slightly fuller on that side. How do you proceed?"

Your locked answer:
"A cystic neck node in an adult is metastatic HPV+ oropharyngeal SCC until excluded, not a branchial cyst."
HPV+ oropharyngeal SCC (tonsil/tongue base) typically presents with a small primary and a cystic level-II node in a younger non-smoker.
US-guided FNAC + p16 of the node (not open biopsy), nasendoscopy, EUA + biopsy of tonsil/tongue base ± tonsillectomy, MRI/CT and PET-CT for the primary and staging.
Confirm HPV/p16 status, stage with the HPV-specific TNM, and treat via the head-and-neck MDT (Topic 40); good prognosis if HPV+.
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: HPV-16 infects the tonsillar crypt epithelium, where viral oncoproteins (E6/E7) inactivate p53 and Rb, driving carcinoma with characteristic p16 over-expression. The crypt anatomy promotes early lymphatic spread, so a small primary metastasises to an upper-neck node that often becomes cystic. Because HPV+ tumours are more radioresponsive and occur in fitter patients, they carry a markedly better prognosis.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"A smoker with a painful indurated ulcer on the lateral tongue and a level-II node." → biopsy (SCC), MRI/CT, depth of invasion, US-FNAC node, panendoscopy, staging → MDT (Topic 40, usually surgery + reconstruction ± adjuvant).
"A 48-year-old with a cystic neck node and a fuller tonsil." → FNAC + p16, EUA/biopsy of tonsil/tongue base, imaging; HPV+ oropharyngeal SCC with favourable prognosis; MDT treatment.
Changing sexual behaviour has increased oral HPV-16 exposure, so HPV+ oropharyngeal SCC now exceeds tobacco-related cases in many countries. It affects younger, fitter, often non-smoking patients and has a better prognosis; HPV vaccination is expected to reduce it long-term.
With clinical fixation, OPG and CT (± MRI); bone invasion upstages the tumour (T4) and dictates marginal vs segmental mandibulectomy and reconstruction (e.g. fibula free flap).
They indicate aggressive behaviour and a higher risk of local recurrence and nodal spread, and are among the adverse features prompting adjuvant radiotherapy after surgery.
Leukoplakia, erythroplakia, oral submucous fibrosis (betel) and lichen planus (variants). Manage with biopsy, risk-factor cessation, excision of dysplastic lesions, and surveillance for malignant transformation.
