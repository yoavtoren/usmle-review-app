📖 Full topic explanation

This topic covers dysphonia (its organic, neurological and functional causes and the benign vocal-fold lesions), the red flag of persistent hoarseness, and the rehabilitation of voice after total laryngectomy. The recurring rule: hoarseness over 3 weeks needs the larynx looked at.

🗂️ Classifying dysphonia

Dysphonia is classified as , , or , all sitting beneath the overriding .

Organic: structural vocal-fold lesions — nodules, polyps, Reinke's oedema, cysts, papillomatosis, granuloma, and carcinoma — plus cord palsy. The voice change reflects altered mass/stiffness or closure of the cords.
Neurological: vocal-cord palsy (Topic 25), spasmodic dysphonia (focal laryngeal dystonia — strained/strangled or breathy breaks), Parkinson's (hypophonia), and tremor.
Functional/muscle-tension dysphonia: abnormal laryngeal muscle use without structural disease (often with stress/voice misuse); includes psychogenic aphonia (whispered voice but normal cough/cord closure on examination).
Red flag: hoarseness lasting >3 weeks, especially in a smoker/drinker or with a neck lump/dysphagia/otalgia, requires laryngoscopy to exclude laryngeal carcinoma before attributing it to a benign cause.

🎚️ Benign vocal-fold lesions

The common benign lesions are , , , and , each with a typical story.

Vocal nodules: bilateral, symmetrical, at the junction of the anterior and middle thirds (point of maximal contact), from chronic voice overuse/misuse ("singer's/screamer's nodules", children, teachers). Treated primarily with voice therapy.
Vocal polyp: usually unilateral, often haemorrhagic/pedunculated, from a single phonotrauma episode or vocal abuse; typically needs phonosurgical excision ± voice therapy.
Reinke's oedema (polypoid corditis): diffuse bilateral oedema of the superficial lamina propria (Reinke's space), strongly linked to smoking (and reflux/voice abuse), classically in middle-aged women, giving a low-pitched, gravelly voice. Stop smoking ± surgical reduction.
Recurrent respiratory papillomatosis: HPV 6 and 11 wart-like lesions; in children it can cause airway obstruction and recurs after removal. Managed with repeated debulking (microdebrider/laser), avoiding tracheostomy where possible; HPV vaccination is preventive.

🔎 Voice assessment

Assess with .

Assessment: history (onset, smoking/voice use, reflux, red flags), perceptual voice rating (GRBAS scale), flexible laryngoscopy and videostroboscopy (assesses the mucosal wave — distinguishes lesions and detects subtle disease), and voice-lab measures. Microlaryngoscopy + biopsy is definitive for suspicious lesions.

💊 Management of dysphonia

Treatment combines with and targeted measures.

Conservative: speech-and-language voice therapy (first-line for nodules and muscle-tension dysphonia), smoking cessation, treat reflux, and vocal hygiene. Many benign disorders resolve without surgery.
Procedures: microlaryngoscopy/phonosurgery for polyps, cysts, Reinke's and biopsy; medialisation/injection for cord palsy; botulinum toxin for spasmodic dysphonia; debulking for papillomatosis. Treat any carcinoma per its own pathway (Topics 43–44).

🗣️ Speech disorders (brief)

Beyond voice, speech disorders include .

Types: resonance (hypernasal speech from velopharyngeal insufficiency, e.g. cleft palate), articulation/dysarthria (neuromuscular), fluency (stammering/stuttering), and language/aphasia (cortical). The ENT-relevant ones are VPI (cleft, post-adenoidectomy) and laryngeal causes of voice.

🔁 Laryngectomy rehabilitation

After total laryngectomy the patient is a and needs plus .

Anatomy after laryngectomy: the airway and food passage are separated — the trachea is brought to a permanent end stoma, so the patient breathes through the neck (no airflow through the nose/mouth). This underlies the rehabilitation needs and the emergency point that you ventilate a laryngectomee via the stoma, not the mouth.
Voice restoration (3 options): (1) Tracheo-oesophageal puncture + voice prosthesis (Blom–Singer) — the gold standard: a one-way valve shunts pulmonary air into the oesophagus/pharynx to vibrate the mucosa for fluent voice (occlude the stoma to speak). (2) Oesophageal speech — swallow/inject air and eructate it (no device, but harder to learn). (3) Electrolarynx — a hand-held vibrator producing a robotic voice (useful early/as backup).
Other rehabilitation: stoma care; humidification + heat-moisture exchanger (the nose's warming/filtering/humidifying is lost → crusting, infection); swallowing rehabilitation; pulmonary care; and counselling about anosmia (no nasal airflow — use the "polite yawning"/nasal-airflow-inducing manoeuvre) and psychosocial support, all via the head-and-neck MDT.

📈 Prognosis

Benign dysphonias do well with voice therapy ± phonosurgery; the key safety net is excluding cancer in persistent hoarseness. After laryngectomy, most patients achieve functional voice (usually with a TEP voice prosthesis) and adapt to neck breathing with good MDT rehabilitation.

🎯 Demonstrate it like an examiner wants

Commit first, then reveal the model structure and build the chain step by step.

Prompt: "A patient 2 months after total laryngectomy wants to talk again. What options do you offer and what else does rehabilitation involve?"

Your locked answer:
"After total laryngectomy the airway is separated from the throat and the patient is a neck breather, so voice must be generated by vibrating the pharyngo-oesophageal mucosa."
(1) TEP + voice prosthesis (Blom–Singer) — gold standard; (2) oesophageal speech; (3) electrolarynx. Most achieve fluent voice with a TEP valve.
Stoma care, humidification + heat-moisture exchanger (lost nasal conditioning), swallowing rehab, pulmonary care, manage anosmia, and psychosocial support — via the MDT/SLT.
Educate that in an emergency they are ventilated/oxygenated through the stoma, not the mouth; provide stoma protection and a medical-alert. Long-term valve maintenance with SLT.
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: A tracheo-oesophageal puncture creates a controlled fistula holding a one-way valve between the trachea and the oesophagus. When the patient occludes the stoma and exhales, pulmonary air is diverted through the valve into the oesophagus, setting the pharyngo-oesophageal segment mucosa into vibration to produce sound. Articulation by the tongue and lips then shapes this into fluent speech.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"A teacher has months of hoarseness; laryngoscopy shows bilateral mid-cord swellings." → vocal nodules: voice therapy and vocal hygiene first; surgery only if they persist. Exclude red flags before labelling benign.
"A laryngectomee wants to speak." → offer TEP + voice prosthesis (best), with oesophageal speech and an electrolarynx as alternatives/backup; arrange SLT, stoma care, HME and counselling about anosmia and neck breathing.
A perceptual voice-rating scale: Grade (overall), Roughness, Breathiness, Asthenia, Strain, each scored 0–3. It standardises the clinical description of dysphonia and tracks change with treatment.
It visualises the mucosal wave of the vibrating cords, revealing subtle lesions, scar, or stiffness not seen on standard laryngoscopy, and helps distinguish nodules/polyps/cysts and detect early malignancy — guiding therapy vs surgery.
Valve leakage (through or around the prosthesis → aspiration), Candida colonisation shortening valve life, granulation, and enlargement of the puncture. Managed with regular SLT review, valve changes, antifungals and sizing adjustments.
Repeated conservative debulking (microdebrider/CO₂ laser) to maintain the airway and voice while avoiding tracheostomy (which can spread disease distally); adjuvants (e.g. cidofovir, bevacizumab) in severe cases. HPV vaccination is preventive.
