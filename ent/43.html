📖 Full topic explanation

Laryngeal cancer is almost always squamous cell carcinoma in a smoker/drinker. Its behaviour and prognosis hinge on the subsite: glottic tumours present early with hoarseness and do well (poor lymphatics), whereas supraglottic tumours present late with nodal spread. This topic is presentation/diagnosis; treatment is Topic 44.

🗂️ Histology & subsites

Over 95% are , classified by , which determines symptoms, spread and prognosis.

Histology: >95% SCC, arising from premalignant change (dysplasia/CIS — Topic 45); benign lesions include papilloma (HPV) and the vocal nodules/polyps of Topic 29. Risk factors are smoking and alcohol (synergistic).
Subsites: glottic (vocal cords — commonest), supraglottic (epiglottis, false cords, aryepiglottic folds), and subglottic (rare). The larynx's embryological "watershed" at the cords explains the different lymphatic drainage and behaviour.

🎙️ Glottic carcinoma

Glottic cancer presents and has a , with .

Early presentation: even a tiny cord tumour causes persistent hoarseness, so glottic cancer is often caught early — the reason hoarseness >3 weeks mandates laryngoscopy.
Good prognosis: the true cords have sparse lymphatic drainage, so nodal spread is uncommon in early disease and cure rates are high. This contrasts sharply with the node-rich supraglottis.
Staging milestones: impaired cord mobility (T2) and cord fixation (T3) reflect deep invasion; thyroid-cartilage invasion or extralaryngeal spread = T4. Mobility is assessed on laryngoscopy/stroboscopy and is central to TNM.

🔺 Supraglottic & subglottic carcinoma

; .

Supraglottic: a "silent" region with rich, often bilateral lymphatics, so tumours present late with neck nodes, throat pain, referred otalgia, a "hot-potato" voice, dysphagia and later airway compromise — and require treatment of both necks.
Subglottic: rare; presents late with stridor/airway obstruction and can spread to paratracheal/mediastinal nodes and the thyroid. Often advanced at diagnosis.

👤 Presentation & red flags

Core features are .

Presentation: hoarseness >3 weeks (glottic), a neck node (supraglottic), throat pain, referred otalgia, dysphagia, weight loss, haemoptysis, and stridor/airway obstruction in advanced disease. Any persistent hoarseness in a smoker → urgent laryngoscopy.

🔎 Diagnosis & staging

Assess with , plus and .

Endoscopy + biopsy: flexible laryngoscopy and videostroboscopy (lesion, cord mobility, mucosal wave) then microlaryngoscopy with biopsy (definitive) and panendoscopy for synchronous primaries. Assess the airway throughout.
Imaging: CT/MRI for deep extent, cartilage invasion, and nodal disease; ultrasound-guided FNAC of nodes; CT chest/PET-CT for distant disease/second primary.
Staging: TNM by subsite, with cord mobility (impaired/fixed) and cartilage invasion as key T descriptors. Staging drives the treatment choice (Topic 44).

📈 Prognosis (overview)

Early glottic cancer has excellent cure rates (early hoarseness + sparse lymphatics); supraglottic/subglottic and advanced cartilage-invading tumours do worse and need combined-modality treatment and neck management. Treatment is Topic 44.

Commit first, then reveal the model structure and build the chain step by step.

Prompt: "A 60-year-old smoker has 6 weeks of hoarseness. Outline your assessment."

Your locked answer:
"Persistent hoarseness >3 weeks in a smoker is a laryngeal carcinoma until excluded — it mandates laryngoscopy."
Most likely a glottic SCC; assess cord mobility (T2 impaired / T3 fixed) and look for cartilage invasion (T4) and nodes.
Flexible laryngoscopy + stroboscopy, microlaryngoscopy + biopsy (+ panendoscopy), CT/MRI (cartilage/extent/nodes), US-FNAC, CT chest/PET-CT; assign TNM.
Refer to the head-and-neck MDT for stage-directed treatment (Topic 44): early disease → laser surgery or radiotherapy; advanced → chemoRT or laryngectomy with neck treatment.
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: A squamous carcinoma on the vocal cord disrupts the smooth mucosal vibratory edge, so even a small lesion changes the voice early — explaining the favourable detection of glottic cancer. The true cords have sparse lymphatics, limiting early nodal spread, whereas the supraglottis drains richly and bilaterally, so its tumours metastasise to the neck before causing symptoms. Deep invasion fixes the cord and breaches the thyroid cartilage, defining higher T stages and shifting treatment toward surgery.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"A smoker with persistent hoarseness; a small lesion on the mobile mid-cord." → early glottic SCC: microlaryngoscopy + biopsy, imaging; likely T1 → single-modality treatment (laser or radiotherapy) with excellent cure (Topic 44).
"Throat pain, referred otalgia, a hot-potato voice and a level-II node." → supraglottic SCC: laryngoscopy + biopsy, CT/MRI, US-FNAC; treat the primary and both necks; airway vigilance.
It develops from the buccopharyngeal (midline) anlage with bilateral lymphatic drainage, so supraglottic tumours readily metastasise to both sides of the neck — hence bilateral neck treatment even in a clinically N0 neck.
