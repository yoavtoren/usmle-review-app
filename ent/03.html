👂🩹 Ear injuries

📖 Full topic explanation

Ear trauma runs from the auricle inward to the temporal bone. The structured approach is to work through each anatomical level — auricle, canal, drum, ossicles, inner ear, temporal bone — while never losing sight of the three emergencies: auricular haematoma, perilymph fistula, and a temporal-bone fracture with facial palsy or CSF leak.

🗂️ Classification

Classify by site (auricle, external canal, tympanic membrane, ossicles, labyrinth, temporal bone) and by mechanism: mechanical (blunt/sharp), , , thermal/caustic and . Stating this scheme first, then triaging for the emergencies, is what an examiner rewards.

Barotrauma: pressure injury from flying/diving when the Eustachian tube cannot equalise — affecting the middle ear (retraction, effusion, haemotympanum) or, dangerously, the inner ear (window rupture/perilymph fistula).
Acoustic/blast: energy trauma to the cochlea (4 kHz notch) and the pressure-sensitive tympanic membrane (the commonest blast lesion).
Iatrogenic/self-inflicted: cotton buds and syringing are leading causes of canal laceration and TM perforation; a bud pushed too far can dislocate the stapes and cause a perilymph fistula.

👂 Auricle & external canal

A is a time-critical injury that produces a "cauliflower ear" if not drained. Lacerations need ; complete avulsions may be re-attached as composite grafts or by microvascular replantation. Canal lacerations and burns risk ; thermal and caustic injuries threaten cartilage.

Auricular haematoma: blood between perichondrium and cartilage strips the cartilage of its only blood supply → avascular necrosis/fibrosis. Treat urgently with incision/aspiration plus a through-and-through bolster or pressure dressing to obliterate the dead space, with antibiotics; review at 24–48 h for re-accumulation.
Lacerations: irrigate, debride minimally and cover exposed cartilage with skin (cartilage has no independent blood supply); repair in layers with anti-pseudomonal antibiotic cover and tetanus prophylaxis. Avoid through-cartilage sutures.
Canal stenosis: circumferential canal laceration or burn heals with fibrosis that narrows the canal, trapping debris and causing conductive loss; debris removal, antibiotic-steroid drops and sometimes a stent prevent it.

Traumatic TM perforation: sudden pain, brief bleeding, hearing loss ± tinnitus; ~80–90% heal within weeks–3 months. Keep dry, analgesia, review; refer for myringoplasty if large, marginal, thermal (welding slag — heals poorly) or non-healing at 3 months.
Trap: with any perforation, do not use aminoglycoside drops (ototoxic) and do not irrigate; use quinolone drops only if infected. Most uncomplicated traumatic perforations need no drops at all.
Ossicular disruption: the incudostapedial joint is the weakest link; dislocation gives a persistent conductive loss (>30 dB, often flat) despite a healed drum. Confirm with audiometry/CT; treat with ossiculoplasty after the drum heals.

🌊 Barotrauma & inner-ear injury

follows flying/diving with a blocked tube; the danger is , while give a characteristic cochlear injury.

Middle-ear (otitic) barotrauma: failure to equalise on descent → negative pressure, mucosal haemorrhage, effusion or haemotympanum (Teed grading). Prevent with decongestants, Valsalva and not flying with a URTI; most settle, occasionally needing grommets.
Inner-ear barotrauma / perilymph fistula: a tear of the round or oval window leaks perilymph → sudden SNHL, vertigo and tinnitus after straining/diving/trauma, with a positive fistula test. Manage with bed rest head-up, avoid straining; surgical window repair if hearing falls or vertigo persists.
Acoustic & blast trauma: noise damages outer hair cells → a 4 kHz notch, initially a temporary then a permanent threshold shift. Blast most commonly ruptures the TM and can cause SNHL; assess hearing and the drum in any blast victim.

🧠 Temporal-bone fractures

Classified as , but the more predictive modern scheme is . Look for and assess the two key sequelae: and a .

Longitudinal (~80%): temporoparietal blow, parallel to the petrous axis → conductive loss (TM tear, haemotympanum, ossicular disruption), bleeding from the ear, VII palsy ~20% (often delayed/incomplete). Transverse (~20%): occipital blow, crosses the otic capsule → SNHL + vertigo, intact drum with haemotympanum, VII palsy ~50% (often immediate/complete).
Otic-capsule status predicts outcome better: capsule-violating fractures have far higher rates of facial palsy, profound SNHL, CSF leak and intracranial injury than capsule-sparing ones, regardless of the longitudinal/transverse label.
Signs: Battle's sign (mastoid bruising), raccoon eyes (periorbital bruising), haemotympanum, blood/CSF otorrhoea, a canal step/laceration. Document facial-nerve function and hearing immediately on arrival.
Facial palsy timing: immediate, complete palsy suggests transection/impingement → imaging + ENoG/EMG and surgical exploration/decompression (>90–95% degeneration favours surgery); delayed/incomplete palsy is from oedema → steroids + observation, with good recovery.
CSF leak: clear/bloody fluid with a double-ring (halo) sign; confirm with β-2 transferrin. Most close with bed rest/head elevation; the danger is ascending (pneumococcal) meningitis. Do not pack or irrigate the ear; a leak persisting >7–10 days needs repair.

🔎 Assessment & investigation

After ATLS and exclusion of major head/cervical-spine injury, perform a focused ear assessment: otoscopy (canal laceration, drum, haemotympanum), facial-nerve grading (House–Brackmann), tuning forks/audiometry, fistula test, and look for a CSF leak. Imaging is HRCT temporal bone; MRI/angiography for vascular or intracranial concerns. ENoG/EMG guides facial-nerve decisions.

💊 Management principles

Keep the injured ear ; treat perforations expectantly and ossicular/conductive lesions electively. Drain auricular haematomas urgently. Manage facial palsy by timing (explore immediate-complete, steroids for delayed). Repair persistent CSF leaks and perilymph fistulas; give analgesia, tetanus prophylaxis and vaccination as indicated.

Why dry & unpacked: packing or irrigating an ear with a CSF leak or perforation risks driving infection intracranially. Water precautions protect a healing perforation. Most decisions follow from "is there a leak, a palsy, or a fistula?"

📈 Prognosis

Most traumatic perforations heal and most delayed facial palsies recover fully; conductive losses are correctable by ossiculoplasty. Profound SNHL from otic-capsule-violating fractures and complete immediate transections carry the worst outcomes, and CSF leaks must be followed for meningitis risk.

🎯 Demonstrate it like an examiner wants

Commit first, then reveal the model structure and build the chain step by step.

Prompt: "A motorcyclist has bleeding from the right ear, Battle's sign and a facial droop. Walk me through it."

Your locked answer:
"This is a temporal-bone fracture. After ABCDE/ATLS and excluding life-threatening head injury, my ear priorities are documenting facial-nerve function and hearing now, and assessing for a CSF leak."
Bleeding ear + Battle's sign + conductive features favour a longitudinal, otic-capsule-sparing fracture. Define longitudinal vs transverse and the more predictive capsule-sparing vs -violating system.
Grade the palsy (House–Brackmann) and note onset/completeness; tuning forks/audiogram; check for halo sign/β-2 transferrin; HRCT temporal bone. If complete immediate palsy → ENoG/EMG for degeneration.
Keep the ear dry, no packing/irrigation. CSF leak → head-up, observe, repair if persistent. Immediate complete palsy with >90–95% ENoG degeneration → surgical decompression; delayed/partial → steroids + observe. Treat conductive loss/ossicular disruption electively. Vaccinate against pneumococcus.
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: A temporoparietal impact propagates a fracture parallel to the petrous ridge, tearing the drum and disrupting the ossicular chain to give a conductive loss. Shearing or bony impingement of the facial nerve (often at the perigeniculate region) produces palsy, immediate if transected, delayed if from oedema. A dural tear over the tegmen or through the fracture line lets CSF escape into the middle ear and out of the canal, creating a meningitis risk until it seals.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"After ATLS, this stable patient has bleeding from the ear, Battle's sign and a partial, delayed facial weakness." → likely longitudinal capsule-sparing fracture: document VII (House–Brackmann) & hearing, exclude CSF leak (halo/β-2 transferrin), HRCT; steroids + observe the delayed palsy; keep ear dry; treat conductive loss electively.
"A scuba diver has sudden roaring tinnitus, vertigo and one-sided deafness after a forceful Valsalva." → perilymph fistula: bed rest head-up, no straining, serial audiograms; positive fistula test supports it; tympanotomy and window sealing if hearing falls or vertigo persists.
Electroneuronography compares the injured to the normal side. >90–95% degeneration within 2 weeks of a complete palsy predicts poor spontaneous recovery and favours surgical decompression; below that, conservative treatment is preferred. EMG voluntary motor units indicate intact axons and argue against surgery.
Wrap the part in saline gauze, place in a sealed bag on ice (not directly on ice). Options: microvascular replantation (best if feasible), composite re-attachment, banking cartilage under postauricular skin, or delayed reconstruction. Antibiotics with anti-pseudomonal cover; tetanus prophylaxis.
A bud pushed through the drum can dislocate the stapes into the vestibule → perilymph fistula / labyrinthine injury with vertigo and SNHL. This is a surgical emergency for exploration; never irrigate. Distinguish from a simple healing perforation (no vertigo, conductive only).
Real fractures are usually oblique/mixed, so the old axis-based labels are unreliable. Whether the fracture violates the otic capsule correlates far better with clinically important outcomes — SNHL, facial palsy, CSF leak and intracranial complications — and so guides prognosis and intervention.
House–Brackmann (facial-nerve function I–VI); Teed/O'Neill grading of middle-ear barotrauma; ENoG percentage degeneration for VII; and audiometric air–bone gap for conductive injury. Quoting a grading scale signals depth.
