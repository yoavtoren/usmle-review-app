📖 Full topic explanation

Facial trauma runs from the simple (and commonest) nasal fracture to complex midface and orbital fractures. The exam wants the emergencies — septal haematoma, CSF leak and orbital entrapment — and the classifications (Le Fort, blowout, ZMC, NOE), all on a foundation of ATLS.

👃 Nasal fractures

The nasal bone is the , best assessed and reduced with attention to timing; two things must never be missed — a and a .

Nasal fracture: assess for deformity, epistaxis and airway; re-examine at 5–7 days once swelling settles to judge deformity, and perform closed reduction within ~2–3 weeks (before the bones set). X-rays are unhelpful for management; it is a clinical diagnosis.
Septal haematoma (emergency): a boggy, bluish swelling of the septum that occludes the airway. Blood between the perichondrium and septal cartilage strips its blood supply → cartilage necrosis, abscess and a saddle-nose deformity. Incise and drain urgently, pack/quilt and give antibiotics. Always look for it after nasal trauma.
CSF rhinorrhoea: clear watery discharge (positive halo/double-ring sign, β-2 transferrin) signals an anterior skull-base/cribriform-plate fracture with meningitis risk. Manage conservatively initially (head-up, avoid straining/nose-blowing), repair if persistent, and vaccinate.

🦴 Midface fractures (Le Fort)

Midface fractures follow the , all crossing the .

Le Fort I–III: I = horizontal (floating palate, maxilla separated above the teeth); II = pyramidal (through the maxilla, infraorbital rims and nasal bridge — the central face moves); III = craniofacial dysjunction (separation of the facial skeleton from the skull base, through the orbits and zygomaticofrontal sutures). They are often mixed/bilateral.
Common factor: all Le Fort fractures involve the pterygoid plates. Clinically, test mobility of the maxilla/palate; high-energy patterns (II/III) carry airway, CSF-leak and ocular risks and need CT and maxillofacial fixation.

👁️ Orbital & zygomatic fractures

Key patterns are the , the , and the .

Orbital blowout (floor) fracture: a blow to the globe fractures the thin orbital floor into the maxillary sinus. Features: diplopia (especially on upgaze) from inferior-rectus/fat entrapment, enophthalmos, infraorbital nerve numbness, and a "tear-drop" of orbital contents in the maxillary sinus on CT. Entrapment with a non-distractable globe ("white-eyed blowout" in children) + nausea/bradycardia is an urgent surgical release.
ZMC ("tripod") fracture: the zygoma separates at its articulations → cheek/malar flattening, a palpable infraorbital/zygomatic step, trismus (impingement on the coronoid), infraorbital nerve numbness, and lateral subconjunctival haemorrhage. CT confirms; fixation if displaced.
NOE fracture: central-face injury disrupting the medial canthal attachments → traumatic telecanthus (widened intercanthal distance), a flattened nasal bridge, and frequent CSF leak. Needs careful CT assessment and reconstruction with canthal reattachment.

Frontal-sinus fractures: an anterior-table fracture is mainly cosmetic; a posterior-table fracture risks dural tear/CSF leak and intracranial injury, and damage to the frontonasal outflow risks later mucocele. Management ranges from observation to cranialisation depending on displacement/leak.
ATLS + focused exam: facial fractures can threaten the airway (bleeding, displaced midface) and accompany head/cervical-spine injury. Assess airway, C-spine, and the eye (acuity, diplopia, pupils, entrapment), occlusion, facial sensation and CSF leak; image with CT facial bones.

💊 Management principles

Prioritise the , drain a septal haematoma, then .

Emergencies first: secure the airway (severe midface bleeding/displacement); urgent ophthalmology for entrapment, retrobulbar haematoma (an orbital compartment emergency → lateral canthotomy) or visual loss; drain a septal haematoma the same day.
Definitive: closed reduction of simple nasal fractures (≤2–3 weeks); open reduction and internal fixation (ORIF) of displaced midface/ZMC/orbital/NOE fractures; orbital floor repair for entrapment/significant enophthalmos; manage CSF leaks and frontal-sinus injuries per pattern. Restore occlusion and facial projection.

📈 Prognosis

Most nasal fractures do well with timely reduction; missing a septal haematoma causes a permanent saddle deformity. Complex fractures have good outcomes with accurate CT-guided fixation, but carry risks to vision, the infraorbital nerve, occlusion and CSF integrity.

🎯 Demonstrate it like an examiner wants

Commit first, then reveal the model structure and build the chain step by step.

Prompt: "A young man is punched in the nose; it is swollen and bleeding. What do you assess and do?"

Your locked answer:
"After ATLS, my nasal priorities are to exclude a septal haematoma and a CSF leak — these change management immediately."
Control epistaxis; inspect the septum for a boggy swelling (haematoma); check for clear rhinorrhoea (CSF); assess deformity, airway and the eyes.
Septal haematoma → incise and drain today + antibiotics; CSF leak → conservative + β-2 transferrin/imaging; otherwise review at 5–7 days to assess deformity.
If deformed, perform closed reduction within ~2–3 weeks; counsel that residual deformity may later need septorhinoplasty. No routine X-rays.
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: Blunt force fractures the nasal bones and can shear the septal mucoperichondrium from the cartilage, allowing blood to collect in the subperichondrial space. Because the cartilage depends on the perichondrium for its blood supply, an undrained haematoma causes ischaemic necrosis, infection and collapse (saddle nose). Prompt drainage reapposes the perichondrium to the cartilage and preserves the nasal framework.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"A boxer has a swollen nose and a soft bilateral septal swelling occluding the airway." → septal haematoma: incise and drain today, pack/quilt, antibiotics; then reassess for nasal-bone reduction at 5–7 days.
"A blow to the eye causes diplopia on looking up, a numb cheek, and a tear-drop in the maxillary sinus on CT." → orbital floor blowout: ophthalmology assessment; if entrapment (esp. white-eyed/paediatric) → urgent surgical release; otherwise repair for persistent diplopia/enophthalmos.
Airway compromise, retrobulbar haematoma (→ canthotomy), muscle entrapment (white-eyed blowout), septal haematoma, and major haemorrhage/CSF leak. These are treated immediately, ahead of definitive fracture fixation.
Confirm with β-2 transferrin (and the halo sign), localise with HRCT ± cisternography. Most close conservatively (head-up, avoid straining/nose-blowing); persistent leaks need endoscopic repair; vaccinate against pneumococcus.
The infraorbital nerve (V2) runs in the orbital floor/anterior maxilla, so blowout, ZMC and Le Fort II fractures commonly cause cheek/upper-lip numbness — a useful localising sign.
Obstruction of the frontonasal drainage pathway can cause a delayed mucocele (or mucopyocele) years later, with expansion and orbital displacement — a reason to address outflow and to follow up frontal-sinus fractures.
