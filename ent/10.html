👂🦻 Otosclerosis

📖 Full topic explanation

Otosclerosis is a primary disease of the otic-capsule bone in which abnormal remodelling fixes the stapes footplate, producing a progressive conductive hearing loss with a normal drum. The classic patient is a young white woman with a family history whose hearing worsens in pregnancy.

🧬 Definition & pathology

Otosclerosis is focal disordered bone remodelling of the , passing from an active vascular phase to a dense sclerotic phase that . When foci reach the cochlear endosteum it becomes .

Site: the commonest focus is the fissula ante fenestram, just anterior to the oval window — explaining why the stapes footplate is the structure that fixes. The otic capsule is normally the most stable bone in the body, which is why this localised remodelling is pathological.
Phases: the otospongiotic (active) phase shows vascular, lytic, immature bone; it later matures into dense sclerotic bone. Activity correlates with the Schwartze sign and CT lucency, and is the rationale (limited) for medical therapy.
Footplate fixation: new bone anchors the footplate in the oval window, abolishing its piston movement → a progressive conductive loss with a normal, mobile drum and an intact ossicular chain otherwise.
Cochlear otosclerosis: foci involving the cochlear endosteum release toxic enzymes/inflammatory mediators into the inner ear → sensorineural loss, giving an overall mixed picture in advanced disease.

📊 Epidemiology & genetics

It typically presents in , and .

Demographics: onset typically 20–40 years; female:male ≈ 2:1; commonest in white populations; ~50% have a family history with autosomal-dominant inheritance and incomplete penetrance (~40%). A measles-virus association has been proposed.
Pregnancy: hearing often deteriorates during pregnancy and with oestrogen exposure, suggesting a hormonal influence on disease activity — a classic history point.

👂 Clinical features

The hallmark is , often with and tinnitus; occasionally a is visible.

Core picture: slowly progressive conductive loss, usually bilateral and asymmetric, in a young adult with an entirely normal tympanic membrane and ear canal. The normal drum with a conductive loss is what points away from middle-ear infection.
Paracusis Willisii: the patient hears better in noisy surroundings, because others raise their voices while the patient's conductive loss filters out background noise. A near-pathognomonic symptom.
Schwartze sign: a reddish hue over the promontory seen through the drum, reflecting the hypervascular active otospongiotic phase. Present in a minority but a useful confirmatory clue.

🔎 Investigation

Tuning forks and audiometry show a conductive loss with a and ; demonstrates otospongiotic foci.

Carhart notch: an artefactual dip in bone-conduction thresholds maximal at 2 kHz caused by loss of the ossicular resonance, not true cochlear loss; it typically improves after successful stapes surgery. Audiometry shows a conductive (later mixed) loss with an air–bone gap.
Stapedial reflexes: absent (or biphasic on/off) reflexes reflect footplate fixation and are an early objective sign. Tuning forks give a Rinne negative result bilaterally with Weber lateralising to the worse (more conductive) ear.
HRCT: shows lucent otospongiotic foci at the fissula ante fenestram and (in cochlear disease) a "double-ring/halo" around the cochlea. CT confirms the diagnosis, excludes other causes, and plans surgery.

💊 Management

Options are ; the definitive operation is a . , and a is used for far-advanced disease.

Conservative options: a well-fitted hearing aid reliably rehabilitates the conductive loss and is a valid alternative to surgery, especially in mild disease, bilateral cochlear involvement, an only-hearing ear, or patient preference.
Stapes surgery: stapedotomy (small fenestra in the footplate + a piston prosthesis from incus to vestibule) — now preferred over total stapedectomy — bypasses the fixation and closes the air–bone gap in ~90%+. Operate the worse-hearing ear; never both at once.
Medical therapy (limited): sodium fluoride (± vitamin D/calcium) and bisphosphonates aim to stabilise active otospongiotic foci, with weak evidence; they may slow cochlear progression but do not reverse fixation.
Far-advanced otosclerosis: when SNHL is profound, a cochlear implant is used (with care, as the otosclerotic cochlea can cause facial-nerve stimulation and is harder to drill).

⚠️ Complications of stapes surgery

The feared outcomes are a .

Risks: sensorineural loss / "dead ear" (~1%), vertigo, a perilymph fistula/floating footplate, dysgeusia from chorda tympani injury, facial-nerve injury, tinnitus, and prosthesis displacement/erosion (causing recurrent conductive loss). These are the core of consent and why only one ear is operated.

📈 Prognosis

Stapes surgery closes the air–bone gap in most and is durable; the conductive component is highly treatable. Cochlear otosclerosis may progress despite surgery and is the main long-term concern, ultimately rehabilitated with amplification or implantation.

🎯 Demonstrate it like an examiner wants

Commit first, then reveal the model structure and build the chain step by step.

Prompt: "A 32-year-old woman has bilateral progressive hearing loss, normal drums, and hears better in noisy rooms. Her mother is similar. Diagnose and manage."

Your locked answer:
"Progressive conductive loss with normal drums, paracusis Willisii and a family history is otosclerosis — stapes-footplate fixation — until proven otherwise."
Define otic-capsule remodelling at the fissula ante fenestram → footplate fixation. Confirm with tuning forks (Rinne −), audiometry (conductive loss + Carhart notch), absent stapedial reflexes, and HRCT.
Three options: observe, hearing aid, or stapes surgery. Discuss CT findings, the worse ear first, and that medical therapy (fluoride) is of limited value.
Stapedotomy + piston prosthesis on the worse ear closes the air–bone gap in ~90%+. Consent for ~1% dead ear, vertigo, dysgeusia and facial-nerve risk; a hearing aid is the safe alternative.
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: Disordered otic-capsule remodelling lays down bone at the fissula ante fenestram that fixes the stapes footplate in the oval window. The fixed footplate can no longer transmit the ossicular piston motion to the cochlear fluid, producing a progressive conductive loss. Stapedotomy bypasses the fixation with a prosthesis from the incus through a footplate fenestra into the vestibule, restoring sound transmission.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"A 30-year-old woman has slowly worsening bilateral hearing, normal drums, paracusis Willisii, Rinne negative, a conductive loss with a Carhart notch and absent reflexes." → otosclerosis; HRCT; offer hearing aid vs stapedotomy (worse ear), with consent for the surgical risks.
"Good initial result, then the gap returns." → suspect prosthesis displacement, incus erosion, or progressive footplate disease (or a perilymph fistula if vertigo). Re-image and consider revision stapes surgery.
SSCD creates a "third window" giving a low-frequency air–bone gap that looks conductive. Unlike otosclerosis, reflexes are present (often low-threshold), with autophony, the Tullio phenomenon, and a CT dehiscence of the superior canal. Operating on an SSCD as if it were otosclerosis is a classic error.
Weak. Sodium fluoride (± vitamin D/calcium) and bisphosphonates aim to stabilise active otospongiotic foci and may slow cochlear progression, but they do not reverse footplate fixation; surgery or amplification remains definitive.
Because stapes surgery carries a ~1% risk of a dead ear. Operating the worse ear preserves the better ear as a safety net; the second ear is considered only after a good, stable result on the first.
When loss is profound and mixed/sensorineural, cochlear implantation is indicated. The surgeon must anticipate a harder-to-drill cochlea, possible facial-nerve stimulation, and use careful electrode selection; outcomes are generally good.
