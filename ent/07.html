👂🔧 Surgical treatment of chronic otitis

The Overview is a full walk-through of the topic. Tap any coloured word for an extra exam pearl. Then work through all 5 tabs.

📖 Full topic explanation

Surgery for chronic otitis follows two non-negotiable goals, in order: first eradicate disease to create a safe, dry, self-cleaning ear, then reconstruct hearing. The operation chosen depends on whether the disease is mucosal or cholesteatomatous and on how far it has spread into the mastoid.

🎯 Aims & decision framework

The cardinal principle is that . The choice of operation is driven by , the state of the , and the status of the .

Safety first: in cholesteatoma you must remove all matrix even at the cost of hearing, because residual disease leads to recurrence and intracranial complications. Hearing reconstruction is done at the same sitting or staged, but never by leaving disease behind.
Type/extent: mucosal disease (central perforation) → myringoplasty/tympanoplasty ± limited mastoidectomy; cholesteatoma → mastoidectomy (canal-wall-up or -down) + tympanoplasty. Extent on CT (attic, antrum, mastoid tip, fistula, tegmen) dictates the approach.
Ossicles & tube: ossicular erosion (often the long process of incus) needs ossiculoplasty; poor Eustachian-tube function predicts graft failure and recurrent retraction, and may favour a canal-wall-down cavity.
The other ear: if the contralateral ear is poor or this is an only-hearing ear, you favour the most conservative, lowest-risk option and counsel carefully about the small risk of a "dead ear".

🪟 Myringoplasty & tympanoplasty

repairs the drum alone; repairs the drum and reconstructs the sound-conducting mechanism, classified by . Grafts are usually .

Myringoplasty (= type I tympanoplasty): closure of a perforation with an intact, mobile ossicular chain. Best done on a dry ear; success ~85–90%. It stops recurrent infection (water can no longer enter) and improves the conductive loss.
Tympanoplasty: graft of the drum plus reconstruction of the middle-ear transformer mechanism (ossiculoplasty) when the chain is eroded or removed for disease. Combines disease control with hearing restoration.
Wullstein types: I = graft on an intact chain; II = graft onto incus (eroded malleus); III = graft onto stapes head ("myringostapediopexy"); IV = graft onto a mobile footplate (round-window baffle); V = fenestration of a fixed footplate. Modern reconstruction uses prostheses (PORP/TORP) rather than the historical types IV–V.
Graft materials: temporalis fascia (classic), tragal/conchal cartilage with perichondrium (stiffer, preferred for revision, atelectasis, anterior or subtotal perforations and poor tubal function), and perichondrium. Placed by underlay (medial to the remnant, commonest) or overlay technique.

⛓️ Ossiculoplasty

When the chain is disrupted, hearing is restored with or a sculpted autograft.

PORP vs TORP: a PORP bridges the drum/malleus to the stapes head (superstructure present); a TORP bridges to the stapes footplate (superstructure absent). Materials include titanium, hydroxyapatite or a remodelled autograft incus; a cartilage cap protects the drum from extrusion.

🛠️ Mastoidectomy — the core operations

clears the air cells with an intact canal wall; for cholesteatoma the decision is , and historical procedures still describe cavity types. The dangerous structures are .

Cortical (Schwartze) mastoidectomy: exenteration of mastoid air cells preserving the posterior canal wall and middle ear; used for acute coalescent mastoiditis/subperiosteal abscess and as the first step of combined-approach tympanoplasty. Landmarks: MacEwen's triangle, the antrum and the lateral semicircular canal.
Canal-wall-up (combined approach tympanoplasty): removes disease while preserving the posterior canal wall and normal anatomy (no open cavity), but has higher residual/recurrent cholesteatoma, so it needs a planned second-look or DWI-MRI surveillance. Canal-wall-down: takes down the posterior wall to exteriorise the disease into an open cavity — lower recurrence and direct surveillance, but a cavity needing lifelong care and water precautions.
Radical vs modified radical: a radical mastoidectomy removes the middle-ear mucosa, ossicles (except stapes) and drum, leaving an open cavity with no hearing reconstruction (rarely done now). A modified radical (Bondy) exteriorises an attic cholesteatoma while preserving a functioning drum/ossicles and hearing.
Structures at risk: the facial nerve (tympanic and mastoid segments — facial monitoring is used), the dura/tegmen (CSF leak), the sigmoid sinus (bleeding), the lateral semicircular canal (fistula → dead ear/vertigo), and the chorda tympani (taste). Knowing these is the basis of consent.

📋 Pre-operative assessment & consent

Work-up includes , with documented consent for the .

Work-up: pure-tone audiometry (type/degree of loss, bone-conduction reserve), HRCT temporal bone for disease extent and anatomy, microbiology and aural toilet to settle infection, and assessment of the other ear. DWI-MRI helps plan revision/residual cases.
Consent risks: failure/recurrence, persistent or worsened hearing, sensorineural loss or a "dead ear", facial-nerve palsy, vertigo, altered taste (chorda tympani), tinnitus, CSF leak, and (for open cavities) the need for regular cleaning. These map directly onto the structures at risk.

⚠️ Complications & follow-up

Surgery can injure the structures above; long-term, drive follow-up.

Follow-up: residual (left-behind matrix) and recurrent (new retraction) cholesteatoma are common after canal-wall-up surgery → second-look or DWI-MRI. Open cavities can develop recurrent discharge, debris accumulation, or a small cavity that won't dry, sometimes needing obliteration or revision.

📈 Outcomes

Myringoplasty closes ~85–90% of perforations with hearing improvement; tympanoplasty/ossiculoplasty outcomes depend on tubal function and ossicular status; cholesteatoma surgery aims first for a safe dry ear, accepting that hearing gains are variable and recurrence requires lifelong surveillance.

🧠 Thinking questions (tap to reveal)

(1) Eradicate disease → a safe, dry ear (always first); (2) reconstruct hearing. In cholesteatoma you never leave matrix to preserve hearing — recurrence and intracranial complications are the price.
CWU for limited disease, good tubal function, reliable follow-up (preserves anatomy but higher recurrence → second-look/DWI). CWD for extensive disease, a labyrinthine fistula, an only-hearing ear, or poor follow-up (lower recurrence, open cavity needing care).
PORP (partial) connects the drum/malleus to the stapes head when the superstructure is present; TORP (total) connects to the footplate when the superstructure is absent. A cartilage cap reduces extrusion.
Facial nerve (palsy), lateral semicircular canal (fistula/dead ear, vertigo), sigmoid sinus (bleeding), dura/tegmen (CSF leak), and chorda tympani (taste). These form the core of consent and the basis for facial-nerve monitoring.
Failure is driven by poor Eustachian-tube function, active infection, large/anterior perforations and revision surgery. Mitigate with a dry ear pre-op, cartilage grafting, treating nasal/sinus disease, and good surgical technique.
Exteriorisation of an attic/epitympanic cholesteatoma into an open cavity while preserving a functioning drum and ossicular chain — disease control with hearing preservation, suitable when middle-ear function is intact.

🎯 Demonstrate it like an examiner wants

Commit first, then reveal the model structure and build the chain step by step.

Prompt: "A patient has an attic cholesteatoma eroding the ossicles, confined to the epitympanum and antrum. How would you plan surgery?"

Your locked answer:
"My aims are first to eradicate the cholesteatoma for a safe dry ear, then to reconstruct hearing. Surgery is mandatory — medical treatment cannot cure cholesteatoma."
Audiometry, HRCT for extent/anatomy (fistula, tegmen, facial-nerve course), DWI-MRI if needed, control infection, assess the other ear; consent for facial nerve/SNHL/taste/recurrence.
Disease in epitympanum + antrum with reliable follow-up favours an atticotomy/canal-wall-up mastoidectomy; extensive disease, a fistula, or poor follow-up favours canal-wall-down. Plan a second-look or DWI-MRI if CWU.
Remove all matrix; reconstruct the drum (cartilage graft) and ossicular chain (PORP/TORP). Aural care, audiometry, and surveillance for residual/recurrent disease long-term.
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: Cholesteatoma matrix secretes osteolytic enzymes and activates osteoclasts, eroding the ossicles and threatening the labyrinth, facial canal and tegmen. Surgery physically removes the keratinising epithelium to halt this destruction and create a self-cleaning ear. The conductive defect from ossicular erosion is then bridged with a prosthesis, restoring the middle-ear transformer.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"A young adult has a dry central perforation with a 25 dB conductive loss and an intact chain." → myringoplasty (underlay temporalis fascia/cartilage) once the ear is dry, after audiometry and treating any nasal disease; counsel ~85–90% closure with hearing improvement.
"Cholesteatoma fills the mastoid with a positive fistula sign and an only-hearing ear." → favour canal-wall-down (lower recurrence, direct surveillance, safer with a fistula and an only-hearing ear); remove matrix carefully over the fistula last; reconstruct hearing cautiously.
Be conservative: weigh the small but real risk of a dead ear, consider canal-wall-down for safety, stage hearing reconstruction, and counsel explicitly. Some surgeons defer surgery and manage medically if the ear is dry and stable.
Transcanal endoscopic ear surgery gives a wide-angle view of hidden recesses (sinus tympani, facial recess, attic), allowing more disease to be removed through the canal with less mastoid drilling and potentially lower residual rates for limited cholesteatoma.
A discharging or debris-prone cavity is managed with regular microsuction, water precautions and topical therapy; persistent problems may need revision with cavity obliteration (fat, cartilage, bone pâté or flaps) and a meatoplasty to improve self-cleaning and aeration.
Staged surgery (planned second-look at 6–12 months) is used after canal-wall-up procedures to check for residual cholesteatoma and to reconstruct hearing in a disease-free ear; DWI-MRI increasingly replaces a routine second look.
