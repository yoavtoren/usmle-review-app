🩸👃 Nasopharyngeal angiofibroma

📖 Full topic explanation

Juvenile nasopharyngeal angiofibroma (JNA) is a benign but locally aggressive, highly vascular tumour occurring almost exclusively in adolescent males. The exam-defining rule is absolute: recurrent unilateral epistaxis + nasal obstruction in a teenage boy = JNA, and you NEVER biopsy it — diagnose and plan on imaging.

🗂️ Definition & origin

JNA is a arising at the , in a characteristic .

Nature: histologically benign but locally invasive and very vascular (fibrovascular stroma with thin-walled vessels lacking smooth muscle → it does not contract, hence torrential bleeding). It expands and erodes bone and can extend intracranially, but does not metastasise.
Origin/spread: arises near the sphenopalatine foramen / pterygopalatine fossa and spreads into the nasopharynx, nasal cavity, pterygopalatine and infratemporal fossae, orbit and (via the sphenoid/skull base) intracranially.
Demographics: almost exclusively adolescent males (testosterone-dependent), hence "juvenile". This age/sex profile, with the symptom complex, makes the diagnosis on history alone.

👤 Presentation

The hallmark is , with later .

Classic presentation: a teenage boy with recurrent, often severe, unilateral epistaxis and progressive unilateral nasal obstruction (± a nasopharyngeal mass). This triad is the single most testable fact of the topic.
Later features: as it enlarges — facial/cheek swelling, proptosis/diplopia (orbital extension), a conductive hearing loss (Eustachian-tube obstruction), anosmia, and (with intracranial extension) cranial-nerve involvement.

🔴 The cardinal rule — DO NOT biopsy

JNA must be diagnosed on imaging because .

No biopsy: the tumour's thin-walled non-contractile vessels mean a biopsy can cause life-threatening bleeding. Diagnosis is radiological; a "nasal polyp" in a teenage boy must not be biopsied/removed in clinic without first excluding JNA on imaging.

🔎 Imaging & staging

Diagnosis uses with characteristic signs, plus ; extent is .

CT/MRI: a strongly enhancing nasopharyngeal/pterygopalatine mass; anterior bowing of the posterior maxillary sinus wall (Holman–Miller sign) on CT; MRI shows extent and a "salt-and-pepper" appearance (flow voids) and intracranial/orbital spread.
Angiography: shows the feeding vessels (usually the internal maxillary artery / external carotid); it is both diagnostic and therapeutic, enabling pre-operative embolisation to reduce intra-operative blood loss.
Staging: Radkowski or Fisch systems grade extent (nasal/nasopharyngeal → pterygopalatine/infratemporal fossa/orbit → intracranial), guiding the surgical approach and prognosis.

💊 Treatment

Treatment is , with and a recognised .

Surgery: complete excision following pre-operative embolisation — endoscopic for limited disease, open (e.g. transpalatal, midfacial degloving, lateral rhinotomy, or skull-base approaches) for extensive tumours.
Advanced/unresectable: radiotherapy for intracranial/unresectable disease; hormonal therapy has been tried (testosterone-dependence). Skull-base MDT involvement for intracranial extension.
Recurrence: incomplete excision (especially at the pterygopalatine/sphenoid base) leads to recurrence, so complete removal and post-operative surveillance imaging are important; many tumours stabilise/regress after adolescence.

📈 Prognosis

As a benign tumour, the prognosis is good with complete excision; the challenges are haemorrhage control (embolisation), complete removal at the skull base, and recurrence. Intracranial extension increases complexity and may need radiotherapy.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"A 15-year-old boy with recurrent heavy unilateral nosebleeds, a blocked nose and a nasopharyngeal mass." → JNA: no biopsy; contrast CT (Holman–Miller) + MRI + angiography; stage; embolisation + complete excision; surveillance.
"A JNA with orbital and intracranial extension and proptosis." → skull-base MDT: embolisation + open/skull-base resection (± endoscopic), with radiotherapy for unresectable intracranial disease; careful reconstruction and follow-up.
To occlude the tumour's feeding vessels (internal maxillary artery) ~24–48 h before surgery, dramatically reducing intra-operative blood loss and improving the safety and completeness of excision of this highly vascular tumour.
At the basisphenoid/pterygopalatine origin and skull base, where complete removal is hardest. Residual disease at these sites drives recurrence, so meticulous excision of the attachment and post-operative surveillance imaging are important.
Because JNA is testosterone-dependent, hormonal manipulation (e.g. anti-androgens) has been explored to shrink tumours, and many lesions stabilise or regress after adolescence; however, surgery (after embolisation) remains the mainstay, with radiotherapy for unresectable disease.
Stop — image first to exclude JNA. A unilateral nasopharyngeal/nasal mass in an adolescent male must be assessed with contrast imaging before any biopsy or removal, because an unrecognised angiofibroma can bleed catastrophically.
