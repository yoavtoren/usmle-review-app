🦋 Thyroid gland tumors

The Overview is a full walk-through of the topic. Tap any coloured word for an extra exam pearl. Then work through all 5 tabs.

📖 Full topic explanation

A thyroid nodule is common and usually benign; the task is to identify the few that are malignant and classify the cancers — papillary, follicular, medullary, anaplastic and lymphoma — each with distinct behaviour. The work-up centres on ultrasound + FNAC, and red flags (hoarseness, fixation, rapid growth) point to malignancy.

🗂️ The solitary thyroid nodule

Most nodules are ; assessment targets the .

Benign causes: colloid nodule, follicular adenoma, cyst, and dominant nodules in a multinodular goitre. The great majority of nodules are benign, so over-investigation is avoided by structured (US/FNAC) triage.
Red flags for malignancy: a hard, fixed, rapidly growing nodule, hoarseness (recurrent laryngeal nerve involvement), cervical lymphadenopathy, age (very young/old), male sex, prior neck irradiation, and a family history (MEN2). These prompt urgent assessment.

🧬 Differentiated carcinomas (papillary & follicular)

The commonest cancers are the differentiated types: and .

Papillary carcinoma: the commonest thyroid cancer; lymphatic spread to neck nodes; excellent prognosis. Histology: "Orphan-Annie" empty nuclei, nuclear grooves and psammoma bodies; associated with BRAF/RET and prior radiation. FNAC is diagnostic.
Follicular carcinoma: haematogenous spread (bone/lung); FNAC cannot distinguish it from a follicular adenoma (diagnosis needs histological evidence of capsular/vascular invasion), so a "follicular lesion" is managed by diagnostic lobectomy. Good prognosis overall.

🧪 Medullary, anaplastic & lymphoma

The other cancers are , , and .

Medullary carcinoma: arises from parafollicular C cells → secretes calcitonin (tumour marker), with amyloid stroma. ~25% are familial (MEN2/RET mutation) — screen for phaeochromocytoma before surgery and for hyperparathyroidism. Spreads to nodes; needs total thyroidectomy + central neck dissection.
Anaplastic carcinoma: elderly patients, a rapidly enlarging hard mass with airway/voice compromise; extremely aggressive with a very poor prognosis. Often only palliative/airway management is possible.
Thyroid lymphoma: a rapidly enlarging mass on a background of Hashimoto's thyroiditis; diagnosed by core biopsy and treated with chemoradiotherapy (not surgery).

🔎 Investigation

Work-up combines , with , and a .

Core tests: TFTs (a hot/toxic nodule on isotope scan is rarely malignant), ultrasound (TIRADS risk features), and FNAC reported by Thy/Bethesda category — the cornerstone for triaging benign vs malignant vs indeterminate nodules.
Markers/assessment: calcitonin (medullary), thyroglobulin (post-op surveillance of differentiated cancer); laryngoscopy to document vocal-cord function before surgery; CT/MRI for retrosternal extension or extent (with the contrast caveat).
Trap — iodinated contrast: avoid iodinated CT contrast if radioactive-iodine therapy is planned (it saturates the gland and delays RAI for weeks). Use ultrasound/MRI where possible in differentiated cancer.

💊 Treatment (overview)

Treatment is , by histology (surgical detail in Topic 49).

By type: differentiated (papillary/follicular) → lobectomy or total thyroidectomy ± radioactive iodine and levothyroxine/TSH suppression, with thyroglobulin surveillance; medullary → total thyroidectomy + central neck dissection (no RAI); anaplastic → palliation/airway; lymphoma → chemoRT. Detailed surgery and complications are in Topic 49.

📈 Prognosis

Differentiated (papillary/follicular) carcinoma has an excellent prognosis, especially in younger patients; medullary depends on stage and familial screening; anaplastic carcinoma has a dismal prognosis; lymphoma responds to chemoRT.

🎯 Demonstrate it like an examiner wants

Commit first, then reveal the model structure and build the chain step by step.

Prompt: "A 40-year-old has a firm, enlarging thyroid nodule and recent hoarseness. How do you assess?"

Your locked answer:
"An enlarging firm nodule with hoarseness suggests thyroid malignancy with recurrent-laryngeal-nerve involvement until excluded."
TFTs, ultrasound (TIRADS), FNAC (Thy/Bethesda); laryngoscopy to document the cord; consider calcitonin if medullary features/family history.
Assess nodes on US ± FNAC; imaging (US/MRI; avoid iodinated contrast if RAI planned) for extent/retrosternal spread; histology-specific markers.
Differentiated → surgery ± RAI + TSH suppression; medullary → total thyroidectomy + central neck (exclude phaeo first); anaplastic → palliation/airway; lymphoma → chemoRT (Topic 49 for surgery).
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: A growing thyroid carcinoma can invade or compress the adjacent recurrent laryngeal nerve in the tracheo-oesophageal groove, paralysing the ipsilateral vocal cord and causing hoarseness — a sinister sign. Papillary cancers spread via lymphatics to neck nodes, whereas follicular cancers invade vessels and metastasise haematogenously to bone/lung. FNAC triages most nodules, but follicular lesions require histological proof of capsular/vascular invasion to confirm malignancy.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"A young woman with a thyroid nodule and a small lateral neck node; FNAC shows papillary carcinoma." → total thyroidectomy (± neck dissection) + RAI + TSH suppression, thyroglobulin surveillance; excellent prognosis (Topic 49 for surgery).
"Raised calcitonin and a family history." → exclude phaeochromocytoma first, test RET, screen for hyperparathyroidism; total thyroidectomy + central neck dissection (no RAI); calcitonin/CEA follow-up.
Hypoechogenicity, microcalcifications, irregular/infiltrative margins, taller-than-wide shape, and suspicious nodes (TIRADS). These raise the risk and prompt FNAC; purely cystic/spongiform nodules are low risk.
A structured FNAC cytology classification (e.g. Thy1 non-diagnostic → Thy5 malignant; Bethesda I–VI) that stratifies malignancy risk and guides management (repeat FNAC, diagnostic lobectomy, or definitive surgery), notably for indeterminate (follicular) lesions.
Medullary (C-cell origin), anaplastic, and lymphoma are not iodine-avid, so RAI is ineffective. RAI is used for differentiated (papillary/follicular) cancer of follicular-cell origin after total thyroidectomy.
MEN2 (RET) predisposes to medullary thyroid carcinoma, phaeochromocytoma and (2A) hyperparathyroidism. It mandates RET testing, phaeo exclusion before surgery, and prophylactic thyroidectomy in gene carriers; family screening is essential.
