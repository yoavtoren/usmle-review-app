🦋🔧 Surgical treatment of the thyroid gland & its complications

The Overview is a full walk-through of the topic. Tap any coloured word for an extra exam pearl. Then work through all 5 tabs.

📖 Full topic explanation

Thyroid surgery is defined by its complications: the recurrent and superior laryngeal nerves, the parathyroids, and post-operative haematoma. Knowing the procedures, how to protect these structures, and how to recognise and manage the complications (some of them airway emergencies) is the heart of this topic.

🔪 Procedures & indications

Operations range from , sometimes with .

Extent: hemithyroidectomy/lobectomy for an indeterminate (follicular) nodule or small low-risk cancer (diagnostic + therapeutic); total thyroidectomy for larger/higher-risk differentiated cancer, medullary cancer, bilateral disease, large goitre or to enable radioactive iodine. Also done for compressive goitre or uncontrolled hyperthyroidism.
Neck dissection: central (level VI) compartment dissection for medullary carcinoma and node-positive papillary cancer; lateral neck dissection for lateral nodal disease. Tailored to histology and nodal status.

🛡️ Structures at risk (protect intra-op)

The key structures are the , the , and the .

Recurrent laryngeal nerve (RLN): runs in the tracheo-oesophageal groove near the inferior thyroid artery and the ligament of Berry. Injury → vocal-cord palsy: unilateral = hoarse/breathy voice; bilateral = airway obstruction/stridor (emergency). Identify and preserve it; intra-operative nerve monitoring is widely used.
External branch of the superior laryngeal nerve (EBSLN): supplies the cricothyroid (pitch/voice projection); injury causes loss of high notes/voice fatigue — important for singers. It runs near the superior thyroid pedicle, which should be ligated close to the gland.
Parathyroid glands: four small glands behind the thyroid; injury/devascularisation/removal → hypoparathyroidism and hypocalcaemia. Preserve them with their blood supply (capsular dissection) or auto-reimplant a devascularised gland.

⚠️ Complications

The major complications are , , and a , plus .

RLN palsy: unilateral → hoarse/breathy voice, weak cough (often recovers if neuropraxic); bilateral → cords paramedian with stridor/airway obstruction needing re-intubation/tracheostomy. Document cord function pre- and post-operatively.
Hypocalcaemia (hypoparathyroidism): after total thyroidectomy — perioral and digital paraesthesiae, carpopedal spasm, Chvostek/Trousseau signs, tetany and (severe) laryngospasm/arrhythmia. Monitor calcium/PTH and replace with calcium ± active vitamin D; usually transient but can be permanent.
Post-operative haematoma: a tense, expanding neck swelling causing airway compromise — a surgical emergency. Open the wound at the bedside immediately to evacuate the clot and relieve the airway, then return to theatre. Suture-cutters/clip-removers are kept by the bed.
Others: hypothyroidism (replace with levothyroxine after total/some hemi-thyroidectomy), thyroid storm (now rare — render euthyroid pre-op in Graves'), seroma/infection, scar, and (with long-standing goitre) tracheomalacia.

📋 Peri-operative care

Good outcomes depend on and .

Pre-op: document vocal-cord function (laryngoscopy), render hyperthyroid patients euthyroid (antithyroid drugs ± β-blocker/Lugol's iodine), and exclude phaeochromocytoma before medullary-cancer surgery. Consent for the specific complications.
Post-op: monitor for haematoma (airway), check calcium/PTH, assess voice/cords, watch the airway, and arrange thyroid-hormone replacement. Keep clip-removers at the bedside after total thyroidectomy.

📈 Outcome

Thyroid surgery is generally safe in experienced hands; the principal morbidities are RLN palsy and hypoparathyroidism (mostly transient), and the principal emergency is a post-operative haematoma. Nerve monitoring, meticulous parathyroid preservation and bedside vigilance minimise harm.

🎯 Demonstrate it like an examiner wants

Commit first, then reveal the model structure and build the chain step by step.

Prompt: "A few hours after total thyroidectomy the patient develops a tense neck swelling and breathing difficulty. What do you do, and what else do you monitor?"

Your locked answer:
"A tense expanding neck swelling with breathing difficulty after thyroidectomy is a post-operative haematoma compromising the airway — I open the wound at the bedside immediately."
Remove clips/sutures and evacuate the clot at the bedside to relieve airway pressure, call for senior help/anaesthetics, give oxygen, and return to theatre for haemostasis.
Check calcium/PTH (hypocalcaemia), assess voice and vocal-cord function (RLN), watch the airway, and arrange thyroid-hormone replacement.
Meticulous haemostasis, drain if used, and bedside clip-removers as standard. Counsel on the recognised complications at consent.
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: Bleeding into the closed neck space after thyroidectomy raises pressure that, even when small, causes venous congestion and laryngeal/supraglottic oedema rather than simple tracheal compression — rapidly obstructing the airway. Opening the wound releases the tamponade and decompresses the venous/oedematous swelling, restoring the airway. Definitive haemostasis in theatre then prevents re-accumulation.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"Tense neck swelling and stridor 3 hours after total thyroidectomy." → open the wound at the bedside to evacuate the clot, oxygen, call anaesthetics/senior, return to theatre; keep clip-removers at the bedside.
"Day 1 after total thyroidectomy: perioral tingling and a positive Trousseau sign." → check calcium/PTH, give calcium ± alfacalcidol; monitor for tetany/laryngospasm; most recover (parathyroid preservation/auto-reimplantation reduces permanent hypoPTH).
A stimulating probe and an EMG endotracheal tube confirm recurrent laryngeal (and EBSLN) nerve function during dissection, helping identify and preserve the nerve and predict post-op cord function (e.g. staging a planned contralateral lobectomy if signal is lost).
A rare anatomical variant (almost always right-sided, associated with an aberrant subclavian artery) where the nerve enters the larynx directly without looping — it is at higher risk of injury because the surgeon does not find it in the usual tracheo-oesophageal groove.
The cords lie paramedian → airway obstruction. Manage with re-intubation and, if no recovery, tracheostomy; later airway-widening procedures (cordotomy/arytenoidectomy/lateralisation) if the palsy is permanent (Topic 25).
To avoid injuring the external branch of the superior laryngeal nerve, which runs close to the superior pole vessels; ligating the vessels on the thyroid capsule keeps the nerve safe, preserving cricothyroid function/voice pitch.
