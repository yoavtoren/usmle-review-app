💧🎗️ Salivary gland tumors

📖 Full topic explanation

Salivary tumours follow a useful rule of thumb — the parotid is the commonest site and most are benign, but the smaller the gland, the higher the chance of malignancy. The benchmark benign tumour is the pleomorphic adenoma; the key malignant red flag is a facial-nerve palsy.

🗂️ The "rule of the glands"

Site predicts the chance of malignancy: ; .

Parotid: ~80% of salivary tumours arise here and ~80% are benign (mostly pleomorphic adenoma); ~80% of the benign ones are pleomorphic adenomas ("80% rule"). Most sit in the superficial lobe lateral to the facial nerve.
Smaller glands, higher malignancy: the proportion that is malignant rises as gland size falls — submandibular (~50%) and sublingual/minor glands (highest). So a minor-gland or sublingual mass is much more likely to be cancer.

🟢 Benign tumours

The two important benign tumours are the and .

Pleomorphic adenoma: the commonest salivary tumour — a slow-growing, painless parotid lump. It has a tendency to recur if incompletely excised or the capsule is ruptured, and a small long-term risk of malignant transformation (carcinoma ex-pleomorphic adenoma) — hence formal excision, not enucleation.
Warthin's tumour (papillary cystadenoma lymphomatosum): benign, in older male smokers, often in the parotid tail and sometimes bilateral/multifocal; takes up technetium on scintigraphy. Benign with negligible malignant potential.

🔴 Malignant tumours

The common malignancies are and , with .

Mucoepidermoid carcinoma: the commonest salivary malignancy (and the commonest in children); behaviour ranges from low- to high-grade. Grade drives treatment and prognosis.
Adenoid cystic carcinoma: notable for perineural spread, a long but relentless course with late lung metastases, and late recurrences — so wide excision, perineural assessment and long follow-up are needed. Other malignancies: acinic cell, carcinoma ex-pleomorphic adenoma, adenocarcinoma.
Red flags for malignancy: facial-nerve weakness (a malignant parotid tumour until proven otherwise), pain, rapid growth, fixation to skin/deep tissue, skin ulceration, and cervical nodes. A painless mobile lump is more likely benign.

🔎 Diagnosis

Assess with , avoiding .

Work-up: ultrasound-guided FNAC (or core biopsy) for cytology and MRI for deep-lobe extent, parapharyngeal spread, perineural invasion and the facial-nerve relationship; assess the facial nerve clinically and look for neck nodes.
Trap: do not perform an incisional/open biopsy of a salivary (especially parotid) mass — risk of tumour seeding and facial-nerve injury. Use FNAC/core biopsy; the definitive diagnosis is on the excised specimen.

💊 Treatment

Treatment is , with and recognised .

Surgery: parotid tumours → superficial (or total) parotidectomy with facial-nerve identification and preservation (the nerve is sacrificed only if directly involved by malignancy); submandibular tumours → gland excision (protect the lingual, hypoglossal and marginal mandibular nerves). Neck dissection for malignant/node-positive disease.
Adjuvant radiotherapy: for high-grade or incompletely excised malignancy, adenoid cystic (perineural) carcinoma, advanced stage, and recurrence. Improves local control where surgery alone is insufficient.
Complications: facial-nerve injury (temporary or permanent), Frey syndrome (gustatory sweating), salivary fistula/sialocele, greater-auricular-nerve numbness, and recurrence (especially pleomorphic adenoma if the capsule is breached).

📈 Prognosis

Benign tumours do well with complete excision (pleomorphic adenoma recurs if incompletely removed). Malignant prognosis depends on histology/grade and stage: low-grade mucoepidermoid does well; high-grade and adenoid cystic carcinomas (with perineural spread and late metastases) are more challenging.

🎯 Demonstrate it like an examiner wants

Commit first, then reveal the model structure and build the chain step by step.

Prompt: "A patient has a parotid lump with a new facial-nerve weakness. How do you assess and manage?"

Your locked answer:
"A parotid mass with facial-nerve weakness is a malignant parotid tumour until proven otherwise — the nerve involvement is the key red flag."
Assess the lump (fixation, skin), grade the facial nerve (House–Brackmann), examine the oropharynx (deep-lobe/parapharyngeal extension) and the neck for nodes.
US-guided FNAC/core biopsy (no incisional biopsy) and MRI (deep lobe, perineural spread, nerve relationship); stage the neck/chest.
Parotidectomy (sacrificing only involved nerve, with grafting if resected) + neck dissection for malignant/nodal disease + adjuvant radiotherapy (high-grade/adenoid cystic/incomplete/advanced). Facial rehabilitation as needed.
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: The facial nerve runs through the parotid, so a tumour that infiltrates the nerve (rather than merely displacing it) produces facial weakness — a hallmark of malignancy. Benign tumours like pleomorphic adenoma grow slowly and push the nerve aside without paralysing it. Surgery removes the tumour with a cuff of gland while preserving the uninvolved nerve, sacrificing and grafting it only where directly invaded.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"A slow-growing, painless, mobile parotid lump with normal facial-nerve function." → likely pleomorphic adenoma: US-FNAC, MRI if deep; superficial parotidectomy with facial-nerve preservation (avoid capsule rupture/enucleation).
"A rapidly growing fixed parotid mass with facial weakness and a neck node." → malignant: FNAC/core + MRI; parotidectomy ± nerve resection/grafting + neck dissection + adjuvant radiotherapy; facial rehabilitation.
It often presents as medial displacement of the tonsil/soft palate; MRI defines it and its relation to the carotid; do not transorally biopsy/excise (vascular/seeding risk) — resect via a transcervical (± transparotid) approach with facial-nerve care.
Gustatory sweating/flushing of the cheek when eating after parotid surgery, from misdirected parasympathetic fibres reinnervating skin sweat glands. Confirm with the starch–iodine test; treat with topical antiperspirants or botulinum toxin (prevented by interposing a flap/barrier).
Preserve it if not involved; if directly infiltrated, resect the involved segment with the tumour and perform immediate nerve grafting (e.g. great auricular/sural) ± static/dynamic facial reanimation, with eye protection (Topic 16).
Because malignancy is proportionally more common in smaller glands (~50% submandibular, highest in minor/sublingual), any persistent mass there warrants prompt FNAC/MRI and a lower threshold for malignancy than a parotid lump.
