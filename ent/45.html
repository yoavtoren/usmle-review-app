📖 Full topic explanation

Chronic laryngitis is persistent laryngeal inflammation; its importance is that the same risk factors (smoking, alcohol) drive premalignant change — leukoplakia, dysplasia and carcinoma-in-situ — on a path to invasive cancer. The governing rule is to biopsy persistent or suspicious lesions.

🗂️ Chronic laryngitis — causes

Chronic laryngitis is persistent inflammation from , with specific to remember.

Common causes: smoking (the key driver), alcohol, laryngopharyngeal reflux, chronic vocal abuse, and inhaled irritants/occupational exposure. They keep the mucosa inflamed and, with smoking/alcohol, drive dysplasia. Presentation: persistent hoarseness, throat clearing, globus and cough.
Specific causes: laryngeal tuberculosis (posterior larynx, usually with pulmonary TB), fungal laryngitis (often post-inhaled-steroid/immunosuppressed), granulomatosis with polyangiitis, sarcoidosis, amyloidosis, and rhinoscleroma. These can mimic malignancy and need biopsy/specific tests.

🎚️ Reinke's oedema

A common chronic form is .

Reinke's oedema: diffuse bilateral oedema of the superficial lamina propria (Reinke's space) from smoking (± reflux/voice abuse), classically a middle-aged woman with a low, gravelly voice. Stop smoking ± surgical reduction; biopsy to exclude carcinoma given the smoking history.

⚪ Premalignant lesions

The premalignant spectrum is described clinically as and histologically as , often as a .

Clinical descriptors: leukoplakia = a white patch (cannot be wiped off; a clinical term, not a diagnosis); erythroplakia = a red velvety patch with a higher malignant potential than leukoplakia. Both require biopsy because the appearance does not reveal the histology.
Histology: dysplasia (mild → moderate → severe) → carcinoma-in-situ → invasive SCC. Severe dysplasia/CIS carries a high risk of progression. Only biopsy determines the grade and whether invasion has occurred — appearance alone cannot.
Field cancerisation: the whole aerodigestive mucosa is exposed to smoking/alcohol, so multiple synchronous/metachronous premalignant and malignant lesions can arise ("field change"). This justifies panendoscopy and surveillance, not just treating one visible patch.

🧬 Risk factors & malignant potential

The dominant risks are , with a role for .

Smoking + alcohol: both are independent carcinogens for laryngeal/aerodigestive SCC and act synergistically (multiplicatively). Smoking cessation is the single most important intervention to reduce progression.
Other factors: HPV (notably in oropharyngeal cancer; a minor role in larynx), chronic reflux, prior radiation, and occupational exposures. These modulate risk and guide counselling.

🔎 Assessment & the red flag

Any needs to grade dysplasia and exclude invasion.

Red flag: hoarseness >3 weeks (especially smoker/drinker), or with a neck node, dysphagia, otalgia, stridor or haemoptysis → urgent laryngoscopy. Chronic laryngitis is a diagnosis only made after carcinoma is excluded.
Work-up: flexible laryngoscopy + videostroboscopy (abnormal mucosal wave suggests invasion), then microlaryngoscopy and biopsy/excision (definitive) to grade dysplasia and exclude invasive SCC; image (CT/MRI) if cancer is confirmed/suspected.

💊 Management

Treat the cause and the lesion: , and .

Conservative: smoking and alcohol cessation, treat reflux, voice therapy and removal of irritants. These reduce inflammation and the risk of progression and are essential alongside any surgery.
Lesion management: biopsy/excise leukoplakia and dysplasia; severe dysplasia/CIS → endoscopic (CO₂ laser) excision or radiotherapy with close surveillance for recurrence/field change. Invasive carcinoma is treated per its own pathway (Topics 43–44).

📈 Prognosis

With cessation of smoking/alcohol and treatment of reflux, chronic laryngitis improves and the risk of progression falls. Dysplasia/CIS treated by excision/laser or radiotherapy has good control but requires long-term surveillance because of field cancerisation; the priority is early detection of invasive cancer.

🎯 Demonstrate it like an examiner wants

Commit first, then reveal the model structure and build the chain step by step.

Prompt: "A 60-year-old smoker has 6 weeks of hoarseness; laryngoscopy shows a white patch on one vocal cord. How do you proceed?"

Your locked answer:
"A persistent vocal-cord leukoplakia in a smoker is a premalignant lesion that must be biopsied — appearance cannot exclude dysplasia or invasive carcinoma."
Leukoplakia is a clinical term; the histology spans dysplasia → CIS → invasive SCC. Note smoking/alcohol (synergistic), and consider field change.
Flexible laryngoscopy + stroboscopy (mucosal wave), then microlaryngoscopy with excisional biopsy for histology; image if invasion is suspected; panendoscopy for synchronous lesions.
Smoking/alcohol cessation, treat reflux, voice therapy; excise/laser dysplasia; severe dysplasia/CIS → laser/radiotherapy; surveillance for recurrence/field change; invasive cancer → its own pathway.
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: Chronic carcinogen exposure (tobacco/alcohol) accumulates genetic damage in the laryngeal squamous epithelium, producing progressive dysplasia and disordered keratinisation that appears as leukoplakia. With further mutation the dysplasia becomes carcinoma-in-situ and then breaches the basement membrane to become invasive SCC. Because the whole mucosal "field" is exposed, multiple independent lesions can arise, mandating biopsy and surveillance rather than reassurance.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"A smoker with persistent hoarseness and a white cord patch." → treat as premalignant: stroboscopy, microlaryngoscopy + biopsy; cessation/reflux treatment; excise/laser dysplasia; surveillance. Exclude invasive SCC.
"Hoarseness, weight loss and a posterior-larynx ulcer in a patient with a chronic cough." → consider laryngeal TB as well as carcinoma: biopsy, sputum/CXR for TB; treat accordingly. Don't assume cancer or simple laryngitis without histology.
Explain that smoking (synergistically with alcohol) drives the dysplasia and risk of cancer, and that cessation substantially reduces progression and recurrence. Offer structured support (advice, nicotine replacement, services) as part of treatment, not an afterthought.
Loss or marked reduction of the mucosal wave over a lesion suggests the epithelium is tethered by invasion into the deeper layers (rather than a superficial benign lesion), prompting biopsy/excision and staging.
Granulomatosis with polyangiitis (subglottic stenosis, c-ANCA), sarcoidosis, amyloidosis, relapsing polychondritis, TB, fungal infection and rhinoscleroma. They mimic each other and malignancy, so biopsy and targeted tests are key.
After excision, with regular laryngoscopic surveillance (the interval reflecting the grade), continued risk-factor cessation, and re-biopsy of any change — because field cancerisation means new lesions and recurrence are common.
