👅💉 Treatment of oropharyngeal & oral cavity tumors

The Overview is a full walk-through of the topic. Tap any coloured word for an extra exam pearl. Then work through all 5 tabs.

📖 Full topic explanation

Treatment is MDT-led and diverges by site: oral-cavity cancer is primarily surgical (± reconstruction and adjuvant therapy), whereas oropharyngeal cancer is treated with (chemo)radiotherapy or transoral surgery, with HPV+ disease responding especially well. The neck is always addressed, and rehabilitation is integral.

🧭 Principles

Decisions are , balancing .

Decision drivers: tumour site (oral cavity vs oropharynx), stage, HPV/p16 status, resectability, and patient fitness/preference. Early disease → single modality; advanced → combined modality. The neck is treated electively or therapeutically (Topic 46).
Cure vs function: oncological control is balanced against speech, swallowing, airway and cosmesis; reconstruction and rehabilitation are planned from the outset.

🔪 Oral-cavity cancer — surgery-led

Oral-cavity SCC is treated , with and .

Resection + reconstruction: wide local excision with clear margins (± mandibulectomy if bone is involved), reconstructed with free flaps (radial forearm for soft tissue, fibula for the mandible) to restore form and function. Surgery is preferred because oral-cavity SCC responds less reliably to radiotherapy and surgery spares the salivary glands.
Neck: elective neck dissection for a clinically N0 neck with significant occult-metastasis risk (e.g. deep tongue tumours); therapeutic neck dissection for node-positive disease. Sentinel-node biopsy is an option for early oral cancers.
Adjuvant therapy: postoperative radiotherapy for adverse features (multiple nodes, perineural/lymphovascular invasion, close margins); add concurrent chemotherapy for positive margins or extranodal (extracapsular) extension — the two strongest indications for chemoRT.

📡 Oropharyngeal cancer — (chemo)RT or TORS

Oropharyngeal SCC is treated with , with .

Modalities: definitive (chemo)radiotherapy (organ/function preservation, treats the necks) or transoral surgery (TORS/TLM) + neck dissection ± adjuvant therapy. Both give good control; choice depends on stage, anatomy and morbidity.
HPV+ disease: responds better with improved survival; treatment de-escalation (reducing radiation/chemo toxicity) is under clinical-trial evaluation but standard-dose therapy remains usual outside trials.

🧱 Reconstruction & rehabilitation

Functional outcome depends on and intensive .

Reconstruction: free tissue transfer (radial forearm, anterolateral thigh, fibula for mandible) restores the oral lining, tongue bulk and jaw continuity, preserving speech and swallowing. Planned with the resection.
Rehabilitation: speech-and-language therapy (swallow), dietitian/nutrition (PEG), dental assessment before radiotherapy (osteoradionecrosis prevention), prosthetic rehabilitation, and psychological support. Essential for quality of life.

⚠️ Toxicity & follow-up

Treatment carries and needs .

Toxicity: radiotherapy → mucositis, xerostomia, dysphagia/aspiration, fibrosis, dental caries and osteoradionecrosis; surgery → functional loss, fistula, donor-site morbidity, and risk to the lingual/hypoglossal nerves. Chemotherapy adds systemic toxicity.
Follow-up: regular clinical/endoscopic review and imaging (incl. PET-CT to assess the neck after chemoRT), surveillance for recurrence and second primaries (field cancerisation), and continued rehabilitation and smoking/alcohol cessation.

📈 Prognosis

Prognosis is stage- and HPV-dependent: HPV+ oropharyngeal cancer has excellent outcomes, while HPV− and advanced oral-cavity disease (especially with extranodal extension/positive margins) does worse and needs chemoradiotherapy. Early-stage tumours are highly curable by single-modality treatment.

🧠 Thinking questions (tap to reveal)

Oral-cavity SCC responds less reliably to radiotherapy and surgery spares the salivary glands and gives clear margins; the oropharynx is harder to resect with good function and responds well to (chemo)RT (especially HPV+) — though transoral surgery (TORS) is an alternative.
Positive margins and extranodal (extracapsular) extension are the two strongest indications for adjuvant concurrent chemoRT; other adverse features (multiple nodes, PNI/LVI, close margins) prompt adjuvant radiotherapy.
Free tissue transfer: radial forearm or anterolateral thigh flaps for soft tissue/tongue, and a fibula free flap for mandibular reconstruction, to restore lining, bulk and jaw continuity and preserve speech/swallowing.
When the occult-metastasis risk is significant (e.g. oral-tongue tumours with adequate depth of invasion) — by elective neck dissection (or sentinel-node biopsy/RT). It is treated in the same modality as the primary.
Non-healing necrosis of irradiated (usually mandibular) bone, precipitated by trauma/extractions. Prevent with dental assessment and extractions before radiotherapy, meticulous oral hygiene, and avoiding post-RT extractions; manage with conservative care, hyperbaric oxygen and surgery.
With a post-treatment PET-CT (≈12 weeks): a complete response avoids planned neck dissection, while residual nodal disease prompts salvage neck dissection.

🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: Oral-cavity tumours are accessible for wide excision with reconstruction, and because they are less radioresponsive and radiotherapy would damage salivary function, surgery is primary. Oropharyngeal tumours are harder to resect with good speech/swallow, and HPV+ disease is highly radioresponsive, so organ-preserving (chemo)radiotherapy (or minimally invasive transoral surgery) is favoured. In both, extranodal extension or positive margins signal aggressive disease requiring the addition of chemotherapy to radiotherapy.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"A T2N1 lateral-tongue SCC." → wide local excision + radial-forearm free flap + selective/therapeutic neck dissection; adjuvant RT (chemoRT if margins/ENE positive); speech/swallow and dental rehab.
"A T2N1 p16+ tonsil SCC." → definitive (chemo)radiotherapy covering the necks, or TORS + neck dissection; excellent prognosis; manage toxicity and rehabilitate.
Speech, swallowing and airway outcomes: surgery may impair tongue/jaw function (mitigated by reconstruction) but spares salivary glands; radiotherapy preserves anatomy but causes xerostomia/dysphagia/fibrosis. The MDT weighs these against cure for each patient.
A vascularised fibula free flap reconstructs the mandible and can carry osseointegrated dental implants for functional dental rehabilitation; prosthodontic input restores occlusion and mastication.
In selected advanced cases, induction (neoadjuvant) chemotherapy may downstage disease, assess chemo-responsiveness, or aid organ preservation, but it is not standard for all; concurrent chemoradiotherapy remains the backbone of non-surgical treatment.
Tumour burden and treatment toxicity (mucositis, dysphagia, odynophagia) cause weight loss that worsens outcomes and tolerance. Early dietitian input and PEG/feeding support maintain nutrition through surgery/chemoRT.
