👂⚠️ Complications of otitis media

📖 Full topic explanation

Complications of otitis media (acute or chronic/cholesteatoma) are divided into intratemporal (extracranial) and intracranial. They arise when infection escapes the middle-ear cleft by bone erosion, thrombophlebitis, or preformed pathways — and they remain a cause of death, so recognising the red flags is the highest-yield skill here.

🗺️ Classification & routes of spread

Group complications as . Infection spreads by , and the are acute coalescent mastoiditis and cholesteatoma.

Two families: intratemporal — mastoiditis, petrositis, facial palsy, labyrinthitis, labyrinthine fistula, subperiosteal/Bezold abscess; intracranial — meningitis, extradural/subdural empyema, brain abscess, lateral (sigmoid) sinus thrombosis, otitic hydrocephalus. Listing these two groups is the framework for the whole answer.
Routes: (1) direct bone erosion/osteitis (cholesteatoma, coalescent mastoiditis); (2) retrograde thrombophlebitis through venous channels into the cranium; (3) preformed pathways — the oval/round windows, congenital or surgical dehiscences, and the petrosquamous suture. These explain how an ear infection reaches the brain.
High-risk disease: complications cluster around acute coalescent mastoiditis (children) and cholesteatoma/atticoantral CSOM (any age). Any red-flag feature in these patients demands urgent imaging.

🦴 Intratemporal complications

These include , , , and .

Acute mastoiditis: the commonest suppurative complication — postauricular swelling/erythema, a protruding pinna and a sagging canal wall, with subperiosteal or Bezold (neck) abscess. Treat with IV antibiotics + myringotomy ± cortical mastoidectomy (Topic 09).
Petrositis / Gradenigo's syndrome: spread to a pneumatised petrous apex gives the triad otorrhoea + retro-orbital pain (CN V) + lateral rectus palsy (CN VI, Dorello's canal). Needs imaging and aggressive antibiotic ± surgical treatment.
Facial-nerve palsy: in AOM, from a dehiscent facial canal → urgent myringotomy + IV antibiotics, usually full recovery; in cholesteatoma, from erosion of the facial canal → urgent mastoid surgery. New facial weakness in a discharging ear is always a complication.
Labyrinthitis & fistula: serous labyrinthitis (irritation → vertigo, some SNHL, reversible) vs suppurative labyrinthitis (infection → severe vertigo + total SNHL "dead ear", and a gateway to meningitis). A positive fistula sign (vertigo on tragal pressure) indicates a lateral-canal fistula from cholesteatoma.

🧠 Intracranial complications

The major intracranial complications are , , and .

Meningitis is the commonest intracranial complication — headache, photophobia, neck stiffness, fever, reduced consciousness. Pneumococcus predominates. Confirm/treat urgently; image before LP if focal signs or raised ICP are suspected, and treat the ear source.
Abscess: extradural (often silent, found at surgery), subdural empyema (rapidly deteriorating, seizures), and brain abscess in the temporal lobe (from the tegmen) or cerebellum (from the sinus plate) — headache, focal deficit, fever, then raised ICP. Needs CT/MRI, neurosurgical drainage and mastoid eradication.
Lateral/sigmoid sinus thrombosis: swinging "picket-fence" fever with rigors, headache, ± Griesinger's sign (postauricular oedema over the mastoid emissary vein). The Tobey–Ayer test is the classic clinical sign; confirm with MRV/contrast CT. Treat with IV antibiotics, mastoidectomy ± anticoagulation.
Otitic hydrocephalus: raised intracranial pressure (headache, papilloedema, diplopia from VI palsy) without a mass or ventricular dilatation, usually associated with lateral sinus thrombosis impairing CSF reabsorption. Manage ICP (the threat is to vision) and treat the ear/thrombosis.

🚩 Red flags & assessment

In any patient with AOM/CSOM, the warning features are . These mandate .

Red flags: a discharging ear that develops severe headache, altered consciousness, seizures, neck stiffness, swinging fever, vertigo, diplopia, or facial palsy has a complication until proven otherwise. Do not keep changing antibiotics — escalate and image.
Work-up: FBC/CRP, blood cultures, contrast CT temporal bone + brain and MRI/MRV (best for intracranial collections and sinus thrombosis); audiometry; LP only after imaging excludes a mass/raised ICP.

💊 Management principles

Management combines of intracranial collections.

Triple approach: (1) broad-spectrum IV antibiotics crossing the blood–brain barrier (then culture-directed, often with metronidazole for anaerobes); (2) surgical eradication of the ear source — myringotomy for AOM, mastoidectomy for coalescent disease/cholesteatoma; (3) neurosurgical drainage of abscess/empyema and ICP control. Anticoagulation is considered for sinus thrombosis.

📈 Prognosis

Early recognition and the combined medical–surgical approach give good outcomes for most intratemporal complications and meningitis; brain abscess and extensive intracranial sepsis still carry significant mortality, and suppurative labyrinthitis leaves a permanently dead ear.

🧠 Thinking questions (tap to reveal)

Intratemporal: mastoiditis, petrositis (Gradenigo), facial palsy, serous/suppurative labyrinthitis, labyrinthine fistula, subperiosteal/Bezold abscess. Intracranial: meningitis (commonest), extradural/subdural empyema, brain abscess, lateral sinus thrombosis, otitic hydrocephalus.
By direct bone erosion/osteitis (cholesteatoma, coalescent mastoiditis), retrograde thrombophlebitis through venous channels, and preformed pathways (oval/round windows, dehiscences, the petrosquamous suture, or surgical defects).
Petrous apicitis giving the triad otorrhoea + retro-orbital pain (trigeminal) + lateral rectus palsy (abducens, in Dorello's canal). It signals petrous-apex infection needing imaging and aggressive treatment.
Serous = irritation/toxins → vertigo + partial, often reversible SNHL. Suppurative = actual infection of the labyrinth → severe vertigo + total irreversible SNHL ("dead ear") and a route to meningitis. Suppurative is the emergency.
Lateral (sigmoid) sinus thrombosis — picket-fence fever, ± Griesinger's sign, Tobey–Ayer test. Confirm with MRV/contrast CT; treat with IV antibiotics, mastoidectomy, and consider anticoagulation.
Temporal lobe (via the tegmen tympani) and cerebellum (via the sinus plate/Trautmann's triangle). They present with headache, focal deficit and raised ICP, needing CT/MRI, neurosurgical drainage and mastoid eradication.
A combined approach: high-dose IV antibiotics crossing the BBB, surgical eradication of the ear source (mastoidectomy), and neurosurgical drainage of intracranial collections with ICP control — never antibiotics alone when there is pus or a mass.

🎯 Demonstrate it like an examiner wants

Commit first, then reveal the model structure and build the chain step by step.

Prompt: "A patient with a long-standing cholesteatoma develops severe headache, fever and a new facial droop. How do you proceed?"

Your locked answer:
"A discharging cholesteatomatous ear with new headache, fever and facial palsy has an intratemporal and possibly intracranial complication — this is an emergency needing admission, imaging and combined ENT–neurosurgical care."
Facial palsy = eroded facial canal; headache/fever = possible meningitis, abscess or sinus thrombosis. Examine cranial nerves, fundi, neck stiffness, fistula sign and conscious level.
FBC/CRP, cultures; contrast CT temporal bone + brain and MRI/MRV for collections and sinus thrombosis; audiometry; LP only after imaging.
IV antibiotics across the BBB; mastoidectomy to eradicate the cholesteatoma and decompress; neurosurgical drainage of any abscess/empyema; manage facial nerve and ICP; anticoagulation for sinus thrombosis as indicated.
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: Cholesteatoma erodes the bony walls of the middle ear and mastoid, breaching the facial canal, labyrinth, tegmen and sinus plate. Infection then escapes by direct extension, retrograde thrombophlebitis, or preformed pathways into the cranial cavity. The result is a spectrum from facial palsy and labyrinthitis to meningitis, brain abscess and sinus thrombosis — treated by eradicating the ear source and draining intracranial pus.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"A child with otorrhoea develops fever, photophobia and neck stiffness." → resuscitate; IV antibiotics across the BBB; CT before LP if focal signs/raised ICP; identify and eradicate the ear source (myringotomy/mastoidectomy). Meningitis is the commonest intracranial complication.
"Mastoiditis with swinging fevers, rigors and postauricular oedema." → MRV/contrast CT; IV antibiotics, mastoidectomy (± exposing/incising the sinus), and consider anticoagulation. Watch for otitic hydrocephalus and protect vision.
Because an associated brain abscess or raised ICP can cause coning if CSF is removed. Imaging (CT/MRI) excludes a mass effect first; antibiotics should not be delayed for imaging.
A clinical test for lateral sinus thrombosis: compressing the affected jugular vein produces no rise in CSF pressure (the sinus is blocked), whereas compressing the normal side raises pressure. Largely historical now that MRV is available.
AOM complications (mostly children) arise from acute suppuration and a dehiscent facial canal, often reversible with drainage. Cholesteatoma complications arise from relentless bony erosion, so they need mastoid surgery to eradicate the matrix, not antibiotics alone.
Otitic hydrocephalus — raised ICP from impaired CSF reabsorption (with lateral sinus thrombosis) causes papilloedema and can damage the optic nerve. Management lowers ICP (acetazolamide, sometimes shunt/optic-sheath fenestration) alongside treating the ear and thrombosis.
