🗣️💉 Treatment of laryngeal tumors

The Overview is a full walk-through of the topic. Tap any coloured word for an extra exam pearl. Then work through all 5 tabs.

📖 Full topic explanation

Laryngeal-cancer treatment is stage-driven and function-focused: early disease (T1–T2) is cured by single-modality transoral laser surgery or radiotherapy, while advanced disease (T3–T4) is treated by organ-preserving chemoradiotherapy or total laryngectomy, always managing the neck and the airway.

🧭 Principles

Treatment is , prioritising .

Decision drivers: T and N stage, subsite, cartilage invasion, airway, voice priorities, fitness and patient choice. Early single-modality vs advanced combined-modality, with subsite-specific neck treatment.
Cure vs function: the larynx serves voice, airway and swallowing, so treatment aims for organ/function preservation where oncologically safe, reserving total laryngectomy for tumours that cannot be controlled or where the larynx is already non-functional.

🟢 Early disease (T1–T2)

Early laryngeal cancer is cured by , with .

Single modality: transoral laser microsurgery (CO₂ laser) or radiotherapy give comparable high cure rates for early glottic cancer. Surgery is quick and repeatable; radiotherapy avoids surgery but is a one-time treatment for that field.
Choice: often decided by anticipated voice outcome, comorbidity, lesion accessibility and patient preference. Radiotherapy may give better voice for some cords; laser preserves the option of repeat surgery/radiotherapy later.

🔴 Advanced disease (T3–T4)

Advanced cancer is treated by or , with afterwards.

Organ preservation: concurrent chemoradiotherapy (cisplatin) can preserve a functioning larynx in suitable T3 (and selected T4) tumours, with surgery held for salvage.
Total laryngectomy: preferred for T4 with thyroid-cartilage invasion/extralaryngeal spread, or for a non-functional larynx (aspiration) or chemoRT failure (salvage). It creates a permanent end-stoma (the patient becomes a neck breather) with adjuvant (chemo)RT as indicated.
Voice rehabilitation after laryngectomy: tracheo-oesophageal puncture + voice prosthesis (best), oesophageal speech, or electrolarynx; plus stoma care and humidification (Topic 29).

🩺 Neck & airway management

The , and the .

Neck: early glottic N0 needs little neck treatment (sparse lymphatics); supraglottic tumours require elective bilateral neck treatment (rich bilateral drainage); node-positive necks get therapeutic dissection/RT, in the same modality as the primary.
Airway: an obstructing tumour is an emergency — secure the airway (Heliox/steroids temporise; awake tracheostomy if needed) before definitive treatment.

⚠️ Complications & follow-up

Treatment carries and needs .

Complications: radiotherapy → mucositis, oedema, chondroradionecrosis, xerostomia, dysphagia; surgery → pharyngocutaneous fistula (esp. salvage), stomal stenosis, dysphagia, hypothyroidism, and the functional impact of laryngectomy.
Surveillance: regular endoscopy/imaging for recurrence and second primaries; salvage surgery (laryngectomy) for radiotherapy failure; continued smoking/alcohol cessation and speech/swallow rehabilitation.

📈 Prognosis

Early glottic cancer has excellent cure with single-modality treatment and good voice; advanced disease has lower control and greater functional cost. Total laryngectomy offers durable control for T4/salvage at the price of natural voice, mitigated by voice rehabilitation.

Single modality — transoral laser microsurgery (TLM) or radiotherapy — with comparable cure rates; choice depends on voice outcome, accessibility, comorbidity and patient preference.
Organ-preserving concurrent chemoradiotherapy (suitable T3/selected T4) or total laryngectomy (T4 with cartilage invasion, non-functional larynx, or salvage), with adjuvant (chemo)RT and neck treatment.
For T4 with thyroid-cartilage invasion/extralaryngeal spread, a non-functional aspirating larynx, or chemoRT failure (salvage) — situations where organ preservation would not be oncologically safe or functionally worthwhile.
Early glottic N0: minimal neck treatment (sparse lymphatics). Supraglottic: elective bilateral neck treatment (rich bilateral drainage). Node-positive: therapeutic neck dissection/RT in the same modality as the primary.
Pharyngocutaneous fistula — especially in irradiated/salvage cases. Manage with wound care, drainage, NBM/enteral feeding and antibiotics; large/persistent fistulae need flap repair.
Tracheo-oesophageal puncture + voice prosthesis (Blom–Singer, best), oesophageal speech, or an electrolarynx, with stoma care, humidification (HME) and SLT support (Topic 29).

🎯 Demonstrate it like an examiner wants

Commit first, then reveal the model structure and build the chain step by step.

Prompt: "A T4 glottic SCC with thyroid-cartilage invasion. How would you treat it and rehabilitate the patient?"

Your locked answer:
"A T4 tumour invading the thyroid cartilage is generally best treated by total laryngectomy rather than organ-preserving chemoRT, with neck treatment and adjuvant therapy."
Cartilage invasion/extralaryngeal spread = T4; confirm on CT/MRI and biopsy; assess nodes, airway, fitness and swallowing.
Total laryngectomy (permanent stoma) + neck dissection; adjuvant (chemo)RT for adverse features (margins/ENE); manage thyroid/parathyroid and airway.
Voice restoration (TEP + voice prosthesis preferred), stoma care, humidification (HME), swallow rehabilitation, and counselling about neck breathing/anosmia; surveillance for recurrence.
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: Tumour invasion through the thyroid cartilage means radiotherapy is less likely to control the disease and risks chondronecrosis, so surgical removal of the larynx offers the best oncological outcome. Total laryngectomy separates the airway from the pharynx, creating a permanent tracheostome through which the patient breathes. Voice is then re-created by shunting pulmonary air (via a tracheo-oesophageal valve) to vibrate the pharyngo-oesophageal mucosa.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"A T1a mid-cord SCC, mobile cord, N0." → single-modality transoral laser microsurgery or radiotherapy; excellent cure; counsel on voice outcome; minimal neck treatment.
"A T3 supraglottic SCC, N2, larynx still functional." → organ-preserving concurrent chemoradiotherapy with bilateral neck treatment; PET-CT to assess the neck afterwards; salvage surgery if it fails.
Weigh anticipated voice quality, lesion accessibility/extent, comorbidity, the wish to avoid surgery, and the value of keeping radiotherapy in reserve. Both give similar cure, so it is largely a functional and patient-preference decision made in the MDT.
Permanent separation of airway and pharynx with an end-tracheostome (neck breathing), loss of natural voice (rehabilitated by TEP), altered swallowing initially, anosmia (no nasal airflow) and loss of nasal air-conditioning (needs humidification/HME).
Irradiated tissue has impaired vascularity and healing, raising the risk of pharyngocutaneous fistula, wound breakdown and stricture; vascularised flap reconstruction is often used to reduce fistula rates.
Smoking/alcohol cessation, dental assessment before RT, nutrition (PEG), speech-and-swallow therapy, airway/stoma care, thyroid-function monitoring, and psychological support — integral to outcomes and quality of life, with structured surveillance for recurrence/second primaries.
