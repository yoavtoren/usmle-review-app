📊👂 Classification of hearing impairment & hearing examination

The Overview is a full walk-through of the topic. Tap any coloured word for an extra exam pearl. Then work through all 5 tabs.

📖 Full topic explanation

This topic is the toolkit: how to classify hearing loss (by type and by degree) and how to measure it, from tuning forks to objective tests. Mastering the logic of each test — what it stimulates and what an abnormal result localises — is what an examiner probes.
🔴 fatal/critical🟠 risk/demographic🔵 key context🟢 confirmatory🟣 trap

🗂️ Classification by type & degree

By : conductive, sensorineural, mixed, and retrocochlear/central (plus non-organic). By : graded in decibels from normal to profound.

Types: conductive (outer/middle-ear sound-delivery problem), sensorineural (cochlea or cochlear nerve), mixed (both), retrocochlear/central (nerve/brainstem/cortex), and non-organic (functional). Type determines management — surgery/aids for conductive, aids/implants ± MRI for sensorineural.
Degree (better-ear average, dB HL): normal ≤25, mild 26–40, moderate 41–60(–70), severe 71–90, profound >90 (WHO bands vary slightly). The degree sets the rehabilitation: aids for mild–severe, cochlear implant for severe–profound with poor aided benefit.

🔍 History & otoscopy

Begin with a structured history and — examination findings already separate many causes before any test.

Otoscopy: wax/FB/otitis externa (conductive, external), a dull/retracted or perforated drum (middle-ear disease), attic keratin (cholesteatoma), a normal drum (otosclerosis or SNHL). Pneumatic otoscopy adds drum mobility. This guides which tests are needed.

🎵 Tuning-fork tests

The and tests (512 Hz) give an instant type/side localisation, but have a key .

Rinne: compares air conduction (at the meatus) with bone conduction (on the mastoid). Rinne positive (AC>BC) = normal or SNHL; Rinne negative (BC>AC) = conductive loss of ≥~20–25 dB on that side.
Weber: a fork on the midline forehead. It lateralises to the affected ear in conductive loss (the impaired ear hears bone-conducted sound better, free of masking noise) and to the better ear in SNHL.
False-negative Rinne (trap): in a dead ear, the mastoid-placed fork is heard by the opposite (normal) cochlea, giving an apparent BC>AC (a "false conductive" result). Weber lateralising to the other ear unmasks it; this is why you cross-check the two tests.

📈 Pure-tone & speech audiometry

quantifies thresholds by air and bone conduction; the defines the conductive component. adds functional and retrocochlear information.

PTA: thresholds at 250 Hz–8 kHz for air conduction (whole pathway) and bone conduction (cochlea onward), with masking of the non-test ear. Configuration helps: sloping high-frequency (presbycusis/NIHL), low-frequency (Ménière's), a 4 kHz notch (noise).
Air–bone gap: AC worse than BC by a measurable margin = the conductive component; identical AC and BC = pure SNHL. The size of the gap predicts the benefit of conductive surgery (e.g. stapedotomy).
Speech audiometry: the speech reception threshold and word discrimination score. Rollover (scores fall at high intensities) and discrimination worse than the pure-tone average predicts suggest a retrocochlear lesion.

🥁 Tympanometry & acoustic reflexes

measures middle-ear compliance (Types A/As/Ad/B/C), and the tests the stapedial reflex arc.

Tympanogram types: A normal; As shallow (otosclerosis/ossicular fixation); Ad deep/hypermobile (ossicular discontinuity, flaccid drum); B flat (effusion if normal volume, perforation/patent grommet if high volume); C negative pressure (ET dysfunction).
Acoustic reflex: a loud sound triggers bilateral stapedius contraction. Absent in conductive loss, ossicular fixation, severe SNHL or VII lesions; reflex decay suggests a retrocochlear lesion. Reflexes are intact/low-threshold in superior canal dehiscence (vs absent in otosclerosis).

🔬 Objective tests

test outer-hair-cell function and tests the nerve/brainstem; together they identify and screen those who cannot do PTA.

OAEs: sounds generated by healthy outer hair cells in response to stimulation; present = cochlear (OHC) function intact. Used for newborn screening and to localise lesions.
ABR/BERA: far-field recording of waves I–V from the auditory nerve and brainstem. Gives an objective threshold (infants, non-organic loss) and localises retrocochlear lesions (delayed wave V or prolonged I–V interval).
Auditory neuropathy spectrum disorder: present OAEs (OHCs work) but absent/abnormal ABR — the lesion is at the inner hair cell/synapse/nerve. A key pattern that explains a child who "passes" OAE screening yet hears poorly.

👶 Paediatric & non-organic testing

Test choice is age-matched: ; and the detects feigned unilateral loss.

By age: newborns — OAE + automated ABR; ~6 months–2.5 y — visual reinforcement audiometry; ~2.5–4 y — play audiometry; older — standard PTA. Tympanometry complements all ages (commonest cause of paediatric loss is OME).
Non-organic loss: suspect with inconsistent results; confirm with objective tests (OAE/ABR normal despite claimed loss) and the Stenger test (simultaneous tones — a feigning patient denies hearing the louder tone in the "bad" ear).

📈 Putting it together

Otoscopy + tuning forks localise; PTA quantifies and defines the air–bone gap; tympanometry/reflexes characterise the middle ear; OAE/ABR give objective and retrocochlear data; MRI follows asymmetric/retrocochlear findings. The classification (type + degree) then dictates rehabilitation.

🎯 Demonstrate it like an examiner wants

Commit first, then reveal the model structure and build the chain step by step.

Prompt: "Take me through how you would assess and classify a patient's hearing in clinic."

Your locked answer:
"My aim is to classify the loss by type and degree and localise it, using a stepwise battery from bedside to objective tests."
History + otoscopy, then tuning forks (Rinne/Weber) to separate conductive from sensorineural and identify the side; cross-check for a false-negative Rinne.
Pure-tone audiometry (air/bone, masking → air–bone gap and degree), tympanometry + reflexes (middle-ear status), speech audiometry (function/rollover).
OAE/ABR for infants, non-organic loss or suspected retrocochlear lesions; MRI for asymmetric/retrocochlear pictures. Then state the classification and the rehabilitation it implies.
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: Each test interrogates a different segment of the pathway — tuning forks/PTA compare air (whole pathway) with bone (cochlea onward) to localise the block, tympanometry probes middle-ear mechanics, and OAE/ABR isolate hair cells versus nerve. Combining them converts a symptom into a precise type and degree of loss. That classification then dictates whether the answer is wax removal, surgery, a hearing aid, or a cochlear implant.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"Right Rinne negative, Weber right, PTA shows a right air–bone gap, As tympanogram, absent reflexes, normal drum." → right conductive loss from otosclerosis (stapes fixation). Offer aid vs stapedotomy.
"Claims a total right loss, but Weber goes right and OAE/ABR are normal on the right." → non-organic loss: confirm with the Stenger test and objective normality; manage sensitively.
Sound can cross the skull to the opposite cochlea (interaural attenuation, especially for bone conduction). Masking the better ear with noise prevents it "answering" for the test ear, giving true thresholds and an accurate air–bone gap.
A sustained tone normally maintains the stapedial reflex; abnormal decay (the reflex fades) suggests a retrocochlear (nerve) lesion and is one of the audiometric clues prompting MRI.
Adult — behavioural PTA + speech audiometry with tuning forks; newborn — objective OAE + automated ABR (behavioural testing isn't possible). Tympanometry complements both.
A low-frequency air–bone gap (negative bone-conduction thresholds) mimicking conductive loss, but with intact/low-threshold acoustic reflexes, abnormal VEMPs and a CT dehiscence — separating it from otosclerosis, where reflexes are absent.
