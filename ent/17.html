🌀 Peripheral vertigo & vestibular examination

The Overview is a full walk-through of the topic. Tap any coloured word for an extra exam pearl. Then work through all 5 tabs.

📖 Full topic explanation

Vertigo is the illusion of movement. The whole topic is built on one safety question — peripheral (labyrinth/nerve) or central (brainstem/cerebellar stroke)? — and the bedside examination, above all the HINTS battery, is what answers it.
🔴 fatal/critical🟠 risk/demographic🔵 key context🟢 confirmatory🟣 trap

🗂️ Define & classify the symptom

First separate true , then decide and characterise by .

Vertigo vs dizziness: vertigo = an illusion of movement (spinning) from a vestibular asymmetry; distinguish presyncope (cardiovascular), disequilibrium (gait/proprioceptive) and non-specific light-headedness. The mechanism differs, so the work-up differs.
Peripheral vs central (the key split): peripheral (labyrinth/vestibular nerve) is usually severe, episodic, with horizontal-torsional unidirectional nystagmus and intact consciousness; central (brainstem/cerebellum) may be milder but has direction-changing or vertical nystagmus, other neurology, and a NEGATIVE head impulse test — and can be a stroke.
Timing/triggers narrow it: seconds with position change → BPPV; days, constant, after a viral illness → vestibular neuritis/labyrinthitis; minutes–hours, recurrent, with aural symptoms → Ménière's; with headache/photophobia → vestibular migraine.

🧬 Vestibular anatomy & physiology

The labyrinth's drive the ; an asymmetry of tonic input is perceived as vertigo.

Sensors: the semicircular canals detect angular acceleration (rotation) and the utricle/saccule detect linear acceleration/gravity. Their tonic firing is balanced left–right; any imbalance (disease or head turn) signals "movement".
VOR: head movement is sensed and drives compensatory eye movements via the vestibular nuclei to stabilise gaze. A unilateral peripheral lesion breaks the VOR on that side, producing nystagmus and a positive head impulse test toward the lesion.

🔄 Peripheral causes

The big peripheral causes are (commonest), , and .

BPPV: displaced otoconia in a semicircular canal (usually the posterior canal) → brief (seconds) vertigo triggered by position change (rolling over, looking up). Diagnosed by the Dix–Hallpike test (latency, torsional upbeating nystagmus, fatigability) and cured by the Epley repositioning manoeuvre.
Vestibular neuritis vs labyrinthitis: neuritis = acute, prolonged (days) severe vertigo, often post-viral, with no hearing loss, horizontal-torsional nystagmus beating away from the affected ear and a positive head impulse. Labyrinthitis = the same plus hearing loss (cochlea involved).
Others: Ménière's (episodic vertigo + fluctuating SNHL + tinnitus + fullness — Topic 18), perilymph fistula (positive fistula test), superior canal dehiscence (Tullio/autophony), herpes zoster oticus and ototoxicity (often bilateral, oscillopsia).

🩺 Vestibular examination

Examine , the , , and — combined in the .

Nystagmus: peripheral nystagmus is horizontal-torsional, unidirectional (fast phase away from the lesion), suppressed by fixation; vertical, purely torsional, or direction-changing nystagmus is central.
Head impulse test (HIT): a rapid head turn while fixating; a corrective saccade (positive HIT) indicates a peripheral VOR deficit on that side. In acute vertigo, a NORMAL (negative) head impulse is a red flag for a central cause/stroke.
Dix–Hallpike: for posterior-canal BPPV — on rapidly lying the patient head-down and turned, a typical attack shows latency, a torsional/upbeating nystagmus that lasts <60 s and fatigues. The supine roll test diagnoses horizontal-canal BPPV.
Stance & gait: Romberg (falls toward the lesion), Unterberger/Fukuda stepping (rotates toward the lesion), and gait observation. Inability to stand/walk at all suggests a central (cerebellar) cause.
HINTS (Head Impulse, Nystagmus, Test of Skew) in acute continuous vertigo: peripheral = abnormal (positive) HIT + unidirectional nystagmus + no skew; a "central/dangerous" pattern (normal HIT, direction-changing nystagmus, or skew deviation) outperforms early MRI for detecting posterior-circulation stroke.

🔬 Special investigations

When needed, add .

Tests: caloric testing (canal paresis on the affected side), videonystagmography/posturography, VEMPs (otolith/canal function, abnormal in SCD/Ménière's), audiometry, and MRI for asymmetric SNHL (schwannoma) or suspected central disease.

💊 Management principles

Treat the cause: , , and urgent neurology for central causes.

BPPV: Epley (posterior canal) or Lempert/barbecue roll (horizontal canal) repositioning manoeuvres are curative; vestibular sedatives are unhelpful and may delay compensation.
Vestibular neuritis: short-course vestibular sedatives (prochlorperazine/antihistamine) for ≤72 h only for acute symptoms, then early vestibular rehabilitation exercises to drive central compensation; a steroid taper may help. Prolonged sedatives impair compensation.

📈 Prognosis

BPPV resolves with repositioning (may recur); vestibular neuritis compensates over weeks with rehabilitation; the priority throughout is to not miss a central (stroke) cause using HINTS and the red-flag features.

🧠 Thinking questions (tap to reveal)

Peripheral: unidirectional horizontal-torsional nystagmus suppressed by fixation, positive head impulse, no other neurology. Central: direction-changing or vertical nystagmus, NORMAL head impulse, skew deviation, other CNS signs. The HINTS battery formalises this.
Displaced otoconia (usually posterior canal) cause brief positional vertigo. Diagnose with the Dix–Hallpike (latency, torsional-upbeating nystagmus, fatigability); treat with the Epley repositioning manoeuvre.
Both = acute prolonged vertigo with a positive head impulse. Neuritis has no hearing loss; labyrinthitis has hearing loss (the cochlea is involved). Both are treated with short sedatives then vestibular rehabilitation.
A vestibular neuritis should give an abnormal (positive) head impulse; a normal head impulse in a patient with acute continuous vertigo and nystagmus suggests the lesion is central (e.g. cerebellar stroke) — image and refer.
Nystagmus fast phase beats away from the lesion; the head impulse is positive toward the lesion; Romberg and Unterberger deviate toward the lesion; calorics show canal paresis on the affected side.
A "central HINTS" pattern, vertical/direction-changing nystagmus, inability to stand, new headache/neck pain, or any focal neurology — especially with vascular risk factors. Arrange urgent imaging (MRI/DWI) and stroke-team review.

🎯 Demonstrate it like an examiner wants

Commit first, then reveal the model structure and build the chain step by step.

Prompt: "A 60-year-old has acute, constant vertigo for a day with nystagmus. How do you tell vestibular neuritis from a cerebellar stroke?"

Your locked answer:
"Acute continuous vertigo in a vasculopath is an acute vestibular syndrome — my job is to exclude a posterior-circulation stroke using HINTS before settling on vestibular neuritis."
Vestibular neuritis = peripheral VOR loss (positive HIT, unidirectional nystagmus, no hearing loss); cerebellar stroke = central pattern, often with subtle signs and vascular risk factors.
HINTS: peripheral = positive head impulse + unidirectional nystagmus + no skew; central = normal HIT, direction-changing/vertical nystagmus, or skew. Add gait (can't stand = central), hearing, and a full neuro exam.
Peripheral pattern → short vestibular sedative + early vestibular rehabilitation. Central pattern/any red flag → urgent MRI-DWI and stroke pathway. When uncertain in a vasculopath, treat as central.
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: A unilateral loss of vestibular input removes the tonic balance between the two labyrinths, so the brain perceives constant rotation and generates nystagmus beating away from the weak side. The VOR fails on that side, producing a corrective saccade on head impulse — the hallmark of a peripheral lesion. A cerebellar stroke instead spares the peripheral VOR (normal head impulse) but disrupts central gaze-holding, giving direction-changing or vertical nystagmus and a skew.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"Brief spinning when she rolls in bed or looks up; Dix–Hallpike triggers torsional-upbeating nystagmus with latency and fatigue." → posterior-canal BPPV: perform the Epley manoeuvre, reassure, teach home exercises; review if persistent.
"A day of constant vertigo, vomiting, unidirectional nystagmus, positive head impulse, can walk with support, normal hearing and neuro exam." → vestibular neuritis: short sedative, fluids, early vestibular rehabilitation; safety-net for central features.
Head Impulse: positive (corrective saccade) = peripheral; normal = central. Impaired direction — Nystagmus: unidirectional = peripheral, direction-changing/vertical = central. Test of Skew: vertical ocular misalignment on cover test = central. "INFARCT" (Impulse Normal, Fast-phase Alternating, Refixation on Cover Test) = stroke.
Warm/cold irrigation of each ear induces nystagmus via convection currents in the horizontal canal (COWS: Cold-Opposite, Warm-Same). A reduced response = canal paresis on that side, localising a peripheral vestibular lesion; bilateral loss suggests ototoxicity.
Graded exercises (gaze stabilisation, habituation, balance) drive central compensation — the brain recalibrates to the asymmetric vestibular input. It is the mainstay after neuritis and for uncompensated peripheral loss; sedatives hinder it.
Superior semicircular canal dehiscence — Tullio phenomenon (sound-induced vertigo) and Hennebert sign (pressure-induced). Confirm with a CT showing the dehiscence and low-threshold VEMPs; it also mimics a conductive loss with intact reflexes.
