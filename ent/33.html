👃🚫 Nasal obstruction — differential diagnosis

The Overview is a full walk-through of the topic. Tap any coloured word for an extra exam pearl. Then work through all 5 tabs.

📖 Full topic explanation

Nasal obstruction is approached by two questions: structural or mucosal? and, crucially, unilateral or bilateral? — because a persistent unilateral obstruction must exclude a foreign body, a tumour, or (after trauma) a CSF leak, while bilateral obstruction is usually inflammatory or septal.
🔴 fatal/critical🟠 risk/demographic🔵 key context🟢 confirmatory🟣 trap

🗂️ Framework

Divide causes into , and triage by and age.

Structural vs mucosal: structural = a fixed anatomical narrowing (septal deviation, turbinate hypertrophy, polyps, adenoids, choanal atresia, valve collapse, tumour, foreign body); mucosal/inflammatory = swelling from rhinitis (allergic/non-allergic), rhinosinusitis, or rhinitis medicamentosa. Structural tends to be constant/positional; mucosal fluctuates.
Unilateral vs bilateral (the key triage): bilateral obstruction is usually rhinitis, sinusitis/polyps or septal deviation; a persistent unilateral obstruction is a red flag for a foreign body (child), tumour, or CSF leak/structural anomaly and must be examined and imaged.

🧱 Structural causes

Common structural causes are , , , and the unilateral red flags of .

Septum & turbinates: a deviated nasal septum (congenital or post-trauma) causes fixed, often unilateral obstruction; inferior turbinate hypertrophy (allergy/rhinitis/compensatory) causes bilateral/alternating obstruction. Managed with septoplasty and turbinate reduction respectively.
Adenoid hypertrophy: the leading cause of bilateral nasal obstruction/mouth breathing in children (snoring, hyponasal speech, OME, adenoid facies — Topic 20); assessed by nasendoscopy and treated by adenoidectomy when indicated.
Nasal valve collapse: dynamic narrowing of the internal nasal valve (the narrowest part of the airway) on inspiration, often after rhinoplasty/trauma or with weak cartilage; demonstrated by the Cottle manoeuvre and treated with grafts/spreaders.
Unilateral red flags: a child with unilateral foul/blood-stained discharge → nasal foreign body; an adult/adolescent with unilateral obstruction, bleeding, pain or a mass → tumour (inverted papilloma, sinonasal SCC, esthesioneuroblastoma; angiofibroma in a teenage male — don't biopsy). Examine and image.

🤧 Mucosal/inflammatory causes

Mucosal causes are , , and .

Rhinitis: allergic (sneezing, itch, clear rhinorrhoea, pale boggy turbinates, atopy — seasonal/perennial) and non-allergic/vasomotor (triggered by temperature/odours, no allergy). Treated with allergen avoidance, intranasal steroids and antihistamines.
Rhinitis medicamentosa: rebound congestion from overuse of topical decongestants (and from cocaine/some drugs). The history of prolonged decongestant-spray use is diagnostic; treat by stopping the spray and bridging with intranasal steroids.
Chronic rhinosinusitis ± polyps: bilateral obstruction with discharge/smell loss and mucosal disease/polyps on endoscopy (Topic 30). Bilateral polyps in a child → exclude cystic fibrosis.

👶 Congenital & paediatric causes

Specific paediatric causes are and .

Choanal atresia: failure of the posterior choana to canalise. Bilateral choanal atresia is a neonatal emergency — neonates are obligate nasal breathers, so they present with cyclical cyanosis relieved by crying; confirm by failure to pass a catheter and CT, secure the airway, and repair. Associated with CHARGE syndrome. Unilateral atresia presents later with unilateral discharge/obstruction.
Midline nasal masses (trap): a congenital dermoid, glioma or encephalocele can present as a midline nasal mass — do NOT biopsy until imaging (MRI/CT) excludes an intracranial connection (CSF leak/meningitis risk).

🔎 Assessment

Examine with , add , and act on the .

Examination: assess airflow each side, anterior rhinoscopy (septum, turbinates, polyps, mass), the Cottle manoeuvre (valve collapse), and flexible nasendoscopy (posterior cavity, postnasal space, adenoids, choanae, masses).
Investigations: allergy testing (skin-prick/specific IgE) for rhinitis; CT sinuses for CRS/structural/tumour assessment; MRI for tumours/midline masses; biopsy of suspicious lesions (not midline masses or angiofibroma until imaged).
CSF rhinorrhoea: unilateral clear watery rhinorrhoea, worse on bending forward, after trauma/surgery → suspect a CSF leak; confirm with β-2 transferrin and image (anterior skull-base defect). Don't dismiss it as "allergic rhinitis".

💊 Management principle

Treatment is cause-specific: intranasal steroids/antihistamines and allergen avoidance for rhinitis, stop the spray for rhinitis medicamentosa, septoplasty/turbinate reduction/valve surgery for structural causes, adenoidectomy for children, and prompt management of the red-flag causes (foreign body, tumour, choanal atresia, CSF leak).

🧠 Thinking questions (tap to reveal)

Structural vs mucosal, and unilateral vs bilateral with age. Bilateral = usually rhinitis/sinusitis/septal; persistent unilateral = red flag for foreign body (child), tumour, or CSF leak — examine and image.
Nasal foreign body until proven otherwise — examine and remove. Persistent unilateral discharge in a child is a classic FB presentation; a battery is an emergency.
Neonates are obligate nasal breathers, so bilateral atresia causes cyclical cyanosis relieved by crying and feeding difficulty. Secure the airway (oral airway/feeding), confirm (failure to pass a catheter, CT), and repair; screen for CHARGE.
Rhinitis medicamentosa — rebound congestion from topical decongestant overuse. Treat by stopping the spray, bridging with intranasal steroids, and managing the original cause.
CSF rhinorrhoea from an anterior skull-base defect. Confirm with β-2 transferrin and image; manage the leak (meningitis risk). Don't label it allergic rhinitis.
A dermoid, glioma or encephalocele may have an intracranial connection; biopsy risks CSF leak and meningitis. Image (MRI/CT) first to exclude intracranial extension.

🎯 Demonstrate it like an examiner wants

Commit first, then reveal the model structure and build the chain step by step.

Prompt: "A 50-year-old has 3 months of progressive unilateral nasal obstruction with occasional blood-stained discharge. How do you assess?"

Your locked answer:
"Progressive unilateral obstruction with blood-stained discharge in an adult is a sinonasal tumour until excluded — this is not simple rhinitis."
Unilateral red flags: tumour (inverted papilloma, SCC, esthesioneuroblastoma), foreign body, fungal disease, CSF leak. Take a smoking/occupational history.
Anterior rhinoscopy + flexible nasendoscopy (mass, bleeding, side), CT and MRI (extent, bone/orbit/skull-base), then biopsy of a suspicious lesion (not if angiofibroma/midline mass suspected).
If tumour confirmed → head-and-neck/skull-base MDT and stage-directed treatment (Topic 42). If benign structural cause → cause-specific surgery/medical therapy.
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: A unilateral mass mechanically narrows one nasal cavity and erodes/ulcerates mucosa, causing progressive obstruction and intermittent bleeding. Because benign mucosal disease is typically bilateral and symmetrical, persistent one-sided signs point to a localised structural lesion. Endoscopy and cross-sectional imaging define it, and biopsy provides the histological diagnosis that directs treatment.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"Year-round bilateral blockage, sneezing, clear rhinorrhoea, pale boggy turbinates." → allergic rhinitis: allergen avoidance, intranasal steroid ± antihistamine, allergy testing; consider turbinate reduction if refractory.
"Progressive one-sided obstruction with blood-stained discharge in a smoker." → treat as a sinonasal tumour: nasendoscopy, CT/MRI, biopsy of a suspicious lesion; refer to the skull-base MDT.
Anterior rhinoscopy for deviation/spurs; the Cottle manoeuvre (lateral cheek traction opens a collapsing valve, improving airflow) and modified Cottle (cotton bud support) localise internal nasal valve collapse, guiding septoplasty vs spreader grafts.
A paradoxical sensation of nasal obstruction and dryness despite a wide airway, usually after aggressive turbinate resection (loss of the turbinates' airflow-sensing/conditioning function). It is difficult to treat — a reason to preserve turbinate tissue at surgery.
Later than bilateral (which is a neonatal emergency): a child or adult with persistent unilateral obstruction and discharge. Confirm by failure to pass a catheter and CT; repair endoscopically. Always reconsider FB/tumour in unilateral disease.
Examine with good light; remove a cooperative child's anterior FB by positive-pressure ("parent's kiss"), suction, hook or forceps; a button battery is an emergency (necrosis) — remove urgently, no irrigation. Refer/GA if uncooperative or posterior; beware aspiration risk.
