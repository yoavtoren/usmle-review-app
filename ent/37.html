📖 Full topic explanation

Head-and-neck cancer of the upper aerodigestive tract (UADT) is mostly squamous cell carcinoma driven by tobacco + alcohol and increasingly HPV. The general principles — risk factors, field cancerisation, TNM staging, multidisciplinary work-up and the surgery/radiotherapy/chemotherapy modalities — apply across all the sites covered in the following topics.

🗂️ Scope & histology

The UADT comprises the , and the dominant histology is .

Sites: oral cavity, nasopharynx (epipharynx), oropharynx, hypopharynx, larynx, and the sinonasal tract; salivary glands are considered separately. Each site has distinct lymphatic drainage and behaviour, but shares general oncological principles.
Histology: >90% are squamous cell carcinoma arising from the mucosal lining; others include salivary-type carcinomas, lymphoma, melanoma, sarcoma and (nasopharynx) undifferentiated EBV-related carcinoma.

🚬 Risk factors

The dominant risks are and , with several .

Tobacco + alcohol: independent carcinogens that act synergistically (multiplicatively) for UADT SCC. Smoking cessation is the key modifiable factor and improves treatment outcomes.
Viral: HPV (esp. type 16) drives a rising proportion of oropharyngeal SCC — younger, often non-smokers, with cystic neck nodes and a better prognosis (p16-positive); EBV drives nasopharyngeal carcinoma.
Site-specific: betel/areca nut (oral cavity), wood/leather dust and nickel (sinonasal adenocarcinoma), salted fish/nitrosamines (nasopharynx), Plummer–Vinson (post-cricoid), GORD, immunosuppression and prior radiation.

🌾 Premalignancy & field cancerisation

SCC often arises from in a , which mandates panendoscopy.

Premalignant lesions: leukoplakia, erythroplakia and dysplasia (→ carcinoma-in-situ); erythroplakia has the highest malignant potential. Any persistent lesion is biopsied (Topic 45).
Field cancerisation: the whole mucosa is exposed to carcinogens, so synchronous and second primary tumours are common. This justifies panendoscopy at diagnosis and long-term surveillance.

👤 Presentation

UADT cancer presents with , with red-flag features that demand urgent referral.

Presentation/red flags: a non-healing ulcer or mass, persistent hoarseness or dysphagia >3 weeks, odynophagia, referred otalgia, a neck lump, unexplained weight loss, trismus, bleeding or cranial-nerve palsy — any in a smoker/drinker → urgent (2-week-wait) head-and-neck referral.

🔎 Diagnosis & staging

Work-up combines , , and .

Tissue diagnosis: flexible nasendoscopy, then biopsy; examination under anaesthesia + panendoscopy maps the primary and excludes synchronous tumours. Never do an open neck biopsy first in suspected SCC (use FNAC).
Imaging: CT and/or MRI for local extent and nodal staging; ultrasound-guided FNAC of a neck node; PET-CT for distant disease/unknown primary and treatment response. Stage the chest for distant metastases (lung commonest).
Staging: TNM (AJCC), with a separate, more favourable staging for p16-positive (HPV) oropharyngeal cancer. Test p16/HPV (oropharynx) and EBV (nasopharynx) as they alter prognosis and management.

💊 General treatment principles

Treatment is , using , with and essential .

MDT: decisions are made by a head-and-neck multidisciplinary team (surgeon, oncologist, radiologist, pathologist, SLT, dietitian, clinical nurse specialist), individualised to site, stage, HPV/EBV status, comorbidity and patient choice.
Modalities: surgery, radiotherapy, and chemotherapy (cisplatin-based), plus targeted therapy (cetuximab) and immunotherapy (PD-1 inhibitors in recurrent/metastatic disease). Concurrent chemoradiotherapy enables organ preservation.
Stage strategy: early disease (stage I–II) → single modality (surgery OR radiotherapy); advanced (III–IV) → combined modality (surgery + adjuvant (chemo)RT, or definitive chemoRT). The neck is treated electively or therapeutically depending on nodal risk/status (Topic 46).
Rehabilitation & supportive care: speech and swallowing therapy, nutrition (PEG), dental assessment before radiotherapy (osteoradionecrosis prevention), airway management, and psychological support. Palliative care for incurable disease. Survivorship and surveillance follow.

📈 Prognosis

Prognosis depends on site, stage, and HPV/EBV status: early glottic cancer and HPV-positive oropharyngeal cancer do well, while hypopharyngeal and advanced/ENE-positive disease do poorly. Extranodal extension and positive margins worsen outcome and prompt adjuvant chemoradiotherapy.

Prompt: "Outline your general approach to a patient with a suspected head-and-neck squamous cell carcinoma."

Your locked answer:
"A non-healing mucosal lesion or neck node with red flags in a smoker/drinker is a head-and-neck SCC until excluded, managed on an urgent cancer pathway by the MDT."
Tobacco/alcohol (synergistic), HPV/EBV, betel, occupational dust; symptoms >3 weeks (ulcer, hoarseness, dysphagia, otalgia, neck lump, weight loss).
Nasendoscopy + biopsy, EUA/panendoscopy (synchronous primaries), US-FNAC of the node (not open biopsy), CT/MRI ± PET-CT, and p16/EBV; assign TNM.
Early → single modality; advanced → combined modality; treat the neck; provide dental, nutrition, speech/swallow rehabilitation and smoking cessation; surveillance.
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: Carcinogen exposure (tobacco/alcohol) or oncogenic virus (HPV/EBV) accumulates mutations across the mucosal field, producing dysplasia that progresses to invasive squamous carcinoma. The tumour invades locally and spreads via lymphatics to predictable cervical node levels, with extranodal extension worsening prognosis. Treatment matches the modality (surgery/radiotherapy/chemotherapy) to the site, stage and biology, while preserving function where possible.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"A 62-year-old smoker/drinker has a non-healing throat ulcer, referred otalgia and a hard level-II node." → suspected H&N SCC: nasendoscopy + biopsy, EUA/panendoscopy, US-FNAC of the node, CT/MRI ± PET-CT, p16; stage and treat by MDT.
"A 45-year-old non-smoker has a painless cystic upper-neck node." → consider HPV+ oropharyngeal SCC (a cystic node is not just a branchial cyst in this age group): FNAC + p16, examine/biopsy the tonsil/tongue base, imaging; treat per HPV+ pathway.
To detect distant metastases and synchronous primaries, to find an unknown primary presenting as a neck node, and to assess treatment response (e.g. after chemoRT, to decide on neck dissection). It complements CT/MRI of the primary and neck.
Acute: mucositis, dysphagia/odynophagia, skin reaction. Late: xerostomia, dysphagia/aspiration, fibrosis, hypothyroidism, dental caries and osteoradionecrosis (hence pre-RT dental assessment). These shape rehabilitation and follow-up.
Secure the airway first (senior ENT/anaesthetics; awake tracheostomy if the larynx is obstructed/disrupted), then proceed with staging and definitive treatment. Airway always precedes oncological work-up.
Continued smoking reduces radiotherapy efficacy, increases complications and second primaries, and worsens survival. Cessation improves treatment response and outcomes, so it is an active component of management.
