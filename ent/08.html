🥁 Tympanoplasty & myringoplasty

The Overview is a full walk-through of the topic. Tap any coloured word for an extra exam pearl. Then work through all 5 tabs.

📖 Full topic explanation

These are the reconstructive operations of the tympanic membrane and ossicular chain. Myringoplasty repairs the drum alone; tympanoplasty repairs the drum and rebuilds the sound-conducting mechanism. The exam wants the Wullstein classification, graft choices and techniques, the factors that decide success, and the middle-ear transformer physics behind it.

🗂️ Definitions & the transformer mechanism

Myringoplasty: repair of the tympanic membrane without touching the ossicles (= Wullstein type I tympanoplasty). Indicated for a persistent perforation with an intact, mobile chain to stop recurrent infection (water entry) and close the conductive gap.
Tympanoplasty: repair of the drum plus reconstruction of the ossicular chain when it is eroded or removed for disease, re-establishing sound transmission from drum to oval window.
Transformer (≈ a 22:1 gain, ~25–30 dB): two mechanisms — the areal ratio of the large drum to the small footplate (~17–21:1) and the ossicular lever ratio (~1.3:1) — match low-impedance air to high-impedance cochlear fluid. Reconstruction tries to recreate this; losing it produces a ~30 dB conductive loss.

📐 Wullstein classification

Tympanoplasty is graded by the level onto which the graft transmits sound, though modern practice describes reconstruction by .

Types: I = graft on an intact chain (myringoplasty); II = graft to the incus (eroded malleus); III = graft to the stapes head (myringostapediopexy); IV = graft to a mobile footplate with a round-window air pocket; V = fenestration for a fixed footplate.
Modern descriptors: reconstruction is now usually described by whether a PORP (to stapes head) or TORP (to footplate) is used, which is more clinically useful than the historical types IV–V.

🧵 Graft materials & techniques

The common grafts are , placed by through one of several .

Grafts: temporalis fascia (the classic, readily available, good take); tragal/conchal cartilage with perichondrium (stiffer — preferred for revisions, subtotal/anterior perforations, atelectasis and poor tubal function because it resists retraction); perichondrium and (historically) vein/fat. All are autologous and become incorporated.
Underlay vs overlay: underlay places the graft medial to the drum remnant/annulus (technically easier, less blunting/lateralisation, commonest); overlay places it lateral after removing canal skin (good for anterior perforations but risks blunting and lateralisation and is more demanding).
Approaches: permeatal/transcanal (small posterior perforations), endaural, postauricular (best exposure, anterior perforations), and increasingly transcanal endoscopic (wide-angle minimally invasive view). The approach is chosen by perforation site and access.

⛓️ Ossiculoplasty

When the chain is disrupted, hearing is rebuilt with a or a sculpted autograft; a .

Reconstruction by defect: eroded long process of incus → an incus interposition or a prosthesis from malleus/drum to stapes; absent superstructure → TORP to the footplate; present stapes head → PORP. Materials: titanium, hydroxyapatite, or remodelled autograft incus.
Trap — extrusion: a synthetic prosthesis can erode through the drum; interposing a cartilage cap between prosthesis and graft markedly reduces extrusion. The stapes/footplate must be mobile for any reconstruction to work.

✅ Pre-operative assessment & patient selection

Success depends on , with documented .

Selection factors: ideally a dry, infection-free ear; adequate Eustachian-tube function (poor function predicts retraction/failure); the contralateral ear (operate the worse-hearing ear first, be conservative on an only-hearing ear); age (defer in very young children with active tubal disease); and treatment of nasal/sinus disease.
Audiometry: documents the air–bone gap (the surgically closable conductive component) and the bone-conduction reserve (cochlear potential). A large air–bone gap with good bone conduction predicts the best hearing gain.

📊 Outcomes & factors affecting graft take

Perforation closure is high (~85–90%), but .

Predictors of failure: poor Eustachian-tube function, active infection/discharge, large or anterior perforations (poorer blood supply/visualisation), revision surgery, smoking, and a wet contralateral ear. Cartilage grafting and meticulous technique improve take in these settings.

⚠️ Complications

Beyond graft failure, risks include .

Complications: graft failure/residual perforation, sensorineural loss or "dead ear" (especially with stapes/footplate work), facial-nerve injury, chorda tympani injury → dysgeusia, prosthesis extrusion/displacement, lateralisation or blunting of the graft, and iatrogenic cholesteatoma from entrapped squamous epithelium.

📈 Prognosis

Myringoplasty reliably closes perforations and removes the recurrent-infection problem; hearing improves in most. Ossiculoplasty results are more variable and depend on tubal function, a mobile footplate and the prosthesis used; cartilage techniques have improved long-term stability.

🧠 Thinking questions (tap to reveal)

Myringoplasty = repair of the drum only with an intact ossicular chain (Wullstein type I). Tympanoplasty = repair of the drum plus ossicular reconstruction when the chain is eroded/removed.
The drum-to-footplate areal ratio (~17–21:1) plus the ossicular lever (~1.3:1) give ~22:1 gain (~25–30 dB), matching air to cochlear fluid. Losing the mechanism causes a maximal conductive loss of ~30 dB, which reconstruction aims to recover.
Cartilage (stiffer, retraction-resistant) is preferred for revisions, subtotal/anterior perforations, atelectatic ears and poor Eustachian-tube function, where fascia is more likely to retract or fail.
Underlay (medial) is easier with less lateralisation/blunting and is the default; overlay (lateral) gives better anterior-perforation closure but risks blunting and graft lateralisation and is more demanding.
Failure follows poor tubal function, active infection, large/anterior perforations, revision and smoking. Optimise with a dry ear, cartilage grafting, treating nasal disease, and good technique; operate the worse ear first.
The footplate must be mobile. A fixed footplate (e.g. otosclerosis or congenital fixation) will not transmit sound regardless of the prosthesis and needs a different procedure (stapedotomy).

🎯 Demonstrate it like an examiner wants

Commit first, then reveal the model structure and build the chain step by step.

Prompt: "A 25-year-old has a dry central perforation, a 30 dB conductive loss and an intact ossicular chain. Plan the operation and counsel her."

Your locked answer:
"With a dry perforation and an intact chain this is a myringoplasty (type I tympanoplasty), to stop recurrent infection and close the air–bone gap."
Confirm a dry ear and good Eustachian-tube function; audiometry (air–bone gap + bone reserve); assess the other ear; treat nasal/sinus disease; consent.
Postauricular/permeatal approach; underlay temporalis fascia or cartilage graft; preserve the chain; pack and review. Choose cartilage if there is atelectasis or doubt about tubal function.
~85–90% closure with likely hearing improvement; risks include graft failure, rare SNHL/dead ear, taste change (chorda tympani) and tinnitus. Keep the ear dry post-op.
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: A perforation breaks the closed middle-ear air space, reducing the drum's effective vibrating area and allowing sound to reach both windows out of phase, which causes the conductive loss. A graft re-establishes an intact, mobile drum that again couples sound efficiently to the ossicular chain. Restoring the closed, aerated middle ear also stops water and pathogens entering, ending recurrent infection.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"A large anterior perforation in a frequently-wet ear." → optimise (dry the ear, treat nasal disease), choose a postauricular approach for exposure and a cartilage graft, and counsel a higher failure rate; consider staging.
"Drum healed but a 30 dB gap persists." → suspect ossicular discontinuity (eroded incus) or a fixed/over-stiff reconstruction; re-image/re-explore and perform ossiculoplasty (PORP/TORP); exclude an iatrogenic cholesteatoma.
History (autophony, recurrent OME), otoscopy for retraction, tympanometry/Valsalva, and the state of the other ear. Poor tubal function predicts graft retraction/failure and recurrent effusion, favouring cartilage grafting and cautious counselling.
"Take" is revascularisation and epithelialisation of the graft. It is supported by a dry, well-vascularised bed, accurate apposition, absence of infection, and middle-ear packing to support the graft until it heals.
In young children with active Eustachian-tube dysfunction/recurrent OME, repair often fails; many surgeons defer myringoplasty until ~age 8–10 (or until the contralateral ear shows good tubal function), unless infection control demands earlier surgery.
The endoscope gives a superior transcanal view of the anterior annulus and hidden recesses, allowing minimally invasive repair with less drilling and morbidity; useful for anterior perforations and selected ossicular work.
