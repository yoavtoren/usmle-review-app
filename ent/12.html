🦻 Prosthetics & surgical therapy of hearing loss & deafness

📖 Full topic explanation

Rehabilitation of hearing loss is a ladder matched to the type and severity of loss: conventional aids → bone-conduction and middle-ear implants → cochlear implants → auditory brainstem implants, alongside reconstructive surgery. The exam wants you to pick the right device for the loss and know how a cochlear implant works.

🗂️ Framework — match device to loss

Choose by loss type: favours surgery, bone-conduction or middle-ear implants; is treated with air-conduction aids and, when severe–profound, a ; an needs an auditory brainstem implant.

Conductive/mixed: first consider correcting the mechanism (tympanoplasty, ossiculoplasty, stapedotomy). If the ear is unsuitable (atresia, chronic discharge, failed surgery) use a bone-conduction device that bypasses the outer/middle ear, or an active middle-ear implant.
Sensorineural: outer/middle ear are intact, so amplification must drive the cochlea — air-conduction hearing aids for mild–severe loss; when aided speech understanding is inadequate, escalate to a cochlear implant.
Cochlear implant is for severe–profound bilateral SNHL with poor benefit from optimally-fitted hearing aids; it bypasses the hair cells and stimulates the cochlear nerve directly.
No nerve, no CI: if the cochlear nerve is absent (NF2, nerve aplasia) or the cochlea is unimplantable (Michel, total ossification), an auditory brainstem implant stimulates the cochlear nucleus instead.

🔊 Conventional (air-conduction) hearing aids

These amplify sound into a normally-functioning outer/middle ear. Styles range from ; modern aids are , but have limits.

Styles: BTE (behind-the-ear; powerful, robust, paediatric default), RIC (receiver-in-canal; discreet), and in-the-ear types (ITE/ITC/CIC). All have a microphone, amplifier and receiver; choice balances gain, dexterity, cosmesis and canal size.
Capability & limits: digital aids give frequency-shaped gain, noise reduction and feedback control, but cannot restore very poor speech discrimination, may feed back with large perforations/draining ears, and need a patent canal — situations that push toward implants.

🦴 Bone-conduction devices

Bone-conduction (osseointegrated) devices transmit sound through the skull to the cochlea, bypassing a diseased or absent outer/middle ear. Indications include ; a is used in young children.

BAHA/osseointegrated devices: a titanium implant (percutaneous abutment or transcutaneous magnet) drives the skull. Ideal for congenital atresia, chronically discharging or post-mastoidectomy ears (where an air-conduction aid won't work), and single-sided deafness (routing sound to the contralateral cochlea).
Softband: a bone conductor on an elastic band gives infants and young children (e.g. bilateral atresia) early amplification within the critical period, before they are old enough for an implanted abutment.

🌀 Cochlear implant — the high-yield device

A cochlear implant has and works by . Candidacy is defined by , and there are specific .

Components: external — microphone, speech processor, transmitter coil; internal — a receiver-stimulator under the scalp and an electrode array inserted into the scala tympani of the cochlea. The coil transmits coded signals across the skin to the implant.
Mechanism: the processor converts sound into electrical pulses that the electrode array delivers tonotopically along the cochlea, directly stimulating the spiral ganglion/cochlear-nerve fibres and bypassing the missing hair cells. An intact cochlear nerve is therefore essential.
Candidacy: severe–profound bilateral SNHL with inadequate aided speech scores; best results with post-lingual adults and early-implanted congenitally deaf children (within the critical period). Pre-op CT (cochlear anatomy/ossification) and MRI (patent cochlea + present cochlear nerve) are mandatory.
Risks: facial-nerve stimulation, device failure/extrusion, infection, taste/balance change, loss of any residual hearing, and an increased meningitis risk — so pneumococcal vaccination is given. Bilateral implantation improves localisation and hearing in noise.

🧠 Auditory brainstem implant & CROS

When there is no usable cochlear nerve, an is used; for single-sided deafness, a reroutes sound.

ABI: an electrode placed on the cochlear nucleus of the brainstem, used in NF2 (bilateral vestibular schwannomas), cochlear-nerve aplasia, or a totally ossified cochlea. Outcomes are generally more modest than a cochlear implant (mainly sound awareness/lip-reading support).
Single-sided deafness: a CROS/BiCROS aid transmits sound from the dead side to the hearing ear; alternatives are a BAHA (bone routes sound across the skull) or, increasingly, a cochlear implant on the deaf side (the only option that restores true binaural hearing).

🔧 Reconstructive surgery & rehabilitation

Surgical options correct the conductive mechanism — — and all patients benefit from aural rehabilitation (lip-reading, assistive listening devices, communication tactics).

Surgery by lesion: stapedotomy for otosclerosis, tympanoplasty/ossiculoplasty for perforation/ossicular erosion, and atresiaplasty for congenital atresia (good Jahrsdoerfer score). These restore natural hearing where the ear is reconstructable, and are chosen before or alongside prosthetic options.

📈 Choosing & outcomes

The decision integrates audiometry, the state of each ear, age and patient goals. Conductive losses are highly correctable; cochlear implants transform severe–profound SNHL, especially with early intervention; bone-conduction devices solve the "unusable ear" and single-sided-deafness problems.

🎯 Demonstrate it like an examiner wants

Commit first, then reveal the model structure and build the chain step by step.

Prompt: "A 3-year-old with profound bilateral SNHL gets little benefit from hearing aids. How do you assess and rehabilitate?"

Your locked answer:
"Profound SNHL with poor aided benefit in a young child is a cochlear-implant indication, and timing is critical — implant early within the language-acquisition window."
Document severe–profound bilateral SNHL on age-appropriate audiometry/ABR with an adequate hearing-aid trial; assess development and family support via the MDT.
CT (cochlear anatomy, ossification, anomalies) and MRI (patent cochlea + present cochlear nerve); exclude active middle-ear disease; vaccinate against pneumococcus.
Cochlear implantation (bilateral if appropriate) with intensive auditory-verbal rehabilitation; if the nerve is absent, consider an ABI. Long-term audiology/speech-therapy follow-up.
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: In SNHL the cochlear hair cells are lost but the spiral-ganglion neurons often survive. A cochlear implant's intracochlear electrode delivers tonotopically-coded electrical pulses that directly depolarise these surviving nerve fibres, recreating a frequency map. Early implantation exploits cortical plasticity during the critical period, giving the best speech and language outcomes.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"A 60-year-old with profound bilateral SNHL struggles despite well-fitted aids." → CI assessment: aided speech scores, CT/MRI, counselling; cochlear implantation (consider bilateral); expect good speech outcomes given post-lingual onset and intact nerve.
"A post-mastoidectomy cavity that discharges intermittently." → an air-conduction aid feeds back/macerates; offer a bone-conduction device (BAHA) once disease is controlled, or address the cavity surgically first.
Sound → microphone → speech processor (codes the signal) → transmitter coil across the skin → internal receiver-stimulator → electrode array in the scala tympani → tonotopic electrical stimulation of cochlear-nerve fibres → brainstem/cortex.
Poor speech discrimination (cochlear "distortion"), recruitment/loudness intolerance, feedback with perforations/open canals, and very severe losses needing more gain than is comfortable. These signal a move toward implantable solutions.
After bilateral vestibular schwannoma surgery the cochlear nerves may be lost, so a cochlear implant won't work; an auditory brainstem implant on the cochlear nucleus is used (modest but useful sound awareness with lip-reading).
Surgeon, audiologist, speech-and-language therapist, teacher of the deaf, and psychologist. Post-op device mapping/programming and intensive auditory-verbal therapy are as important as the surgery for a good outcome, especially in children.
