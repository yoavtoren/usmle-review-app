👶👂 Congenital malformations of the ear

📖 Full topic explanation

Congenital ear malformations are best organised by embryological origin, because the outer and middle ear arise from the branchial apparatus while the inner ear arises from the otic placode. This separation is the single highest-yield principle: an atretic outer ear usually hides a normal cochlea, so the loss is conductive and treatable.

🗂️ Definition & classification

Malformations are classified by site (outer, middle, inner ear — often combined), by cause (genetic vs acquired/teratogenic), and by syndromic vs non-syndromic. About half of congenital sensorineural loss is ; of that, ~70% is non-syndromic and ~30% syndromic; the remainder is (especially congenital CMV). A clean opening sentence — "I'll split this into outer/middle/inner, then genetic vs acquired" — frames the answer.

Genetic share: ~50% of congenital SNHL is genetic. Of the genetic group, ~70% is non-syndromic (mostly autosomal-recessive, the DFNB1/GJB2 connexin-26 locus) and ~30% is syndromic. Most non-syndromic deafness has no other clinical features, so the diagnosis is genetic.
Acquired (non-genetic): intrauterine TORCH infections — congenital CMV is the leading non-genetic cause — plus rubella; and perinatal factors (prematurity, hypoxia, hyperbilirubinaemia/kernicterus, ototoxic drugs, neonatal meningitis). CMV matters because the loss may be progressive/delayed and is treatable with neonatal valganciclovir.

🧬 Embryology — say this first

The (1st & 2nd arches); the (a solid meatal plug recanalises in the 7th month); the (footplate partly from the otic capsule); the middle-ear cavity and Eustachian tube from the 1st pharyngeal pouch endoderm; and the (otocyst → membranous labyrinth, with the bony labyrinth ossifying around it). Different origins explain why branchial-arch syndromes deform the outer/middle ear but commonly spare the cochlea.

Hillocks of His: hillocks 1–3 from the mandibular (1st) arch, 4–6 from the hyoid (2nd) arch, fuse around the first cleft. Faulty fusion → preauricular sinus/pit, accessory tags, microtia. The auricle is largely complete by ~20 weeks.
EAC recanalisation: ectoderm forms a solid epithelial plug that hollows out in month 7; failure → congenital aural atresia/stenosis, almost always with ossicular anomalies and frequently microtia.
Ossicles: malleus head & incus body from the 1st arch (Meckel); malleus handle, long process of incus and stapes superstructure from the 2nd arch (Reichert); the stapes footplate/vestibular surface from the otic capsule — so isolated footplate fixation can occur with a normal superstructure.
Otic placode origin: the cochlea and vestibular labyrinth develop independently of the arches. This is why a child with microtia/atresia typically has a normal cochlea and a purely conductive loss, and is a candidate for bone-conduction devices or atresia repair.

👂 Outer-ear anomalies

commonly accompany . Minor anomalies include accessory auricles, prominent ("bat") ears and Darwin's tubercle. A is a common pit that can become infected, and must be distinguished from a deeper that runs near the facial nerve.

Microtia grading: Grade 1 = small but recognisable auricle; Grade 2 = some structures + partial/absent canal; Grade 3 = "peanut" remnant (classic); anotia = complete absence. More common on the right and in males; associated with hemifacial microsomia (Goldenhar) and Treacher Collins.
Atresia → conductive loss ~50–60 dB, with preserved bone conduction (normal cochlea). The Jahrsdoerfer CT score (out of 10: stapes, oval/round windows, middle-ear space, facial-nerve course, ossicles, mastoid pneumatisation, external ear) grades candidacy for atresia repair; ≥7 favours surgery, lower scores favour a bone-anchored device.
Preauricular sinus: a blind epithelial tract at the ascending helix from incomplete hillock fusion; asymptomatic until infected (discharge/abscess). Treatment when recurrently infected is complete excision of the whole tract. Bilateral sinuses → screen for branchio-oto-renal syndrome.
Trap — first branchial cleft anomaly: a cyst/sinus near the angle of the mandible/parotid or opening into the EAC, whose tract has an intimate relationship with the facial nerve. Excision risks VII injury and needs formal nerve identification — not a simple "cyst removal".

🥁 Middle-ear anomalies

These include , , and dangerous that must be recognised before any middle-ear surgery.

Ossicular anomalies: congenital stapes footplate fixation or a discontinuous/absent long process of incus give a conductive loss behind an intact drum — a paediatric differential of otosclerosis, confirmed at exploratory tympanotomy.
Congenital cholesteatoma: a white pearl behind an intact drum, classically in the anterosuperior quadrant, in a child with no history of otorrhoea or perforation; from epithelial rests. Needs excision before it erodes the ossicles.
Operative hazards: a dehiscent facial nerve over the oval window, a high/dehiscent jugular bulb, or an aberrant internal carotid artery (pulsatile red middle-ear mass). Recognise these on CT before myringotomy or surgery to avoid catastrophic haemorrhage or facial palsy.

🌀 Inner-ear anomalies

Most are with a normal CT; bony malformations include , common cavity and cochlear hypoplasia. The commonest radiological anomaly is the , and a changes implant planning entirely.

Membranous dysplasias: Scheibe (cochleosaccular, the most common; normal bony labyrinth so CT is normal), Bing–Siebenmann, Alexander. Diagnosis is functional/genetic, not radiological.
Bony malformations: Michel aplasia = complete absence of the inner ear (no cochlear implant possible); Mondini = incomplete partition, only 1.5 turns (normal 2.5), with a CSF-gusher risk at stapes/cochleostomy. Common cavity and cochlear hypoplasia also occur.
Enlarged vestibular aqueduct (EVA/LVAS): aqueduct >1.5 mm; causes fluctuating, progressive, stepwise SNHL often triggered by minor head trauma. Counsel to avoid contact sports/barotrauma; strongly linked to SLC26A4/Pendred.
Cochlear-nerve aplasia: if the cochlear nerve is absent (best seen on MRI), a cochlear implant cannot work and an auditory brainstem implant is considered instead — which is why MRI is mandatory before paediatric implantation.

🧩 Genetics & syndromes

The commonest non-syndromic cause is . High-yield syndromes carry you must screen for, and several cause the microtia/atresia picture.

GJB2 (connexin-26): a gap-junction protein for cochlear K⁺ recycling; the commonest autosomal-recessive non-syndromic SNHL (DFNB1). Severity varies; targeted genetic testing is widely available.
Screen for: Jervell–Lange–Nielsen (KCNQ1/KCNE1 — SNHL + long QT → syncope/sudden death; do an ECG); Pendred (SLC26A4 — SNHL + EVA + goitre; perchlorate test); Usher (SNHL + retinitis pigmentosa); Alport (COL4A5 — SNHL + nephritis); branchio-oto-renal/EYA1 (branchial + otic + renal).
Craniofacial: Treacher Collins (TCOF1 — bilateral symmetric mandibulofacial dysostosis, microtia/atresia, down-slanting fissures/coloboma); Goldenhar (unilateral hemifacial microsomia + epibulbar dermoid + vertebral anomalies); CHARGE, Crouzon/Apert, Stickler and Waardenburg (PAX3 — white forelock, heterochromia, dystopia canthorum).

🔎 Presentation, screening & investigation

Hearing loss may be detected on or later through speech delay, and outer-ear anomalies are obvious at birth. Work-up combines with a .

Screening: OAEs (outer-hair-cell/cochlear function) and automated ABR aim to diagnose by 3 months and intervene by 6 months; a failed screen prompts diagnostic ABR and ENT/audiology referral. Early detection is what protects language development.
Imaging: HRCT defines bony anatomy (atresia, ossicles, EVA, facial-nerve course, Jahrsdoerfer score); MRI shows the membranous labyrinth and confirms a present cochlear nerve before implantation.
Syndrome screen: ECG (Jervell–Lange–Nielsen), renal ultrasound (BOR, Alport), ophthalmology/ERG (Usher), thyroid/perchlorate or genetics (Pendred), and a craniofacial/genetics assessment. The screen is driven by the associated features you find.

💊 Management

The overriding principle is . Options range from to , with surgical reconstruction for the outer ear and excision of infected sinuses, alongside .

Critical period: the auditory cortex needs early stimulation; untreated bilateral loss causes permanent speech/language delay. Amplification or implantation is therefore started in infancy — the timing is itself a high-yield exam point.
Conductive/atresia: conventional or bone-conduction aids; a softband BAHA in infancy, then an implanted BAHA or atresiaplasty (good Jahrsdoerfer score) later. Microtia is reconstructed with autologous rib cartilage at age 6–10 or a prosthesis/porous polyethylene.
Severe–profound SNHL: cochlear implantation, provided MRI confirms a cochlea and cochlear nerve (exclude Michel/nerve aplasia). Bilateral atresia gets bone-conduction amplification first to enable speech.
Genetic counselling: recurrence-risk advice (e.g. autosomal-recessive GJB2), connexin and syndrome-specific testing, and family screening (audiology for siblings). It also guides expectations about progression (e.g. EVA/Pendred).

📈 Prognosis

Conductive losses from atresia are usually correctable and, with early amplification, language develops normally. Severe SNHL outcomes depend on early implantation and an intact cochlear nerve. Progressive forms (EVA/Pendred, congenital CMV) need ongoing audiological surveillance.

🎯 Demonstrate it like an examiner wants

Commit first, then reveal the model structure and build the chain step by step.

Prompt: "A newborn fails the hearing screen and has a small right ear with no ear canal. How do you assess and manage?"

Your locked answer:
"This is microtia with congenital aural atresia. My priorities are to define the hearing in both ears, habilitate within the critical period, and screen for an associated syndrome — including an ECG for long-QT if there is any inner-ear loss."
Outer/middle ear are branchial-arch derivatives; the cochlea is from the otic placode, so atresia typically gives a ~60 dB conductive loss with a normal cochlea. Grade the microtia and look for hemifacial microsomia/Treacher Collins.
Diagnostic ABR for thresholds in each ear; HRCT temporal bone (Jahrsdoerfer score, facial-nerve course); MRI if SNHL/CI is considered. Screen associations: renal US, ECG, ophthalmology, genetics.
Bilateral atresia: early bone-conduction hearing aid (softband BAHA) to enable speech. Unilateral with a normal other ear: can monitor and aid later. Surgical options: atresiaplasty (good Jahrsdoerfer score) or implanted BAHA, with staged auricular reconstruction (rib cartilage age 6–10) or a prosthesis.
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: Failure of the 1st pharyngeal cleft to recanalise leaves a bony/membranous atresia plate, and faulty hillock fusion deforms the auricle. Sound cannot reach the otic-capsule-derived cochlea, which is itself normal, producing a maximal conductive loss. Because cortical auditory pathways require early stimulation, bone-conduction amplification is started in infancy to prevent permanent language delay.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"A newborn has Grade 3 right microtia with aural atresia and failed the screen on that side. The left ear is normal." → diagnostic ABR both ears; as the better ear hears, monitor, fit a softband BAHA if any concern; HRCT later for Jahrsdoerfer scoring; screen for hemifacial microsomia/Treacher Collins; plan reconstruction from age 6–10.
"A 5-year-old with congenital deafness has had two unexplained faints." → think Jervell–Lange–Nielsen: ECG for long QT, cardiology referral, β-blockade ± ICD. Never attribute syncope in a deaf child to "breath-holding" without an ECG.
~50% of congenital SNHL is genetic; of that, ~70% non-syndromic (mostly autosomal-recessive, DFNB1/GJB2 connexin-26) and ~30% syndromic. The rest are acquired (TORCH — esp. congenital CMV, the leading non-genetic cause; rubella; prematurity, hyperbilirubinaemia, ototoxic drugs, meningitis).
CT shows bony anatomy (Mondini, ossification after meningitis, facial-nerve course, incomplete partition → CSF gusher risk). MRI confirms a patent cochlea and present cochlear nerve — a cochlear-nerve aplasia or Michel aplasia contraindicates a standard implant.
A 10-point CT grading of candidacy for atresia repair: points for the stapes, oval window, round window, middle-ear space, facial-nerve course, malleus-incus complex, incus-stapes connection, mastoid pneumatisation and external-ear appearance. ≥7/10 predicts a good surgical hearing result; lower scores favour a BAHA.
Treat acute infection with antibiotics ± drainage, then elective complete excision of the entire tract when quiescent (supra-auricular/wide local approach, often with methylene-blue cannulation) to prevent recurrence. Screen for BOR if bilateral or with renal/branchial features.
It is the commonest non-genetic cause of congenital SNHL, the loss is often progressive and may be delayed, and it is the one cause that is treatable — neonatal valganciclovir can improve hearing outcomes. Diagnose within the first 3 weeks of life (saliva/urine PCR) before it is unprovable.
Explain the loss is progressive and fluctuating, can drop suddenly after head trauma or barotrauma; advise helmet sports avoidance, prompt review of any sudden change (consider steroids), serial audiograms, hearing aids, and cochlear implantation if it becomes profound. Test for Pendred.
