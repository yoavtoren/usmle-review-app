👂🔁 Chronic otitis media

The Overview is a full walk-through of the topic. Tap any coloured word for an extra exam pearl. Then work through all 5 tabs.

📖 Full topic explanation

Chronic suppurative otitis media (CSOM) is permanent disease of the middle-ear cleft with a non-healing perforation ± otorrhoea. The entire topic turns on one split: tubotympanic ("safe", mucosal) versus atticoantral ("unsafe", squamous = cholesteatoma), because only the latter erodes bone and causes lethal complications.
🔴 fatal/critical🟠 risk/demographic🔵 key context🟢 confirmatory🟣 trap

🗂️ Definition

CSOM is chronic inflammation of the middle ear and mastoid with a permanent tympanic-membrane perforation, with or without discharge, persisting beyond about 3 months. It is a major global cause of acquired hearing loss and of preventable intracranial sepsis. The first task is always to decide whether the ear is "safe" or "unsafe".

🔵 The fundamental classification

CSOM divides into and disease. The discriminators are the and the .

Tubotympanic ("safe"/mucosal): a central perforation of the pars tensa with mucosal disease; profuse, mucoid, non-foul discharge worsening with URTIs/water; conductive hearing loss. No cholesteatoma → low complication risk, but it still needs control of infection and repair.
Atticoantral ("unsafe"/squamous): defined by cholesteatoma with an attic or marginal (posterosuperior) perforation/retraction; scanty, foul, ± blood-stained discharge. Because cholesteatoma erodes bone, this causes ossicular destruction, labyrinthine fistula, facial palsy and intracranial complications.
Perforation site decides safety: a central perforation (drum remnant all around) = safe/mucosal; a marginal or attic perforation (reaching the bony annulus/pars flaccida) lets squamous epithelium migrate in → cholesteatoma = unsafe.
Discharge character: profuse, mucoid, odourless → mucosal disease; scanty, foul, cheesy, blood-stained → cholesteatoma (keratin + osteitis + anaerobes). The smell and small volume are the giveaways of unsafe disease.

⚙️ Aetiology & pathogenesis

Mucosal CSOM follows with ongoing Eustachian-tube dysfunction. Cholesteatoma arises by .

Mucosal disease: a perforation that fails to heal, recurrent infection through it, and chronic tubal dysfunction perpetuate middle-ear mucosal inflammation. Predisposed by childhood ear disease, poor access to care, and recurrent URTIs.
Cholesteatoma genesis: primary acquired — a pars-flaccida/attic retraction pocket (negative pressure) traps keratin (commonest); secondary acquired — squamous epithelium migrates through a marginal perforation; congenital — a white pearl behind an intact drum with no infection history.

🧀 Cholesteatoma

Cholesteatoma is — not a tumour, but locally destructive. It erodes bone by , threatening the ossicles, labyrinth, facial canal and tegmen.

Definition: misplaced keratinising squamous epithelium forming an expanding keratin sac in the middle ear/mastoid, chronically infected (Pseudomonas, anaerobes). It must be removed surgically.
Bone erosion: the matrix releases collagenases and lysosomal enzymes and activates osteoclasts (RANKL), with pressure necrosis from expansion. It erodes the scutum and ossicles (esp. long process of incus), the lateral semicircular canal (fistula), the facial canal (palsy) and the tegmen (intracranial spread).

Typical organisms are , which guides topical therapy and warns of cholesteatoma when the discharge is foul.

Organisms: Pseudomonas (commonest), Staph aureus, Proteus and anaerobes (the foul smell). This supports topical anti-pseudomonal quinolone therapy with aural toilet; anaerobic foul discharge points to cholesteatoma needing surgery.

👂 Clinical features & danger signs

Core features are . Alarm features signal complications: , and .

Symptoms: long-standing discharge and hearing loss through a perforation, ± tinnitus. Conductive loss dominates, but labyrinthine erosion or chronic toxins can add a sensorineural component (mixed loss).
Fistula sign: tragal pressure or pneumatic otoscopy provokes vertigo/nystagmus → a lateral semicircular canal fistula from cholesteatoma erosion. Urgent CT + surgery; handle matrix over the fistula carefully to avoid a dead ear.
Red flags = complication: facial palsy (eroded facial canal), vertigo (fistula/labyrinthitis), severe headache, fever, neck stiffness, drowsiness (meningitis/abscess), postauricular swelling (mastoiditis). Any of these → admit, image, treat aggressively.

🔎 Investigation

Assess with , adding to detect/monitor cholesteatoma.

Work-up: microsuction + otomicroscopy (perforation type, attic crust/keratin, ossicular status), fistula test, pure-tone audiometry, a swab for culture, and HRCT temporal bone mapping erosion (scutum, ossicles, fistula, tegmen, facial canal).
Non-EPI / DWI MRI shows restricted diffusion in cholesteatoma — used to detect residual/recurrent disease without a second-look operation, complementing CT (which shows bone).

💊 Management

Mucosal disease is treated ; , and topical drops are chosen with the in mind.

Tubotympanic: aural toilet + topical quinolone drops, keep dry, treat the nose/sinuses; once dry, repair with myringoplasty/tympanoplasty to stop recurrent infection and improve hearing. Outcomes are good and complications rare.
Cholesteatoma surgery is mandatory: mastoidectomy to eradicate disease — canal-wall-up (preserves anatomy, higher residual/recurrence, needs second-look/DWI) vs canal-wall-down (lower recurrence, open cavity needing lifelong care) — with tympanoplasty/ossiculoplasty for hearing. Medical treatment never cures cholesteatoma.
Drops caveat: prefer topical quinolones; aminoglycoside drops are potentially ototoxic through a perforation, so use them only briefly when needed for an actively discharging mucosal ear, with counselling. They do not treat cholesteatoma.

🔴 Complications & follow-up

Untreated unsafe disease causes (covered fully in Topic 11), and even after surgery, mandates long-term follow-up.

Complications: intratemporal — ossicular erosion/conductive loss, labyrinthine fistula & SNHL, facial palsy, mastoiditis, petrositis; intracranial — meningitis, extradural/subdural/brain abscess, lateral (sigmoid) sinus thrombosis, otitic hydrocephalus.
Why follow-up matters: residual (left-behind) and recurrent (new retraction) cholesteatoma are common, especially after canal-wall-up surgery. Surveillance with second-look surgery or DWI MRI catches it before complications recur; open cavities need regular cleaning.

📈 Prognosis

Mucosal CSOM has an excellent prognosis with toilet, topical therapy and tympanoplasty. Cholesteatoma is controllable but recurrence-prone, so the goals are a safe, dry ear first and improved hearing second, with lifelong surveillance.

🧠 Thinking questions (tap to reveal)

Safe: central perforation, profuse mucoid odourless discharge, no keratin. Unsafe: attic/marginal perforation or retraction with keratin/crust, scanty foul discharge, ± danger signs. Perforation site and the smell/volume of discharge are the keys.
Keratinising squamous epithelium trapped in the middle ear, expanding and releasing enzymes that erode bone. That erosion destroys ossicles and can breach the labyrinth, facial canal and tegmen → deafness, vertigo, facial palsy and intracranial sepsis.
Positive fistula sign → a lateral semicircular canal fistula eroded by cholesteatoma. Urgent HRCT and surgery, with careful matrix handling over the fistula.
CWU: preserves anatomy/hearing-aid use but higher residual/recurrence → planned second-look or DWI MRI. CWD: open mastoid cavity, lower recurrence and better visualisation, but needs lifelong cavity care and water precautions.
Bacterial toxins and inflammatory mediators cross the round window, and cholesteatoma can erode the labyrinth, damaging the cochlea → a mixed loss. A reason to treat chronic discharge rather than tolerate it.
Two goals: (1) eradicate disease → a safe dry ear (priority); (2) reconstruct hearing (graft the drum, ossiculoplasty). Wullstein types I–V classify the level of reconstruction.

🎯 Demonstrate it like an examiner wants

Commit first, then reveal the model structure and build the chain step by step.

Prompt: "A 30-year-old has years of scanty, foul-smelling right ear discharge and hearing loss, with crusting in the attic. How do you proceed?"

Your locked answer:
"Scanty, foul discharge with attic crusting is atticoantral CSOM with cholesteatoma until proven otherwise — an 'unsafe' ear that can cause intracranial complications, so it needs surgery."
Define CSOM and the safe/unsafe split; define cholesteatoma as trapped keratinising squamous epithelium that erodes bone. State the likely primary-acquired (retraction-pocket) origin.
Otomicroscopy (attic retraction/keratin, ossicular status), fistula test, cranial-nerve & cerebellar screen, audiometry, swab, HRCT temporal bone (erosion, fistula, tegmen, facial canal), MRI-DWI for extent/recurrence.
Initial aural toilet/antibiotics to settle infection, then definitive surgery — mastoidectomy (CWU vs CWD) + tympanoplasty/ossiculoplasty to eradicate cholesteatoma and reconstruct hearing. Treat/along-the-way manage any complication. Long-term follow-up for recurrence.
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: Chronic Eustachian-tube dysfunction retracts the pars flaccida into a pocket that traps desquamated keratin, forming cholesteatoma. Its matrix secretes collagenases and recruits osteoclasts, eroding the scutum, ossicles and surrounding bony walls. Once it breaches the labyrinth, facial canal or tegmen, it produces vertigo, facial palsy or intracranial sepsis — the hallmarks of "unsafe" disease.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"A young adult has a long history of profuse, odourless mucoid discharge through a large central perforation, with a 30 dB conductive loss and no danger signs." → tubotympanic CSOM: aural toilet + quinolone drops, keep dry, treat nasal disease; once dry, myringoplasty/tympanoplasty to close the perforation and improve hearing.
"Foul scanty discharge, attic keratin, now with vertigo and a positive fistula test." → cholesteatoma with a labyrinthine fistula: HRCT, admit, IV antibiotics if labyrinthitis/sepsis, and mastoid surgery with careful matrix removal over the fistula. Screen for intracranial spread.
Intratemporal: ossicular erosion/conductive loss, labyrinthine fistula & SNHL, facial palsy, mastoiditis, petrositis. Intracranial: meningitis, extradural/subdural abscess, brain abscess (temporal lobe/cerebellum), lateral (sigmoid) sinus thrombosis, otitic hydrocephalus. (See Topic 11.)
Two goals: (1) eradicate disease and create a safe, dry ear; (2) reconstruct the hearing mechanism (graft the drum, ossiculoplasty). Disease eradication takes priority over hearing. Wullstein classified tympanoplasty types I–V by reconstruction level.
A combination of pressure necrosis from the expanding sac and biochemical osteolysis — matrix-derived collagenases, lysosomal enzymes and cytokines (RANKL) activating osteoclasts. Chronic infection accelerates it. This explains the relentless bony destruction despite no malignancy.
Residual disease (left-behind matrix) and recurrent disease (new retraction) are common, especially after canal-wall-up surgery. Surveillance with second-look surgery or non-EPI DWI MRI detects it early before complications recur. Open cavities also need regular cleaning.
