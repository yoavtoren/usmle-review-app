📖 Full topic explanation

This topic covers the oesophageal problems that reach ENT: foreign bodies and food bolus, caustic ingestion, perforation, the pharyngeal pouch, and reflux. The unifying anatomy is the three physiological constrictions where things lodge and tear, and the unifying danger is perforation/mediastinitis.

🧬 Anatomy — three constrictions

The oesophagus has that are the sites of foreign-body impaction and injury, the uppermost being the .

Constrictions (approx. distance from incisors): cricopharyngeus / upper sphincter (~15 cm), the aortic arch & left main bronchus crossing (~25 cm), and the diaphragmatic hiatus / lower sphincter (~40 cm). Foreign bodies and corrosive damage concentrate at these points.
Cricopharyngeus: the narrowest point and the commonest site of foreign-body/food-bolus impaction. It is also the muscle whose dysfunction underlies cricopharyngeal bars and (with Killian's dehiscence) the pharyngeal pouch.

🪙 Foreign bodies & food bolus

Impaction usually occurs at the ; two scenarios are emergencies — a and a .

Presentation: sudden dysphagia/odynophagia, drooling, the sensation of something stuck, ± retrosternal pain after swallowing a bolus or object. A food bolus impacting in an adult may reveal an underlying stricture or eosinophilic oesophagitis that needs later endoscopy.
Button battery = emergency: an oesophageal battery causes liquefactive (alkaline) necrosis within hours, risking perforation and tracheo-oesophageal/aorto-oesophageal fistula. Remove endoscopically without delay; do not wait.
Other emergencies: sharp objects, complete obstruction with inability to swallow saliva (drooling), or airway compromise require urgent rigid/flexible endoscopic removal. A smooth distal coin/bolus in a stable patient may be observed briefly; glucagon is of limited benefit.

🧪 Caustic ingestion

Acid and alkali ingestion cause , are graded by , and carry late ; several .

Acid vs alkali: alkalis cause liquefactive necrosis that penetrates deeply (worse, transmural, higher perforation risk); acids cause coagulative necrosis with a protective eschar (more gastric injury). Both can be life-threatening; assess airway and circulation first.
Assessment: early flexible endoscopy (within ~12–24 h) grades the burn (Zargar grade) and guides management; CT assesses transmural injury. Examine the oropharynx/larynx for airway burns.
Sequelae: healing causes oesophageal strictures (weeks–months → dysphagia, needing dilatation) and a long-term increased risk of oesophageal squamous carcinoma (surveillance years later).
Traps — do NOT: induce vomiting, attempt chemical neutralisation, or pass a blind nasogastric tube (re-exposure/perforation risk). Avoid early dilatation of acute burns. Secure the airway early if there are oropharyngeal/laryngeal burns.

💥 Oesophageal perforation

Perforation — — is a surgical emergency presenting with .

Causes: iatrogenic (endoscopy/dilatation — commonest), Boerhaave syndrome (spontaneous transmural rupture after forceful vomiting), foreign body/caustic, and trauma. Mortality rises sharply with delay.
Features & diagnosis: severe chest/neck pain, odynophagia, surgical emphysema (crepitus), and Hamman's crunch; Mackler's triad (vomiting, chest pain, subcutaneous emphysema) for Boerhaave. CXR (pneumomediastinum/effusion), water-soluble contrast swallow and CT confirm. Treat with NBM, IV antibiotics, drainage and surgical/endoscopic repair.

👜 Pharyngeal pouch & reflux (LPR)

ENT-specific entities are and .

Pharyngeal (Zenker's) pouch: a pulsion diverticulum through Killian's dehiscence (between thyropharyngeus and cricopharyngeus) from cricopharyngeal dysfunction. Presents with regurgitation of undigested food, halitosis, gurgling and aspiration; diagnose by barium swallow (avoid blind endoscopy/NG — perforation risk); treat by endoscopic stapling diverticulotomy or open excision. Long-standing pouches rarely harbour SCC.
Laryngopharyngeal reflux: retrograde acid/pepsin reaching the larynx/pharynx causes hoarseness, globus, chronic cough, throat clearing and posterior laryngitis, often without heartburn. Managed with lifestyle measures and a PPI trial after excluding red-flag pathology.

📈 Management principle & prognosis

Across these conditions the priorities are the airway, prompt endoscopy (diagnostic and therapeutic), and early recognition of perforation/mediastinitis. Outcomes are good with timely removal/repair but deteriorate rapidly with delay; caustic injuries leave long-term strictures and a cancer risk.

🎯 Demonstrate it like an examiner wants

Commit first, then reveal the model structure and build the chain step by step.

Prompt: "A toddler is brought in drooling after possibly swallowing a coin-like object. How do you proceed?"

Your locked answer:
"Drooling means complete obstruction, and a 'coin' could be a button battery — a time-critical emergency causing liquefactive necrosis. Airway first, then urgent imaging and removal."
ABC/airway and drooling assessment; plain radiograph (AP + lateral) — a battery shows a "double-ring/halo" sign distinguishing it from a coin and locates it.
If battery, sharp object, or complete obstruction → urgent endoscopic removal. A smooth distal coin in a stable, swallowing child may be observed briefly, but oesophageal batteries are removed immediately.
Endoscopic removal under GA; inspect for mucosal injury; observe for delayed complications (perforation, fistula, stricture). Counsel on prevention (battery safety).
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: A lodged button battery generates a local current and leaches alkali, hydrolysing tissue (liquefactive necrosis) within hours. The narrow cricopharyngeus traps the battery against the mucosa, and progressive necrosis can perforate into the mediastinum or erode into the trachea or aorta. Immediate removal halts the electrochemical injury before these catastrophic fistulae form.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"An adult cannot swallow saliva after a piece of steak; chest discomfort." → assess airway/drooling; if complete obstruction, urgent endoscopic disimpaction; afterwards endoscopy + biopsies to find the cause (stricture/EoE/tumour).
"A child swallowed drain cleaner; oral burns and drooling." → ABC/airway, NBM, IV fluids/analgesia; early endoscopy to grade; no emesis/neutralisation/blind NG; watch for perforation; plan stricture surveillance and long-term cancer follow-up.
NBM, IV broad-spectrum antibiotics + antifungal, fluid resuscitation, drainage of collections, and definitive repair — surgical repair for large/early perforations or endoscopic stenting/clipping for selected contained leaks. Mortality rises with delay, so early diagnosis is key.
Food collects in the pouch and is regurgitated, especially when lying flat, causing recurrent aspiration; the obstruction and avoidance of eating cause weight loss. These, plus the small SCC risk, justify surgical treatment.
A rigid diverticuloscope exposes the common wall (party wall) between the pouch and oesophagus, and a stapler divides it, marsupialising the pouch and dividing the cricopharyngeus. It is quicker with less morbidity than open excision but needs adequate neck extension/exposure.
With a retained sharp/battery foreign body or after caustic injury, a herald arterial bleed followed by massive haematemesis (Chiari's triad: mid-thoracic pain, sentinel haemorrhage, then exsanguination after an interval) suggests an aorto-oesophageal fistula — a surgical emergency.
