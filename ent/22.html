🍽️ Differential diagnosis of swallowing disorders

The Overview is a full walk-through of the topic. Tap any coloured word for an extra exam pearl. Then work through all 5 tabs.

📖 Full topic explanation

Dysphagia is difficulty swallowing. The framework is two questions: oropharyngeal (transfer) or oesophageal (transport)? and mechanical (obstructing) or neuromuscular (motility)? Above all, progressive dysphagia for solids with weight loss is cancer until excluded by endoscopy.

🗂️ Define & localise

Distinguish true , then localise to , and characterise .

Definitions: dysphagia = difficulty moving the bolus; odynophagia = painful swallowing (suggests inflammation/ulcer/infection/malignancy); globus = a persistent lump sensation without true swallowing difficulty (often reflux/anxiety-related, but still needs red-flag screening).
Oropharyngeal (transfer) vs oesophageal (transport): oropharyngeal = difficulty initiating the swallow, with coughing/choking, nasal regurgitation and aspiration (problem at mouth/pharynx/upper sphincter); oesophageal = food "sticking" seconds after swallowing, felt retrosternally (problem in the oesophageal body/lower sphincter).
Mechanical vs motility: mechanical/obstructing lesions cause solids worse than liquids, often progressive; motility disorders cause solids AND liquids equally from the start, often intermittent. This single distinction reshapes the differential.

👄 Oropharyngeal dysphagia

Causes are or , and the key danger is .

Neuromuscular: stroke (commonest), Parkinson's disease, motor neurone disease, myasthenia gravis (fatigable), multiple sclerosis, and bulbar palsies. These impair the coordinated transfer phase, causing coughing, choking and nasal regurgitation.
Structural: pharyngeal (Zenker's) pouch (regurgitation, halitosis, gurgling), post-cricoid or oropharyngeal/hypopharyngeal tumour, cricopharyngeal bar/spasm, retropharyngeal mass, and post-radiation/surgical fibrosis. A pouch and post-cricoid carcinoma are classic ENT causes.
Aspiration: failure to protect the airway during transfer causes aspiration pneumonia and is the major morbidity/mortality of oropharyngeal dysphagia. Assess swallow safety (cough, wet voice) and consider NBM + alternative feeding while investigating.

🔻 Oesophageal dysphagia

Oesophageal causes split into (the cancer group) and .

Mechanical: oesophageal carcinoma (progressive solid dysphagia + weight loss — the must-exclude), peptic stricture (GORD history), rings/webs (Plummer–Vinson: post-cricoid web + iron deficiency, premalignant), eosinophilic oesophagitis (young atopic, food impaction), and extrinsic compression (mediastinal mass, enlarged left atrium, goitre, vascular).
Motility: achalasia (failure of LES relaxation + aperistalsis → dysphagia for solids and liquids, regurgitation, "bird-beak" on barium), diffuse oesophageal spasm (intermittent chest pain/dysphagia, "corkscrew"), and systemic sclerosis (absent peristalsis + reflux). Motility disorders affect liquids and solids together.

🚩 Red flags

Certain features mandate .

Red flags: progressive dysphagia (solids → liquids), weight loss, older age, odynophagia, anaemia/GI bleeding, a neck/supraclavicular node, hoarseness, or referred otalgia. Any of these → urgent upper GI endoscopy / 2-week-wait referral for oesophageal or head-and-neck cancer.

🔎 Investigation

Assess with , then targeted .

Bedside/ENT: history (solids vs liquids, progressive vs intermittent, red flags), flexible nasendoscopy (pooling of saliva in the pyriform fossae suggests obstruction/post-cricoid lesion), cranial-nerve/neuro exam, and a swallow assessment (speech-and-language therapist) for safety.
Investigations: upper GI endoscopy (first-line for suspected mechanical/oesophageal cause — allows biopsy), videofluoroscopy/FEES (oropharyngeal/aspiration assessment), barium swallow (pouch, achalasia, strictures), manometry/pH (motility/reflux), and cross-sectional imaging for tumours.

💊 Management principle

Treat the cause and protect against in the interim.

Supportive + specific: ensure safe swallow / alternative feeding (modified diet, NG/PEG) and nutrition while investigating; then treat the cause — cancer (Topics 37–44), pouch surgery, dilatation for strictures/webs, achalasia (pneumatic dilatation/Heller myotomy/POEM), reflux (PPI), and rehabilitation for neuromuscular dysphagia.

📈 Prognosis

Prognosis depends entirely on the cause: benign strictures/webs/pouches and reflux respond well; neuromuscular dysphagia needs rehabilitation and aspiration prevention; malignancy prognosis hinges on early detection — hence the low threshold for endoscopy.

🧠 Thinking questions (tap to reveal)

Two questions: oropharyngeal (transfer) vs oesophageal (transport), and mechanical (solids worse, progressive) vs motility (solids + liquids, intermittent). Then screen for red flags mandating endoscopy.
Oesophageal carcinoma — a mechanical, progressive obstruction. Urgent upper GI endoscopy with biopsy is mandatory; do not attribute to reflux/age without scoping.
A motility disorder (e.g. achalasia — also regurgitation, "bird-beak" on barium, abnormal manometry; or diffuse oesophageal spasm). Liquids being affected equally points away from a simple obstructing lesion.
On nasendoscopy, pooling of secretions in the pyriform fossae/postcricoid region suggests a hypopharyngeal obstruction or post-cricoid carcinoma and reduced airway protection; it is an indication for urgent imaging/panendoscopy and biopsy.
Impaired airway protection during the transfer phase leads to aspiration pneumonia, the main cause of morbidity/death (e.g. post-stroke). Assess swallow safety and use modified diet or alternative feeding while the cause is treated.
A post-cricoid oesophageal web + iron-deficiency anaemia + dysphagia (classically middle-aged women). It is premalignant for post-cricoid SCC, so it needs treatment (iron, dilatation) and surveillance.

🎯 Demonstrate it like an examiner wants

Commit first, then reveal the model structure and build the chain step by step.

Prompt: "A 68-year-old smoker has 6 weeks of worsening dysphagia for solids, weight loss and referred ear pain. How do you proceed?"

Your locked answer:
"Progressive solid dysphagia with weight loss and referred otalgia in a smoker is a hypopharyngeal/oesophageal carcinoma until excluded — urgent (2-week-wait) work-up."
Solids-worse + progressive = mechanical obstruction; referred otalgia (CN IX/X) and pooling on nasendoscopy point to a hypopharyngeal (post-cricoid) lesion.
Flexible nasendoscopy (pooling, lesion), neck exam (nodes); upper GI endoscopy + biopsy and/or panendoscopy; cross-sectional imaging (CT neck/chest) and nutrition assessment.
Refer to the head-and-neck/upper-GI MDT; ensure safe nutrition meanwhile; treat the confirmed cancer by stage (surgery/chemoradiotherapy). Manage aspiration risk.
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: A progressively enlarging mucosal tumour narrows the lumen, so larger solid boluses are obstructed first and liquids last — the hallmark of mechanical dysphagia. Local infiltration of sensory nerves (glossopharyngeal/vagal) refers pain to the ear. Reduced intake and tumour catabolism produce the weight loss that accompanies malignant obstruction.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"An elderly man has regurgitation of undigested food, halitosis, a gurgling neck and aspiration." → Zenker's pharyngeal pouch: barium swallow (avoid blind endoscopy/NG — perforation risk), then endoscopic stapling diverticulotomy or open repair.
"A stroke patient coughs on fluids with a wet voice." → oropharyngeal dysphagia with aspiration risk: NBM + SALT swallow assessment, modified diet/thickened fluids or NG feeding, videofluoroscopy/FEES, and rehabilitation.
The instrument can enter and perforate the pouch rather than the true lumen. Diagnose with a contrast (barium) swallow first; endoscopy is then done carefully by an experienced operator.
Dysphagia for solids+liquids, regurgitation, weight loss; barium "bird-beak", endoscopy (exclude pseudoachalasia/tumour), and manometry (absent peristalsis + failed LES relaxation). Treat with pneumatic dilatation, Heller myotomy or POEM (± botulinum toxin in the frail).
As globus, throat clearing, chronic cough, hoarseness and posterior laryngitis (arytenoid/interarytenoid oedema on laryngoscopy), often without classic heartburn. Managed with lifestyle measures and a PPI trial after excluding red flags.
Progressive dysphagia, weight loss, age >~55, odynophagia, anaemia/GI bleeding, a neck node, hoarseness or referred otalgia — any of these triggers an urgent cancer pathway (upper-GI ± head-and-neck).
