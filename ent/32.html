🩸 Epistaxis

📖 Full topic explanation

Epistaxis (nosebleed) is best approached by anterior vs posterior bleeding, its vascular anatomy, and a clear escalating treatment ladder from first aid to arterial ligation. Resuscitate the heavy bleeder first, and never forget the tumour hiding behind a recurrent unilateral bleed.

🧬 Vascular anatomy

Most bleeds arise anteriorly from ; severe posterior bleeds come from the , and the add an internal-carotid contribution.

Little's area / Kiesselbach's plexus: an anastomosis on the anterior nasal septum of the anterior ethmoidal, sphenopalatine, greater palatine and superior labial arteries. It is exposed and easily traumatised, so it is the source of most (anterior) nosebleeds, especially in children.
Sphenopalatine artery: the terminal branch of the maxillary artery (external carotid), supplying the posterior nasal cavity. Posterior bleeds from it are heavier, harder to control, and risk airway/aspiration — typically in older, hypertensive or anticoagulated patients.
Ethmoidal arteries: the anterior and posterior ethmoidal arteries arise from the ophthalmic artery (internal carotid). They supply the superior nasal cavity and are relevant in trauma and in high/superior bleeds (and as a ligation target).

🗂️ Anterior vs posterior

Classify as or — this guides management.

Anterior: bleeding from Little's area, usually a visible anterior septal point; common in children and young adults, often mild and self-limiting, and amenable to first aid and cautery.
Posterior: bleeding back from the sphenopalatine territory, with blood in the throat, bleeding from both nares, no visible anterior source; older/hypertensive/anticoagulated patients, heavier and harder to control, with airway/aspiration risk — needs packing/surgery and admission.

🩺 Causes

Causes are or , with two crucial "don't-miss" causes: .

Local: digital trauma (nose-picking) and dry air (commonest, esp. children), infection/rhinitis, foreign body, septal deviation/perforation/spur, post-surgical, and neoplasm.
Systemic: hypertension (contributes/worsens), anticoagulants/antiplatelets, coagulopathies (haemophilia, thrombocytopenia, liver disease), and hereditary haemorrhagic telangiectasia. Always review medications and clotting.
Don't-miss: recurrent unilateral epistaxis with nasal obstruction in an adolescent male → juvenile nasopharyngeal angiofibroma (do NOT biopsy — image; Topic 54); a unilateral nasal mass in an adult → sinonasal carcinoma. HHT (Osler–Weber–Rendu) causes recurrent epistaxis with mucocutaneous telangiectasia and a family history.

💉 Management ladder

Management escalates from to , , and finally , while .

Resuscitate + first aid: for heavy bleeding follow ABC (IV access, bloods/crossmatch, fluids). First aid: sit up and lean forward, pinch the soft (cartilaginous) part of the nose for 10–20 min, ice/cold; spit out blood. Most anterior bleeds stop with this.
Cautery: if a discrete anterior bleeding point is visible, silver-nitrate chemical cautery (or electrocautery) after decongestion/local anaesthetic. Do not cauterise both sides of the septum at the same visit (risk of septal perforation).
Packing: if cautery fails or no point is seen, place an anterior pack (e.g. Rapid Rhino/Merocel/ribbon gauze). Posterior bleeds need a posterior pack or a balloon catheter (Foley); admit, and beware airway compromise and the (debated) "nasopulmonary reflex".
Surgical/endovascular: for refractory bleeding, endoscopic sphenopalatine artery ligation (and anterior ethmoidal artery ligation for high bleeds) or arterial embolisation (maxillary artery) by interventional radiology.
Correct the systemic factor: manage hypertension, and review/reverse anticoagulation as appropriate (balancing thrombotic risk), correct coagulopathy/platelets. This is an essential parallel step, not an afterthought.

📈 Prognosis

Most anterior epistaxis is benign and stops with first aid/cautery. Posterior bleeds and those on anticoagulants/HHT are more troublesome and may need packing, surgery or embolisation. The clinical priorities are resuscitation, controlling the bleed by the ladder, and excluding a tumour in recurrent unilateral disease.

🎯 Demonstrate it like an examiner wants

Commit first, then reveal the model structure and build the chain step by step.

Prompt: "An anticoagulated 75-year-old has heavy bleeding from both nostrils with blood running into the throat. Manage."

Your locked answer:
"Heavy bilateral bleeding with blood in the throat suggests a posterior bleed in an anticoagulated elderly patient — I start with ABC and resuscitation, then control the bleeding."
IV access, bloods/crossmatch, fluids; check INR/clotting and reverse anticoagulation as appropriate; control BP. Sit forward, apply first aid while preparing.
First aid → if no anterior point and ongoing bleeding, place an anterior pack; for a posterior bleed, a posterior pack/balloon and admit (airway monitoring).
If bleeding persists → endoscopic sphenopalatine artery ligation (± anterior ethmoidal) or embolisation; ongoing management of anticoagulation/hypertension and the underlying cause.
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: Posterior bleeding usually arises from the sphenopalatine artery, a high-flow branch of the maxillary artery, so it is brisk and tracks into the nasopharynx and throat. Anticoagulation and hypertension impair haemostasis and raise vessel pressure, perpetuating the bleed. Tamponade (packing/balloon) and, if needed, ligation/embolisation of the sphenopalatine artery occlude the feeding vessel to stop the haemorrhage.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"A child with recurrent small nosebleeds from nose-picking; a visible vessel on the anterior septum." → first aid education, treat dryness (ointment), silver-nitrate cautery of the single point (one side only); reassure.
"Heavy bilateral bleed, blood in the throat, on a DOAC." → resuscitate, correct anticoagulation, posterior pack/balloon, admit; if persistent, endoscopic SPA ligation or embolisation; manage BP and the underlying cause.
Airway compromise, dislodgement/aspiration, pressure necrosis of the septum/ala, sinusitis, toxic shock (consider antibiotic cover for prolonged packs), and discomfort. Posterior packs especially require admission and monitoring.
Beyond acute control: humidification/moisturisers, avoiding trauma, laser ablation of telangiectasia, and in severe cases antifibrinolytics, bevacizumab or septodermoplasty/Young's procedure. Screen for and manage visceral AVMs.
Embolisation (of the maxillary/SPA territory) is useful when surgery is unsuitable or has failed, or in tumours (pre-operative devascularisation, e.g. angiofibroma). Risks include stroke and facial/ocular ischaemia; ethmoidal (ICA) bleeds are generally ligated, not embolised, given stroke risk.
Hypertension is associated with, and worsens/perpetuates, epistaxis rather than being a sole cause; acute anxiety also raises BP during a bleed. Control BP as part of management, but still look for a local source and systemic contributors.
