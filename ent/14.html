📖 Full topic explanation

Tinnitus is the perception of sound without an external source. The whole topic hinges on two splits: subjective vs objective, and pulsatile vs non-pulsatile — because they decide which patient is reassured and which needs imaging for a vascular lesion or a tumour.

🗂️ Definition & classification

Classify first as versus , and separately as . These axes drive the entire work-up.

Subjective tinnitus (the vast majority) has no acoustic source and usually accompanies hearing loss. It reflects altered auditory processing rather than a sound in the ear, which is why it is heard only by the patient.
Objective tinnitus is a real sound generated within the body (vascular flow, muscle clicking) that can be heard by the examiner with a stethoscope. It is rare but important because it points to a specific, often treatable, structural cause.
Pulsatile vs non-pulsatile: pulsatile tinnitus (in time with the heartbeat) suggests a vascular cause and warrants imaging; non-pulsatile tinnitus is usually subjective and associated with hearing loss. This distinction is the highest-yield triage step.

🧬 Pathophysiology of subjective tinnitus

Subjective tinnitus is best understood as following cochlear damage, with of distress.

Central gain: loss of cochlear input (e.g. noise/presbycusis) reduces afferent signalling; the central auditory system up-regulates its gain and reorganises tonotopically, generating a phantom percept. This is why tinnitus is "in the brain" as much as the ear, and why hearing aids/sound therapy help.
Distress network: limbic and autonomic systems attach emotional salience to the percept, so anxiety, poor sleep and attention worsen it — the rationale for CBT and tinnitus retraining therapy, which target the reaction rather than the sound.

🔔 Causes of subjective tinnitus

Most subjective tinnitus accompanies a (noise, presbycusis, ototoxicity, Ménière's, otosclerosis), but a .

Common associations: noise-induced and age-related hearing loss (commonest), ototoxic drugs, Ménière's disease, otosclerosis, wax/middle-ear disease, and head/neck injury. Treating or rehabilitating the hearing loss is often the most effective intervention.
Unilateral tinnitus red flag: persistent one-sided tinnitus, especially with asymmetric SNHL, must prompt MRI of the internal auditory meatus to exclude a vestibular schwannoma — the key "don't miss".

🩸 Objective & pulsatile tinnitus

Pulsatile tinnitus points to a ; rhythmic non-vascular clicking points to . A retrotympanic must never be biopsied blindly.

Pulsatile (vascular): glomus tumours (jugulare/tympanicum), dural arteriovenous fistula, carotid stenosis/dissection, a venous hum, a high/dehiscent jugular bulb or aberrant carotid, and idiopathic intracranial hypertension (typically a young obese woman with headache/visual symptoms). Investigate with otoscopy + CT/MR angiography/venography.
Rhythmic clicking (objective, non-vascular): palatal myoclonus (rapid regular clicks from palatal muscle contraction), middle-ear myoclonus (tensor tympani/stapedius), and a patulous Eustachian tube (autophony, breathing-synchronous sound that eases when lying down).
Trap — retrotympanic mass: a red/pulsatile mass behind the drum (glomus, aberrant carotid, high jugular bulb) must be imaged before any myringotomy/biopsy — biopsy can cause catastrophic bleeding (Brown sign = blanching on pneumatic pressure suggests glomus).

🔎 Assessment

Take a focused , then , and quantify .

History/exam: pulsatile vs not, unilateral vs bilateral, hearing/vertigo, drugs, noise, sleep/mood; otoscopy, auscultation of the ear/mastoid/neck/orbit (for bruits), cranial nerves, and the neck. A pulsatile or objective sound changes the pathway immediately.
Investigations: pure-tone audiometry for all; MRI IAM for unilateral/asymmetric tinnitus; CT/MR angiography & venography for pulsatile tinnitus; consider fundoscopy/LP if IIH is suspected. Most bilateral non-pulsatile tinnitus with symmetrical loss needs no imaging.
Quantify impact: use a validated questionnaire (e.g. Tinnitus Handicap Inventory) and screen for anxiety, depression and insomnia, because distress — not loudness — drives the disability and guides therapy.

💊 Management

Treat any underlying cause, then use , with ; .

First-line: reassurance and education (explaining central gain reduces fear), hearing aids if there is hearing loss (amplifying ambient sound reduces the percept), and sound therapy/maskers. Address sleep and reduce excessive silence.
For distress: cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT) has the best evidence for reducing tinnitus-related distress, alongside tinnitus retraining therapy (TRT) which combines counselling with sound enrichment to promote habituation.
No cure drug: no medication reliably abolishes tinnitus; antidepressants/anxiolytics treat comorbid mood/sleep, not the tinnitus itself. Manage realistic expectations and treat specific causes (e.g. glomus, IIH, dural fistula).

📈 Prognosis

Most subjective tinnitus habituates over time, especially with hearing rehabilitation and CBT; objective and pulsatile causes are treated according to the specific lesion. The clinical priority is to catch the dangerous causes (schwannoma, glomus, dural fistula, IIH) and to relieve distress.

🧠 Thinking questions (tap to reveal)

Subjective or objective? and pulsatile or not? Objective/pulsatile tinnitus points to a structural/vascular cause needing imaging (glomus, dural fistula, IIH), while subjective non-pulsatile tinnitus is usually benign and hearing-loss related.
Unilateral or markedly asymmetric tinnitus, especially with asymmetric SNHL → MRI of the IAM to exclude a vestibular schwannoma. Pulsatile tinnitus instead needs vascular imaging (CTA/MRA/MRV).
Glomus tumours, dural AV fistula, carotid stenosis/dissection, venous hum, high/dehiscent jugular bulb, aberrant carotid, and idiopathic intracranial hypertension. Examine for a bruit and a retrotympanic mass, then image the vessels.
Reduced cochlear input (hearing loss) leads the central auditory system to increase its gain and reorganise tonotopically, producing a phantom sound; limbic systems add distress. This explains why hearing aids and sound therapy help and why the tinnitus persists despite a normal ear exam.
Idiopathic intracranial hypertension — fundoscopy for papilloedema, MRI/MRV (exclude venous sinus thrombosis), and LP with opening pressure. Manage weight, acetazolamide, and protect vision; the tinnitus often resolves with treatment.
Cognitive behavioural therapy (CBT), alongside hearing aids/sound therapy and tinnitus retraining therapy. No drug reliably cures tinnitus; the target is habituation and reduced distress.

🎯 Demonstrate it like an examiner wants

Commit first, then reveal the model structure and build the chain step by step.

Prompt: "A 45-year-old describes a whooshing noise in the left ear in time with her pulse. How do you proceed?"

Your locked answer:
"Pulse-synchronous tinnitus is pulsatile tinnitus, which I treat as a vascular problem until imaging proves otherwise — it needs structural work-up, not just reassurance."
List glomus tumour, dural AV fistula, carotid stenosis/dissection, venous hum, jugular-bulb/carotid anomaly, and IIH. Decide which fit the history (age, headache, visual symptoms, neck signs).
Otoscopy (retrotympanic mass?), auscultate ear/mastoid/neck/orbit for a bruit, cranial nerves, BP; audiometry; CT/MR angiography + venography; fundoscopy ± LP if IIH suspected. Do not biopsy a vascular mass.
Manage per diagnosis — e.g. glomus (surgery/radiosurgery/embolisation), dural fistula (embolisation), carotid disease (vascular team), IIH (weight, acetazolamide, protect vision). Reassure if work-up is negative.
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: Turbulent or abnormal blood flow near the cochlea (a tumour, fistula, stenosis or bony dehiscence) creates a real acoustic signal transmitted to the inner ear, perceived in time with the heartbeat. Because the source is structural, it is potentially audible to the examiner and detectable on vascular imaging. Treatment therefore targets the underlying vascular lesion rather than the auditory perception.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"A 68-year-old has bilateral ringing and symmetrical high-frequency SNHL." → subjective tinnitus with presbycusis: reassure/educate, fit hearing aids, sound therapy and sleep advice; no imaging needed. Offer CBT if distressed.
"A pulsatile noise with a bluish pulsatile mass behind the drum." → suspect a glomus tympanicum/jugulare or vascular anomaly: image (CT/MR/angio) before any intervention; never myringotomise/biopsy blindly; refer to skull-base team.
Blanching/pulsation of a retrotympanic mass on positive pneumatic-otoscopy pressure, suggesting a glomus tumour. It is a clinical clue but does not replace imaging before any biopsy.
By amplifying ambient sound it restores afferent input and reduces the central gain that generates the phantom percept, and it makes the tinnitus relatively less noticeable. It is first-line whenever there is a co-existing hearing loss.
TRT combines directive counselling (reframing the tinnitus as non-threatening) with sound enrichment to promote habituation, so the brain stops attending to the signal. It works over months, complementing CBT.
High-dose aspirin/NSAIDs, quinine, aminoglycosides, loop diuretics, cisplatin — many are dose-related and reversible (salicylates/quinine) while others are permanently ototoxic. Always review the drug chart.
