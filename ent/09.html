👂🦴 Mastoiditis

📖 Full topic explanation

Acute mastoiditis is the commonest suppurative complication of acute otitis media — infection spreading from the middle ear into the mastoid air cells with breakdown of their bony septa. It is a clinical diagnosis with classic post-auricular signs, and it is the gateway to intracranial complications.

🧬 Anatomy & pathophysiology

The mastoid air cells communicate with the middle ear through the . In AOM the mucosa lining these cells inflames; if the aditus blocks, pus is trapped and develops as the bony septa dissolve. Pus then escapes along paths of least resistance to form .

Aditus ad antrum: the narrow channel linking the epitympanum to the mastoid antrum. Mucosal swelling here converts a simple "mastoid reaction" (mucosal inflammation, present in most AOM) into trapped empyema when drainage fails — the step that defines true mastoiditis.
Coalescent mastoiditis: trapped pus under pressure causes osteitis and resorption of the inter-cellular septa, coalescing the cells into an abscess cavity. This is the surgical form, distinct from the radiological "fluid in cells" seen in ordinary AOM.
Abscess routes: through the lateral mastoid cortex → postauricular subperiosteal abscess (commonest); through the mastoid tip into the sternocleidomastoid → Bezold abscess (neck swelling, torticollis); into the digastric groove or zygomatic root (Citelli/zygomatic); and into the deep neck.

🦠 Microbiology & demographics

The organisms mirror AOM — ; it is mainly a .

Organisms: S. pneumoniae is the classic, with group A streptococcus, H. influenzae and S. aureus; chronic/atypical cases add Pseudomonas and anaerobes. Pneumococcus (notably some serotypes) is most associated with aggressive coalescent disease.
Demographics: most cases are in children under 2 with their first or recurrent AOM. Despite antibiotics it remains the commonest suppurative AOM complication; incomplete/partial antibiotic treatment can produce a "masked" mastoiditis.

👂 Clinical features

Suspect mastoiditis when AOM , with the triad of , plus a . Beware .

Time course: persistent fever, deep otalgia and otorrhoea continuing or recurring beyond 10–14 days of AOM, or deterioration after apparent improvement, is the warning that infection has entered the mastoid.
The classic sign: a red, tender, fluctuant postauricular swelling over the mastoid that protrudes the pinna forward and downward and obliterates the postauricular crease — distinguishing it from simple lymphadenitis (which sits behind the crease and does not displace the pinna).
Otoscopy: a bulging/perforated drum with otorrhoea and a sagging posterosuperior (bony) canal wall caused by periosteitis of the medial canal — a near-pathognomonic sign of mastoid suppuration.
Masked mastoiditis (trap): partial antibiotic treatment can suppress the obvious signs while osteitis smoulders, presenting later with vague illness or a sudden intracranial complication. Maintain suspicion in a child with persistent low-grade symptoms after "treated" AOM.

🔎 Investigation

Diagnosis is clinical; confirm and plan with , adding .

Tests: raised WBC/CRP, blood cultures, and ear swab. HRCT temporal bone shows opacified coalescent air cells with loss of septa and any cortical breach/subperiosteal abscess; it guides surgery.
If complications suspected: contrast CT + MRI/MRV to detect intracranial abscess, meningitis or sigmoid sinus thrombosis. New neurology, severe headache, seizures or swinging fever mandate this before/with treatment.

💊 Management

Treat with ; perform for an abscess, failure to respond, or any complication.

First-line: admit, IV broad-spectrum antibiotics (e.g. ceftriaxone ± an anti-staph/anti-anaerobe agent, then culture-directed), and myringotomy ± grommet to drain the middle ear and obtain pus. Many early cases settle on this alone.
Cortical (simple) mastoidectomy drains the coalesced cells and any subperiosteal abscess; indicated for a subperiosteal/neck abscess, failure to improve in ~48 h, or an intracranial complication. It preserves the canal wall and middle ear, unlike cholesteatoma surgery.

🔴 Complications

Untreated mastoiditis spreads to give (covered fully in Topic 11).

Spread: facial-nerve palsy, labyrinthitis, petrous apicitis (Gradenigo's triad), and intracranial complications — meningitis, extradural/subdural/brain abscess and sigmoid (lateral) sinus thrombosis (picket-fence fever, Griesinger's sign). These convert a treatable infection into a life-threatening one.

🔄 Chronic mastoiditis

In the context of chronic suppurative OM/cholesteatoma, low-grade mastoid infection persists with chronic discharge; management is the surgery of the underlying chronic otitis (Topics 06–07), not just antibiotics.

📈 Prognosis

With prompt antibiotics ± surgery the prognosis is excellent. Delay — particularly in masked disease — risks hearing loss and the intracranial complications that still cause mortality.

🧠 Thinking questions (tap to reveal)

Most AOM shows fluid in mastoid cells on imaging (a mucosal reaction). True (coalescent) mastoiditis means trapped pus has caused osteitis with breakdown of the bony septa and clinical postauricular signs — that is the surgical disease.
Mastoiditis pushes the pinna down-and-out and obliterates the postauricular crease, with otoscopic AOM and a sagging canal wall. Lymphadenitis sits behind the crease, does not displace the pinna, and the ear exam is normal.
Pus eroding through the mastoid tip into the sternocleidomastoid, producing a tender neck swelling ± torticollis. It signals advanced disease and needs drainage and mastoidectomy.
Cortical mastoidectomy for a subperiosteal/neck abscess, failure to improve within ~48 h of IV antibiotics + myringotomy, or any intracranial complication. Otherwise many early cases settle medically.
Partial antibiotic treatment suppresses the obvious signs while osteitis continues silently, so the first presentation may be an intracranial complication. Keep a high index of suspicion in a child with persistent vague symptoms after "treated" AOM and image with contrast.
Sigmoid (lateral) sinus thrombosis — picket-fence/swinging fever, ± Griesinger's sign (postauricular oedema over the emissary vein). Confirm with MRV/contrast CT; treat with antibiotics, mastoidectomy ± anticoagulation.

🎯 Demonstrate it like an examiner wants

Commit first, then reveal the model structure and build the chain step by step.

Prompt: "A 3-year-old treated for AOM returns at day 12 with fever and a red, tender swelling behind the ear pushing it forward. Manage."

Your locked answer:
"This is acute mastoiditis — a suppurative complication of AOM and the gateway to intracranial sepsis, so it needs admission and urgent treatment."
Define coalescent mastoiditis; confirm the protruding pinna, obliterated crease, sagging canal wall and AOM; screen cranial nerves and for meningism.
WBC/CRP, blood cultures, ear swab; HRCT temporal bone (coalescence, cortical breach, abscess); contrast CT/MRV if any complication is suspected.
IV antibiotics + myringotomy first; cortical mastoidectomy for a subperiosteal/neck abscess, failure to improve in ~48 h, or a complication. Treat any intracranial complication jointly with neurosurgery.
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: AOM inflames the mastoid mucosa, and when the aditus ad antrum obstructs, pus is trapped under pressure. The trapped empyema causes osteitis that dissolves the bony septa, coalescing the air cells into an abscess. Continued pressure decompresses through the cortex (subperiosteal abscess), the tip (Bezold), or — dangerously — through the tegmen and sinus plate intracranially.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"A toddler with AOM not settling at day 12 has a red, fluctuant postauricular swelling pushing the pinna forward, a sagging canal wall and otorrhoea." → admit; CRP/WBC, cultures; HRCT; IV antibiotics + myringotomy; cortical mastoidectomy for the subperiosteal abscess. Screen for intracranial spread.
"Mastoiditis plus severe headache, drowsiness and swinging fever." → suspect sinus thrombosis/intracranial abscess/meningitis: urgent contrast CT + MRI/MRV, IV antibiotics crossing the blood–brain barrier, mastoidectomy, and joint neurosurgical care ± anticoagulation.
Postauricular subperiosteal (lateral cortex, commonest), Bezold (tip → SCM), Citelli (digastric/occipital), zygomatic/Luc (zygomatic root/under temporalis), and deep-neck extension. Each reflects the path of least resistance for escaping pus.
Antibiotics and pneumococcal vaccination reduced AOM and its complications, but mastoiditis persists due to resistant organisms, under- or partial treatment (masked disease), and virulent serotypes; it remains the commonest suppurative AOM complication.
Spread to a pneumatised petrous apex gives Gradenigo's triad: otorrhoea + retro-orbital pain (CN V) + lateral rectus palsy (CN VI). It needs imaging and aggressive antibiotic ± surgical treatment.
A myringotomy ± grommet drains the middle ear and gives culture, settling many early cases; mastoidectomy is needed when the disease is coalescent with an abscess, fails to respond, or has caused a complication — it drains the mastoid itself.
