🧠🔧 ENT & skull base surgery

📖 Full topic explanation

Skull-base surgery treats lesions at the interface of the nose/ear and the cranium, by anterior (endoscopic endonasal) and lateral approaches. It is fundamentally multidisciplinary, and its defining issues are the dangerous neurovascular anatomy, robust CSF-leak reconstruction, and the balance of access against morbidity.

🗺️ Anatomy & pathology

The skull base divides into , each hosting characteristic .

Regions: anterior (cribriform/ethmoid/orbit/sphenoid/sella), central (clivus, cavernous sinus, petrous apex), and posterolateral (cerebellopontine angle, jugular foramen, temporal bone). Each is reached by different approaches and endangers different structures.
Pathologies: sinonasal/nasopharyngeal tumours, esthesioneuroblastoma, pituitary adenoma, meningioma, chordoma, juvenile angiofibroma, CSF leaks/encephalocele, vestibular schwannoma, glomus jugulare and petrous-apex lesions — spanning ENT, neurosurgery and oncology.

🛠️ Surgical approaches

Approaches are broadly , , and .

Endoscopic endonasal: the "expanded endonasal" route reaches the anterior skull base, sella (pituitary), clivus and orbit through the nose — minimally invasive, no facial scar, with excellent visualisation. Used for pituitary tumours, CSF-leak repair, selected sinonasal malignancies and decompressions.
Open anterior (craniofacial) resection: combines a craniotomy with a facial/transfacial approach for tumours involving the anterior cranial fossa/dura/orbit (e.g. extensive esthesioneuroblastoma/sinonasal carcinoma) that exceed endoscopic limits.
Lateral skull base: translabyrinthine, retrosigmoid, middle-fossa (CPA/IAM — vestibular schwannoma, Topic 52) and infratemporal-fossa approaches (glomus jugulare, clival/petrous lesions). Choice balances access, hearing preservation and facial-nerve risk.

🧵 Reconstruction

Skull-base defects are repaired with to prevent CSF leak/meningitis.

Reconstruction: a multilayer repair (fascia/fat ± rigid support) and, for larger dural defects, the vascularised nasoseptal (Hadad–Bassagasteguy) flap are central to watertight CSF-leak closure after endoscopic skull-base surgery — markedly reducing post-operative leak/meningitis.

👥 MDT & navigation

Skull-base surgery is and relies on .

MDT: ENT/rhinology and otology + neurosurgery + ophthalmology + maxillofacial + oncology + radiology + endocrinology plan and operate jointly, given the shared anatomy and the need for combined approaches and reconstruction.
Imaging/navigation: CT (bone) + MRI (soft tissue/dura/brain) ± angiography define the lesion and vessels; image-guided navigation aids orientation near the orbit, optic nerve, carotid and skull base, especially in revision/distorted anatomy.

⚠️ Complications

The major risks are , .

CSF leak & meningitis: the commonest significant complication; presents with clear rhinorrhoea (β-2 transferrin positive). Prevented by good reconstruction; persistent leaks need re-repair, and pneumococcal vaccination/meningitis vigilance are essential. Pneumocephalus and tension pneumocephalus can also occur.
Vascular & neural: internal carotid artery injury (catastrophic haemorrhage — preparedness/control is vital), cranial-nerve deficits (II–VI anteriorly; VII/VIII and lower nerves laterally), and visual loss (optic-nerve injury). These define consent and the value of navigation/MDT planning.

📈 Outcome

Endoscopic and combined approaches achieve good oncological and functional results for appropriately selected lesions, with reconstruction (nasoseptal flap) having transformed CSF-leak rates. Outcomes depend on lesion type/extent, neurovascular involvement, and multidisciplinary expertise.

🎯 Demonstrate it like an examiner wants

Commit first, then reveal the model structure and build the chain step by step.

Prompt: "Outline the principles of resecting an anterior-skull-base sinonasal malignancy."

Your locked answer:
"This is a multidisciplinary skull-base resection aiming for oncological clearance while protecting the orbit, optic nerve, carotid and dura, with robust CSF reconstruction."
Biopsy/histology; CT + MRI ± angiography for extent (dura/orbit/brain/vessels); decide endoscopic vs open craniofacial by involvement; navigation.
Achieve clear margins (with neurosurgery for dura/brain, ophthalmology for the orbit); preserve/repair critical structures; manage the carotid risk.
Multilayer + nasoseptal-flap reconstruction of the dural/skull-base defect; adjuvant radiotherapy as indicated (Topic 42); monitor for CSF leak/meningitis; long-term surveillance.
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: Anterior-skull-base tumours sit against the cribriform plate, orbit, optic nerves and internal carotid arteries, so resection must remove disease while sparing or repairing these structures. Breaching the dura creates a communication between the sinonasal cavity and the subarachnoid space, the route for CSF leak and ascending meningitis. A vascularised nasoseptal flap re-establishes a watertight barrier, which is why it has transformed the safety of endoscopic skull-base surgery.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"A sellar/anterior-skull-base lesion suitable for an endonasal route." → endoscopic endonasal resection with nasoseptal-flap reconstruction; MDT (ENT + neurosurgery/endocrinology); monitor for CSF leak, visual change and endocrine dysfunction.
"Clear unilateral rhinorrhoea (β-2 transferrin positive) with a cribriform defect." → localise on CT/MRI; endoscopic multilayer repair ± nasoseptal flap; conservative measures and meningitis precautions; vaccinate.
Immediate packing and two-suction control, call for help, maintain circulation, then angiography ± endovascular control (covered stent/sacrifice with balloon-occlusion testing). Pre-op planning, navigation and a prepared team are key to survival/outcome.
Pre- and post-operative pituitary hormone assessment (cortisol, thyroid, etc.), watching for diabetes insipidus and hypopituitarism post-op, and visual-field testing (optic-chiasm proximity) — managed jointly with endocrinology and ophthalmology.
By providing a robust vascularised barrier for large dural defects, it dramatically reduced post-operative CSF-leak and meningitis rates, enabling the safe expansion of endoscopic approaches to high-flow leaks and large tumours.
Tumour size/location, hearing status, and facial-nerve risk: translabyrinthine (non-serviceable hearing, best VII access), middle fossa/retrosigmoid (hearing preservation for small CPA tumours), and infratemporal-fossa for jugular-foramen/clival disease — chosen in the skull-base MDT.
