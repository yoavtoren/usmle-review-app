😴 Sleep apnea syndrome — ENT aspects

📖 Full topic explanation

Obstructive sleep apnea (OSA) is repetitive collapse of the upper airway during sleep, causing intermittent hypoxia, arousals and daytime consequences. The ENT role is to localise the level of obstruction and treat it — and to recognise that in children adenotonsillectomy is first-line while in adults CPAP is the mainstay.

🗂️ Definition & severity

OSA is defined by and graded by the .

Definition: repetitive complete (apnoea) or partial (hypopnoea) upper-airway collapse during sleep, with continued respiratory effort, causing oxygen desaturations and cortical arousals that fragment sleep. Distinguish from central sleep apnoea (absent effort) and simple snoring (no apnoeas).
Severity (AHI = events/hour): mild 5–15, moderate 15–30, severe >30. Severity (with symptoms and comorbidity) guides treatment intensity (e.g. CPAP for moderate–severe).

⚙️ Pathophysiology & levels of obstruction

Collapse results from , at one or more that ENT surgery targets.

Mechanism: a narrow pharyngeal airway (anatomy/fat) plus the loss of dilator-muscle tone during sleep allows collapse; repeated obstruction → hypoxia/hypercapnia, surges in sympathetic tone, and arousals. Over time this drives hypertension and cardiovascular/metabolic disease.
Levels of obstruction: nose (septum/turbinates/polyps), retropalatal (soft palate/uvula/tonsils), retroglossal (tongue base), and the larynx/epiglottis. Identifying the level (often multilevel) is the key to choosing surgery — done by examination and drug-induced sleep endoscopy.

📊 Risk factors & consequences

Risk rises with ; untreated OSA causes important , with a distinct .

Risk factors: obesity (large neck), male sex, increasing age, retrognathia/micrognathia, large tonsils/adenoids, nasal obstruction, alcohol/sedatives, smoking, hypothyroidism and acromegaly. These narrow or destabilise the airway.
Consequences: excessive daytime sleepiness, impaired concentration, road-traffic and occupational accidents, and cardiovascular disease — hypertension (especially resistant), arrhythmias (AF), stroke, ischaemic heart disease and pulmonary hypertension. Treating OSA improves blood pressure and reduces accident risk.
Children: usually from adenotonsillar hypertrophy; present with snoring, restless sleep, witnessed pauses, mouth breathing, and daytime behavioural/learning problems, hyperactivity, enuresis and failure to thrive (rather than sleepiness). Adenotonsillectomy is first-line.

🔎 Assessment

Use , localise with , and confirm/grade with a .

Clinical: snoring, witnessed apnoeas, sleepiness (Epworth Sleepiness Scale), morning headache; screen with STOP-BANG. ENT exam: BMI/neck, nose, oropharynx (tonsil size, Mallampati/Friedman grade, palate, tongue base), and flexible nasendoscopy (± Müller manoeuvre).
Drug-induced sleep endoscopy (DISE): nasendoscopy during sedation-induced sleep to directly observe where and how the airway collapses (palate, tongue base, lateral walls, epiglottis) — essential for selecting site-specific surgery.
Sleep study: polysomnography (gold standard) or home sleep apnoea testing/oximetry measures the AHI and desaturations to confirm and grade OSA. Essential before committing to treatment, especially surgery.

💊 Management

Management combines , and , with and .

Conservative: weight loss, avoiding alcohol/sedatives near bedtime, sleep hygiene, positional therapy (avoid supine sleep), and treating nasal obstruction/hypothyroidism. First step for all and often sufficient in mild disease.
CPAP: continuous positive airway pressure is the gold-standard treatment for moderate–severe adult OSA — it pneumatically splints the airway open, abolishing apnoeas and improving sleepiness/blood pressure. Effectiveness depends on adherence.
Mandibular advancement devices: dental appliances that advance the mandible/tongue base, useful for mild–moderate OSA, snoring, or CPAP-intolerant patients.
ENT surgery (site-directed): nasal surgery (septoplasty/turbinate reduction — improves CPAP tolerance), tonsillectomy (large tonsils), uvulopalatopharyngoplasty (UPPP)/palate procedures, tongue-base reduction, hypoglossal-nerve stimulation, maxillomandibular advancement, and tracheostomy (definitive, last resort for severe disease). Choice is guided by DISE.
Children: adenotonsillectomy is first-line and curative in most; CPAP is reserved for residual/complex OSA (obesity, craniofacial/neuromuscular disease). Sleep studies guide young/comorbid children and post-operative monitoring.

📈 Prognosis

Treated OSA improves sleepiness, quality of life, blood pressure and accident risk. CPAP is highly effective if tolerated; surgery helps selected patients by addressing the specific obstruction level; paediatric OSA usually resolves with adenotonsillectomy.

🎯 Demonstrate it like an examiner wants

Commit first, then reveal the model structure and build the chain step by step.

Prompt: "An overweight 45-year-old man snores, has witnessed apnoeas and falls asleep at work. How do you assess and manage?"

Your locked answer:
"Snoring, witnessed apnoeas and daytime sleepiness in an obese man is obstructive sleep apnea, which carries cardiovascular and driving risks I must address."
Epworth/STOP-BANG, BMI/neck, ENT airway exam (nose, tonsils, Mallampati/Friedman, tongue base), flexible nasendoscopy; advise on driving until controlled.
Sleep study (polysomnography) to confirm/grade (AHI); DISE to localise the obstruction level if surgery is considered.
Weight loss, avoid alcohol/sedatives, positional therapy; CPAP for moderate–severe (gold standard); MAD or site-specific ENT surgery (nasal, UPPP, tongue base, hypoglossal stimulation, MMA) for selected/CPAP-intolerant patients.
🔬 Mechanism in 3 sentences: During sleep, reduced pharyngeal dilator-muscle tone allows an already-narrow airway (fat, large tonsils, retrognathia) to collapse, causing apnoeas with hypoxia and CO₂ rise. The resulting arousals fragment sleep (daytime sleepiness) and trigger repeated sympathetic surges that drive hypertension and arrhythmia. CPAP splints the airway open with positive pressure, while surgery removes or stabilises the specific collapsing segment.

📚 Clinical reasoning & case presentations

Model presentations and advanced examiner follow-ups.
"Obese man, loud snoring, witnessed apnoeas, Epworth high, AHI 35." → severe OSA: weight loss/lifestyle, CPAP (first-line), driving advice; nasal surgery to aid CPAP tolerance, and DISE-guided surgery if CPAP-intolerant.
"A 5-year-old snores with witnessed pauses and is hyperactive and behind at school; large tonsils/adenoids." → adenotonsillectomy (first-line); sleep study if young/comorbid; post-op respiratory monitoring; expect resolution.
Relieving nasal obstruction improves CPAP tolerance and adherence (lower required pressures, less mouth leak) and can reduce snoring, even though the main collapse is usually pharyngeal. It is often an adjunct rather than a cure.
An implanted device that stimulates the hypoglossal nerve in time with respiration to protrude the tongue and open the retroglossal airway. It is used in selected CPAP-intolerant patients with appropriate anatomy/AHI and a favourable DISE pattern (no complete concentric palatal collapse).
Patients with excessive sleepiness must be warned not to drive while sleepy and to inform the licensing authority; driving can usually resume once OSA is adequately treated (e.g. compliant on CPAP) with symptom control. This is a medico-legal safety point.
UPPP addresses only the palate level, so it fails in multilevel or tongue-base collapse; outcomes are variable and it can cause velopharyngeal insufficiency/nasal regurgitation and pain. Patient selection by DISE improves results.
